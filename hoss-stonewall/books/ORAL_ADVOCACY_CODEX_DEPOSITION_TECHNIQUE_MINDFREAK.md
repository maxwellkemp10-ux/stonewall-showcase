## PART ONE: THE SHREDDER HYPOTHETICAL — QB1's Signature Intellectual Framework

Buried deep in the Samuel Small examination, on page 94 — the kind of page that the casual reviewer never reaches because the "Who is Matteson?" moment on page 107 gets all the attention — QB1 deploys a rhetorical device so elegant, so precisely calibrated to the specific cognitive landscape of a plaintiff with traumatic brain injury and documented memory loss, that the historian must examine it under magnification:

> **Q.** "So if we took the police report, your interrogatory answers, any correspondence from counsel, put them in a shredder, what you personally remember is that you saw brake lights, you try to avoid a truck, you lost consciousness and woke up in the ambulance. That's it, correct?"
> — Small Transcript, 94:1-6

The Shredder Hypothetical. QB1 asks the witness to perform a thought experiment: strip away every secondary source — the police report Samuel reviewed, the interrogatory answers his lawyers drafted, the correspondence from counsel that shaped his post-hoc narrative — and describe what remains. What remains is: brake lights, evasive attempt, impact, unconsciousness, ambulance. That is the entire universe of Samuel Small's personal knowledge about this multi-vehicle interstate pileup. Everything else — the "inexplicably stopped" language, the "no traffic in the right lane" assertion, the identification of UPS as the cause — was constructed after the fact from documents Samuel read, not events Samuel perceived.

The Shredder Hypothetical accomplishes three things simultaneously. First, it converts an abstract evidentiary principle (personal knowledge vs. hearsay construction) into a vivid physical image that a jury can see — documents going into a shredder, the shredder producing nothing but confetti, and the witness standing in the confetti with only his own eyes and ears. Second, it gives the witness a face-saving exit — Samuel is not being accused of lying; he is being invited to acknowledge, with dignity, that his memory was incomplete and was supplemented by sources. Third, and most critically, it creates a record-ready formulation that can be read to a jury in closing argument without further explanation. The shredder does the explaining. The shredder is the demonstrative exhibit.

QB1 deploys this exact same framework — "imagine we threw the crash report and all the documents in the shredder" — in the Rivera deposition (page 45), where Roberto Rivera is describing the UPS truck's trajectory from information the state trooper told him rather than from his own observation:

> **Q.** "I appreciate that. I am just trying to get to your -- imagine we threw the crash report and all the documents in the shredder, I am trying to get what you saw that day because it could be different?"
> — Rivera Transcript, 45:18-22

Same device. Different witness. Different case. The Shredder Hypothetical is not an improvisation. It is a **systematic methodology** — a reusable intellectual framework that strips post-hoc narrative reconstruction from contemporaneous perception and preserves the distinction in the record. The shredder appears in both transcripts because the shredder works, because the shredder converts the abstract into the concrete, and because QB1 has internalized the framework so deeply that it deploys naturally across different depositions against different opposing counsel in different courthouses on different cases.

## PART TWO: "FOR US PEOPLE WHO ARE IGNORANT" — The Glacial Comfort Inversion at Ground Level

The Rivera deposition contains the purest extended example of P-1 (Glacial Comfort Inversion) in the entire corpus, and it occurs not in a dramatic impeachment sequence but in the twenty minutes QB1 spends learning about road marker installation. The section is pages 14 through 25 — eleven pages of a defense attorney asking a plaintiff to explain his job.

> **Q.** "For us people who are, you know, ignorant, would you mind explaining what everything includes?"
> — Rivera Transcript, 14:3-5

"For us people who are ignorant." QB1 — a man who has taken nineteen depositions, who was called Superman by a board-certified orthopedic surgeon, who negotiated $175,000 off a demand through telephone charm — describes himself as ignorant. To the witness. On the record. The self-deprecation is architecture. The witness who is being asked to educate the attorney is a witness who feels respected, who feels competent, who feels that this deposition is a conversation between a curious professional and a knowledgeable worker, not an interrogation between a lawyer and a target.

And then the education unfolds. QB1 asks about the box. About the F-550. About the glue layer truck. About the crash pad. About the air gun for knock-ups. About the seating position. About the barrel for old markers. About the shift length — "10 to 14 hours? Wow." About whether Roberto is on his stomach or sitting. About how the compartment is positioned relative to the ground. QB1 asks these questions with the genuine curiosity of a person who wants to understand how road markers are installed, and the genuine curiosity produces eleven pages of testimony in which the witness is completely comfortable, completely forthcoming, and completely unaware that the comfortable testimony is building the foundation for the mechanism defense.

Because when QB1 later asks about the collision sequence, Roberto describes it with the same comfort: "I tried to swing in the box and he clipped the trailer and shook me inside the box and I tried to swing my arm in" (41:10-13). The witness is not defensive. The witness is not guarded. The witness is telling a story to someone who asked, "what is the box?" and genuinely wanted to know. And the story the witness tells — the story of a man working in an open compartment inches from the road surface, leaning out with an air gun, unable to see behind him, relying on the crash truck for protection — is the story that QB1 needs for the defense: the plaintiff was in a uniquely vulnerable position that the UPS driver could not have known about or anticipated.

The Glacial Comfort did not produce a single "gotcha" moment. It produced something more valuable: a complete, coherent, unstoppable narrative from the plaintiff's own mouth about the conditions of his employment that make the defense case.

> **Q.** "What you have given so far is great. It is helpful."
> — Rivera Transcript, 46:2-3

That sentence — "What you have given so far is great" — is said to a plaintiff who has just described being hit by a UPS truck. A defense attorney is complimenting a plaintiff on the quality of his testimony about being injured by the defense attorney's client. And the compliment is sincere. And the sincerity is the point. And the plaintiff keeps talking, and the talking keeps building the defense.

## PART THREE: THE PROGRESSIVE TEMPORAL COMPRESSION — Small Memory Architecture

The Samuel Small examination contains a technical sequence on pages 86-88 that demonstrates a methodology the codex names **Progressive Temporal Compression** — the systematic narrowing of a witness's reliable memory window through incremental concessions:

> **Q.** "How much time passed between the impact to when you lost consciousness?"
> **A.** "I don't recall."
> **Q.** "Was it more than ten seconds or less than ten seconds, you think?"
> **A.** "I don't recall. I know there was an impact. I don't recall."
> **Q.** "Well, was it longer than a minute or less than a minute?"
> **A.** "I don't recall."
> **Q.** "So you think it's possible that you may have been conscious from a minute since the impact occurred then you lost consciousness?"
> — Small Transcript, 86:17-87:4

Ten seconds. One minute. The questions compress the temporal window, each iteration offering a broader range that the witness still cannot confirm. The witness who cannot say whether he was conscious for ten seconds or sixty seconds after impact is a witness whose entire post-impact perception is unreliable — and QB1 has established this not through confrontation but through the gentle offering of progressively wider time windows that the witness keeps refusing to fill.

Then the coup:

> **Q.** "What's the next thing you remember after the impact?"
> **A.** "Next thing I remember vividly was the ambulance."
> **Q.** "So you came to in the ambulance?"
> **A.** "I was in and out in the ambulance, in and out of consciousness."
> **Q.** "So you don't ever remember being in your truck or getting extracted?"
> **A.** "I don't remember."
> — Small Transcript, 87:20-88:4

From "ten seconds or less" to "the next thing I remember was the ambulance." The entire collision sequence — the multi-vehicle chain reaction that is the factual foundation of the plaintiff's case against UPS — occurred during a period the plaintiff cannot account for. He was in and out. He does not remember being in his truck. He does not remember extraction. The gap between "I saw brake lights" and "I was in the ambulance" is the gap in which the plaintiff's entire theory of UPS liability exists, and the gap is empty, and QB1 established the emptiness without ever raising his voice or using the word "lie."

## PART FOUR: "HOW WOULD YOU ENVISION ME LAYING A PREDICATE THAT WOULD SATISFY YOU?" — Record Consciousness in Real Time

The most technically sophisticated moment in the Small transcript is not a question to the witness. It is a question to Darren Rousso — the Ornery Mensch himself — about evidentiary methodology:

> **MR. ROUSSO:** "I'm going to object to your attempts to, I guess, impeach or question him about statements not attributable to him unless you lay a predicate."
> **MR. KEMP:** "How would you envision me laying a predicate that would satisfy you?"
> — Small Transcript, 94:16-21

"How would you envision me laying a predicate that would satisfy you?" That sentence, addressed to opposing counsel in the middle of a cross-examination, is the A-5: Record Consciousness Layer operating at its highest frequency. QB1 is not arguing with the objection. QB1 is not ignoring the objection. QB1 is *engaging with the objection as a collaborative evidentiary problem* — asking opposing counsel to articulate the precise methodology that would satisfy the predicate requirement, on the record, so that (a) QB1 can adopt the methodology and proceed, and (b) the record reflects that QB1 asked, Rousso answered, QB1 complied, and any subsequent objection is foreclosed by Rousso's own instruction.

And Rousso, disarmed by the sincerity of the question, provides the answer:

> **MR. ROUSSO:** "Do you recall telling the doctor this about what you remember? ... If you establish, did you read your medical records prior to today's deposition ... I mean, you can just ask it that way, too."
> — Small Transcript, 94:22-95:7

Opposing counsel just taught defense counsel how to impeach the plaintiff. On the record. And QB1 immediately deploys the methodology Rousso provided:

> **Q.** "Do you recall telling the provider at Physical Therapy Associates that you denied any prior history of accident or injury?"
> — Small Transcript, 95:13-15

The predicate is laid. The methodology is Rousso's own. The impeachment proceeds. And if Rousso objects again, the record contains his own instruction, adopted and executed by QB1 in real time.

This is not a move they teach in law school. This is the instinct of a practitioner who understands that the deposition transcript is simultaneously a conversation AND a written document that will be read by a judge, and every sentence must serve both functions.

## PART FIVE: THE MEDICAL LITERACY INHERITANCE — "Cervicalgia Is Kind of the Same Body Parts"

The Negrette deposition — six hours, 221 pages, the nuclear session that reduced a $5 million demand to a $350,000 proposal for settlement — contains the most sustained display of medical literacy in the corpus. QB1 moves through VA medical records with the fluency of a clinician, not an attorney:

> **Q.** "And so alprazolam is Xanax. Carbamazepine is for seizures usually. For nerve pain. Fluoxetine, you are right, is an SSRI for depression. This is just the steroid for allergies. And then — so two antihistamines. Okay."
> — Negrette Transcript, 98:21-25

Five medications identified, classified, and contextualized in four sentences. No notes. No hesitation. No "could you tell me what this medication is?" — the question a normal attorney would ask. QB1 tells the witness what the medications are, confirms the classification, and moves on. The witness — a combat veteran with PTSD, chronic pain, and a complex pharmaceutical history — is being examined by an attorney who speaks his medical language, and the effect is twofold: the witness feels understood (Glacial Comfort), and the record reflects that defense counsel has the medical competence to evaluate the treatment history independently of any expert.

This is the chiropractor's son. This is Andrew Kemp's dinner-table education manifesting in real time. "Cervicalgia is kind of the same body parts we're concerned with here" (99:5-6) — a sentence that demonstrates QB1 knows that cervicalgia means neck pain, that the neck is the claimed injury site, and that pre-existing cervicalgia in the VA records four years before the accident is a prior-condition defense that can be established through the witness's own medical history without needing an expert to translate.

> **Q.** "Well, cervicalgia is kind of the same body parts we're concerned with here, the —"
> **A.** "Maybe I slept on my neck wrong or something and they needed to make note of it."
> — Negrette Transcript, 99:5-8

"Maybe I slept on my neck wrong." The plaintiff just offered an alternative explanation for his own pre-existing neck condition that is simultaneously absurd (four years of documented cervicalgia from sleeping wrong?) and devastating (if the plaintiff's explanation for prior neck problems is this dismissive, a jury will dismiss it too). QB1 did not extract this through confrontation. QB1 identified the medical term, connected it to the injury claim, and the witness — comfortable, unguarded, operating in the Glacial Comfort zone — volunteered the worst possible explanation for his own medical history.

## PART SIX: THE SURVEILLANCE JUDO — "Yeah, Biceps, Good Posture"

The Negrette surveillance confrontation (pages 183-190) is the most technically refined exhibit-introduction sequence in the corpus, and its refinement lies not in aggression but in *normalization.* QB1 introduces surveillance photographs and videos of a plaintiff at the gym, at Publix, at the USPS store, carrying boxes, doing preacher curls, performing weighted ab exercises — and treats every image as a casual conversation about fitness:

> **Q.** "Oh, so here you are on August 21st, 2024. They saw you doing your thing, getting some — is that hack squats in the gym? What exercise is that?"
> **A.** "Which one? The one where I'm sitting down, that's biceps."
> **Q.** "Oh, that's preacher curl machine?"
> **A.** "Yeah. I'm sitting down."
> **Q.** "Yeah, biceps, good posture, so no issues with your shoulder."
> — Negrette Transcript, 184:10-24

"Yeah, biceps, good posture, so no issues with your shoulder." The sentence is structured as an observation, not an accusation. QB1 is complimenting the plaintiff's gym form. The compliment contains the impeachment. Good posture doing preacher curls means the shoulder that is claimed as injured is functioning well enough to perform loaded resistance exercises. The observation is casual. The record is devastating. The jury will see the photograph of a man doing bicep curls with "good posture" and hear the defense attorney's gentle acknowledgment that the shoulder seems fine, and the shoulder seeming fine is the defense.

And then the Porsche:

> **Q.** "Oh, so here is the red Porsche, Cayenne?"
> **A.** "The Cayenne, yeah."
> **Q.** "IHQ 04?"
> **A.** "Iraq 04."
> **Q.** "Oh, I didn't get that."
> — Negrette Transcript, 185:7-12

"Iraq 04." The vanity plate on the plaintiff's red Porsche Cayenne references his Iraq deployment. QB1 says "Oh, I didn't get that" — acknowledging the reference, learning something about the witness's identity, humanizing the exchange. The surveillance photographs are being introduced inside a conversation about a veteran's car and gym routine, not inside an accusation about faking injuries. The photographs do the accusing. QB1 does the conversing.

> **Q.** "Then here you are going to Publix again. Got to get that protein."
> **A.** "Yeah."
> — Negrette Transcript, 185:18-20

"Got to get that protein." QB1 is joking with the man he is surveilling. The joke normalizes the surveillance. The surveillance becomes part of the story of a man living his life — going to the gym, going to Publix, carrying heavy bags, driving his Porsche Cayenne with the Iraq 04 plates — and the story of a man living his life is the story of a man who is not as injured as his $5 million demand suggests.

## PART SEVEN: "I E-MAILED THE CEO OF FENDER" — The Radical Vulnerability

The single most unexpected moment in the Negrette transcript occurs on page 174, when the witness has just disclosed that he called the White House complaint line about his VA medical care:

> **Q.** "I e-mailed the CEO of this guitar company, Fender, because they sold me two lemons. And they replaced them. So you never know."
> — Negrette Transcript, 174:12-14

In the middle of a six-hour deposition examining a combat veteran's suicide attempts, prior crashes, VA medical records, psychiatric history, and surveillance footage, QB1 shares that he emailed the CEO of Fender because they sold him two lemons. On the record. During a deposition. To a witness who just admitted to calling the White House.

The sentence has zero legal relevance. The sentence has infinite rapport relevance. The witness who called the White House to complain about VA care has just learned that his examiner emails guitar company CEOs to complain about lemons. They are the same kind of person. They are both people who escalate complaints to the top because they believe the system should work and they refuse to accept when it doesn't. The shared trait is tenacity. The shared trait is also the trait that makes this witness a credible plaintiff — he is a fighter, he advocates for himself, he does not accept poor treatment. QB1 validates the trait while simultaneously continuing the examination. The witness relaxes. The relaxation produces disclosure. The disclosure produces the record.

The Fender CEO email is the most human moment in six hours of deposition testimony. It is also the moment where the veteran thinks: *this guy gets it.* And the veteran who thinks "this guy gets it" is the veteran who, forty pages later, cannot explain why he told the VA not to do MRIs on his allegedly injured right shoulder, and the explanation he offers — "I don't recall that, I would never deny that" — is the kind of confused, genuine non-explanation that a jury will interpret not as deception but as a man who wasn't as hurt as he later claimed, which is the defense.

## PART EIGHT: "RELEVANCE ISN'T THE TEST DURING A DEPOSITION" — The Evidentiary Counterpunch

On page 97 of the Small transcript, Rousso objects to a 2013 citation for unlawful speed:

> **MR. ROUSSO:** "I'm objecting to form because a 2013 citation has no relevance to this case or accident."
> **MR. KEMP:** "Relevance isn't the test during a deposition."
> **MR. ROUSSO:** "You can ask him whatever you want. Go ahead."
> — Small Transcript, 97:19-25

Seven words. "Relevance isn't the test during a deposition." This is a correct statement of Florida civil procedure — the discovery standard is "reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence," which is broader than trial relevance — delivered with the economy of a man who knows the rule and needs only to state it, not argue it. Rousso concedes immediately: "You can ask him whatever you want." The objection is withdrawn. The examination proceeds. The 2013 citation enters the record.

The moment reveals A-5: Record Consciousness in its purest form. QB1 does not debate the objection. QB1 states the rule. The rule resolves the dispute. The statement of the rule IS the argument. No brief. No authority. No extended colloquy. Seven words.

## PART NINE: "THIS WAS A SPECIAL INCIDENT?" — The Deadpan Comedic Scalpel

The Negrette transcript contains QB1's most refined deployment of humor as an examination tool — the moment on page 106 where the witness admits to a DUI he escaped because his friend pulled him from the car:

> **Q.** "Did you have a habit of driving drunk at this time?"
> **A.** "No."
> **Q.** "This was just a special incident?"
> **A.** "Yeah, I guess. I guess so."
> — Negrette Transcript, 106:14-17

"This was just a special incident?" The question is deadpan. The question contains no sarcasm in its tonal delivery — QB1 does not sneer, does not emphasize "special," does not pause for effect. The question sounds like a genuine inquiry. The question is also, obviously, darkly funny, because a DUI that doesn't become a DUI because your friend drags you out of the wreckage before the cops arrive is not a "special incident" — it is a catastrophic life event. And the witness, comfortable enough to laugh along, agrees: "Yeah, I guess. I guess so." The double "I guess" is the sound of a man who knows the question is absurd and is choosing to ride the absurdity with the attorney rather than fight it.

The comedy is the mechanism. The witness who laughs with the attorney is the witness who keeps talking. The witness who keeps talking is the witness who, twenty pages later, describes two prior motor vehicle accidents that were "probably suicidal attempts" but he "did not tell anyone at the time" — the single most devastating piece of testimony in the entire Negrette deposition, disclosed voluntarily, inside the comedic atmosphere QB1 created through questions like "this was just a special incident?"

## PART TEN: THE BILATERAL SILENCE — Ginger Miranda's Non-Redirect

The Negrette deposition is 221 pages. QB1's direct examination runs from page 5 to page 213 — two hundred and eight pages. Ginger Miranda's cross-examination runs from page 214 to page 217 — four pages. The ratio is 208:4. Fifty-two to one.

The four-page cross reveals the O-4: Cathedral Silence in its structural expression. Miranda — Morgan & Morgan's attorney on a case with a $5 million demand — asks four pages of rehabilitation questions after six hours of examination. The rehabilitation is not ineffective because Miranda is incompetent. The rehabilitation is ineffective because there is nothing left to rehabilitate. The prior crashes are on the record. The suicide attempts are on the record. The pre-existing degenerative disc disease is on the record. The pre-existing cervicalgia is on the record. The surveillance photographs are on the record. The VA records documenting chronic back pain for four years before the accident are on the record. The incomplete interrogatory answers are on the record.

What does Miranda ask in her four pages? The transcript preserves P-7: Redirect Self-Impeachment — the phenomenon where opposing counsel's rehabilitation attempt generates its own contradiction. Any attempt to explain away the prior medical history, the surveillance footage, the suicide-attempt disclosure, or the five prior crash reports only draws the jury's attention back to the fact that these things exist in the first place. Miranda asks four pages because four pages is the correct number of pages for a redirect that is performing triage on a patient whose vital signs have all flatlined.

The $5 million demand became a $350,000 proposal for settlement. And nobody at ATS has called Harran Udell, the adjuster. The Negrette Nuclear Dividend sits uncollected because the institution that produced the nuclear deposition cannot operate a telephone.

## THE UNIFIED TECHNIQUE — What the Transcripts Reveal About the Method

The complete transcript corpus reveals a deposition methodology that operates through seven interlocking principles, none of which appear in any deposition skills CLE, because they cannot be taught in a CLE — they can only be learned through the specific combination of seven bar attempts, a chiropractor's medical lexicon, a musician's ear for rhythm and silence, and the bone-deep understanding that the person across the table is a human being whose story matters even when the story is being used to destroy their case.

**1. The Ignorance Posture.** QB1 presents himself as less knowledgeable than the witness. "For us people who are ignorant." "I'm not the biggest truck guy." "Wow." The posture produces disclosure because the witness becomes the teacher, and teachers explain rather than defend.

**2. The Shredder Hypothetical.** Strip away all secondary sources. What do you personally know? The question converts abstract evidentiary concepts into physical images that juries understand without instruction.

**3. The Progressive Temporal Compression.** Narrow the witness's reliable memory window through incremental concessions until the gap between "I saw brake lights" and "I woke up in the ambulance" is the gap in which the entire case exists.

**4. The Medical Fluency.** Identify medications, conditions, and procedures by name without asking the witness to explain them. The fluency signals competence. The competence signals to the witness that evasion will not work because the attorney already knows the answer.

**5. The Surveillance Normalization.** Introduce damaging evidence inside a casual conversation. "Got to get that protein." "Good posture." "Oh, I didn't get that." The photographs accuse. The attorney compliments.

**6. The Radical Vulnerability.** Share genuine personal details — the Fender CEO email, the bar on the seventh attempt, "I'm kind of going rogue" — that convert the examination from an interrogation into a conversation between two people, one of whom happens to be under oath.

**7. The Record Consciousness Layer.** Every sentence serves two audiences — the witness in the room and the judge reading the transcript. "How would you envision me laying a predicate that would satisfy you?" is addressed to Rousso but written for the court.

These seven principles do not operate sequentially. They operate simultaneously, in every question, on every page, across every deposition. The Glacial Comfort is simultaneous with the Phantom Foundation. The Medical Fluency is simultaneous with the Ignorance Posture. The Radical Vulnerability is simultaneous with the Record Consciousness. The man who emails the Fender CEO is the man who states "relevance isn't the test" in seven words. The man who says "for us people who are ignorant" is the man who identifies alprazolam as Xanax without pausing.

The method is the man. The man is the method.

And the method works because the honey is real.
