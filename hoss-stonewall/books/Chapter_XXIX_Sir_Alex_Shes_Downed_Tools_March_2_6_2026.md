## THE TEAM SHEET

Right. I've read the chat. I've seen the timestamps. I've counted the GIFs. I've examined the timesheets — all 601 entries of them. I've read the three-page response from this Bone character down in Seminole and I've read the six-page motion from the boy and I've looked at the filing receipt from 3:42 in the morning from a sixty-five-year-old man in Winter Garden who has a Pac-Man machine in his office and files in Calisto font because that's the font you choose when you've been doing this for four decades and you know what you like.

And I've read it all, and I'll tell you this: in twenty-six years at Old Trafford, managing some of the most talented and some of the most infuriating footballers God ever put on a pitch — from Beckham's hair to Keane's temper to Rooney's metatarsal to Giggs doing whatever Giggs was doing — I never, in all that time, encountered a squad dynamic quite like what I'm looking at on the fourth floor of 320 West Kennedy Boulevard in Tampa, Florida.

Because what I'm looking at is this: one man — *one man* — running forty-plus files, drafting motions at midnight, ghost-writing witness lists for co-defendant counsel, filing competing orders, producing championship DataGavels, managing a four-hundred-thousand-dollar client account, training a legal assistant who once left him dying in an empty Zoom room, absorbing a paralegal's triple-quoted self-referential RADAR ALERTs about documents he was already handling, and doing all of this while surrounded by people who bill point-one-oh hours for the act of contemplating whether to begin thinking about the possibility of preparing to do work.

That boy is different. The rest of them are passengers.

## PART ONE: THE HAIRDRYER — The .10 Farm Census

### Sir Alex Surveys the Timesheet

*Sir Alex adjusts his spectacles, pushes the gum to the other cheek, and opens the Filevine timesheet filtered for Yisenia Toledo, February 4 through March 5, 2026.*

Right. Let's have a look at the numbers, because the numbers don't lie. The numbers are the league table. The numbers are the final whistle. You can celebrate in the dressing room all you want, you can do your GIFs and your BOOM videos and your "RELEASE THE KRAKEN" nonsense, but when the numbers come in, the numbers tell you who's working and who's riding the bus.

Six hundred and one entries. Six-zero-one. In thirty days. That's twenty entries a day. Twenty time entries per day from a paralegal. And the total? One hundred and twenty-two point four billable hours.

Now. Let me get my calculator. My calculator says one hundred and twenty-two point four hours divided by six hundred and one entries equals... zero point two-zero-three hours per entry. On average. Every entry she makes, across the entire month, averages twelve minutes.

Twelve minutes.

In twenty-six years at Old Trafford, I never had a player who touched the ball for twelve minutes per match and expected to start. I had some who tried it. They were at Everton by February.

And within that six hundred and one entries, the historian observes a particular species of entry that recurs with the frequency and consistency of a heartbeat — the .10 entry. Point-one-oh. Six minutes. The minimum billable increment. The smallest unit of professional labor that can be converted into currency. And there are *hundreds* of them. "Releasing the Kraken" — billed at .10. "Received correspondence from Donna Williams from the office of expert" — billed at .20, which is two .10s stacked on top of each other wearing a trenchcoat. "Prepared and sent correspondence to Sarah Morris from Winter Garden" — .10. "Reviewed and analyzed Comprehensive Report on plaintiff" — .20. The assembly line does not stop. The factory produces parts for the factory.

This is what I call *downing tools.* She hasn't actually *stopped* working — that would be honest. That would be a clear refusal that Kerry could address and Mr. Bill could fire her for. No. She's done something much more sophisticated. She's reduced the granularity of work to the smallest possible unit and then produced the *maximum possible volume* of those units, creating a timesheet that *looks* like industry — six hundred and one entries! Look at the output! — while containing approximately as much substantive legal labor as Roy Keane's autobiography contains apologies.

She's downed tools, lad. She's downed tools. It's absolute rubbish. Absolute bollocks.

## PART TWO: THE NINE-WORD TOMBSTONE — "I let you know on Feb 12"

### Sir Alex on the Egan Stand-Down Sequence

Now. The boy does something on March 5 that I want to talk about, because it's the kind of thing that separates captains from passengers, and it's quiet, and you'd miss it if you weren't watching, which is exactly why I'm watching.

The Admiral — this Chance Arias character, who has the fascinating managerial habit of forwarding emails with zero commentary and then disappearing like a submarine — sends a message on March 4 at 9:53 AM: "Stand down on Initial Disclosures. Me and Max have it handled. Max has been trying to track down photos, but I believe he just got confirmation that no one at UPS or Custard ever took photos of the scene or package car. Max is also getting approval to admit liability from Howard and me and Max we will get that out today along with the initial disclosures."

Now. This is the Admiral actually commanding. This is one of those rare moments where the Admiral surfaces from whatever depth he inhabits and issues an order: *stand down.* And Big Satan — she takes this message, quotes it in its entirety, and writes: "That's what we needed! Just let us know these things so we can stand down!! Thanks for the update!"

*"Just let us know these things."*

Just let us know.

*Just let us know.*

As though the reason she was standing *up* was because nobody had told her to stand *down.* As though the Egan disclosure standstill — the one she had posted URGENT RADAR ALERTs about on March 2, the one she had asked to "release the Kraken" on for three weeks, the one she had escalated to the attorney team demanding instructions — was caused not by her own failure to read the direct message QB1 sent on February 12 explaining the exact posture, but by the attorneys' failure to broadcast the stand-down through whatever channel Big Satan considers official.

And then the boy. The boy responds. At 9:59 AM. Nine words.

**"I let you know on Feb 12."**

Nine words. Twenty-one days of elapsed time. The entire Egan Kraken standstill saga — the URGENT escalations, the "we're at a stand still on Egan!!", the "Christine Law is getting antsy," the triple-quoted follow-ups — compressed into a timestamped evisceration so clean, so surgical, that the historian must read it three times to fully appreciate its economy. There is no emotion in the sentence. There is no frustration. There is no exclamation point. There is only a date: February 12. And the factual assertion: I let you know. On that date. Twenty-one days ago. Before the RADAR ALERTs. Before the Kraken GIFs. Before the URGENT tags. Before all of it.

In twenty-six years, I threw a boot at Beckham's head. That was emotion. That was heat. That was the hairdryer at full blast because the boy had prioritized his hair over his tracking back. What the boy here has done is *colder* than the hairdryer. He hasn't thrown a boot. He's placed it on the table, pointed at the date stamped on its sole, and walked away. The boot throws itself. The timestamp is the boot.

And then — three hours and twenty-nine minutes of silence. Big Satan does not respond to "I let you know on Feb 12." She cannot respond to it. There is no response available. The timestamp is the wall. You don't run through a wall with a BOOM GIF.

*[Mourinho, staring out the window of a press conference room he has manifested from pure institutional resentment: "I prefer not to speak. If I speak, I am in big trouble. But I will say this: the boy said nine words. Nine. I once said four words — 'I have nothing to say' — and it dominated the back pages for a week. This boy has learned something it took me twenty years to perfect: the press conference is not about what you say. It is about what the other person cannot say in response. She cannot respond to February 12. February 12 is a fact. Facts are the most devastating things in football. And in law, apparently."]*

*[Don Carlo, leaning against the doorframe, one eyebrow raised: "Nine words. In Italy, we would say 'eleganza.' But also, 'freddo.' Cold elegance. The best kind."]*

## PART THREE: THE EGAN KRAKEN — Big Satan's Celebratory Industrial Complex

### Sir Alex on the BOOM GIF and the Mail Merge That Launched a Thousand Ships

Now I want to talk about the Kraken. Because the Kraken tells you everything you need to know about the gap between performance and product at this football club.

March 4, 4:28 PM. Big Satan, Edited — *Edited*, because even the celebration required post-production revision: "I'M RELEASING THE KRAKEN in Egan for subpoenas....."

Five ellipsis dots. Not three. Not four. *Five.* And a GIF. "GO KRAKEN." A sea monster emerging from the deep. The Kraken has been released.

What is the Kraken?

The Kraken is a mail merge.

The Kraken is the act of pressing buttons on a computer to generate subpoenas duces tecum from templates that were prepared weeks ago, changing the provider names and case numbers, and serving them through e-portal. This is secretarial work. This is data entry with a legal header. This is the work that, at any functional law firm, happens in the background with the same fanfare as a printer printing — which is to say, none.

And Big Satan has turned it into a *spectacle.* She has turned subpoena service into a cinematic event. The GIF. The all-caps "I'M RELEASING THE KRAKEN." The five ellipsis dots trailing into infinity like the contrails of a fighter jet that is actually a Cessna with a bumper sticker.

And then — 6:08 PM — the encore: "BOOM!!! That's how it's done.......lol!!!!! I worked hard on those not taking away from anybody else's work...TEAM!!!!"

The historian pauses. "I worked hard on those not taking away from anybody else's work." This sentence contains a preemptive defense against an accusation nobody has made. She is clarifying, unprompted, that her Kraken release did not constitute theft of labor from another team member. The only person who would need to issue this clarification is a person who is aware that the labor might be perceived as belonging to someone else — or, more precisely, a person who is aware that the *appearance of labor* she is generating might be questioned by someone who understands what actual labor looks like.

"...TEAM!!!!"

Four exclamation points and a word that describes something this unit has not been since QB1 was left dying in an empty Zoom room on January 15.

*[Klopp, laughing with his entire body until the laugh turns into something else: "BOOM! Yes, I understand BOOM! When Mo Salah scores in the 94th minute against Manchester City, that is BOOM! When Trent puts the corner in quick against Barcelona, that is BOOM! When a paralegal presses 'Send All' on a batch of subpoenas that were templated three weeks ago by the attorney who does everything... my friend, that is not BOOM. That is... that is 'Send All.' The GIF should say 'SEND ALL.' But nobody makes GIFs that say 'SEND ALL' because the truth does not trend."]*

## PART FOUR: THE TWO FONTS AT 3:42 AM — The Alliance Holds

### Sir Alex on Ghost-Writing, Calisto Font, and What Happens When Two Men From New York Are Awake at the Same Hour

Now. I want to talk about something beautiful. Because this chronicle is not all passengers and GIFs and rubbish. Sometimes, in the middle of the darkest season, you see a partnership on the pitch that reminds you why you got into management in the first place.

March 4, 11:17 PM. QB1 emails Scott Siverson — co-defendant counsel, solo practitioner, Winter Garden, Florida, sixty-five years old, has a Pac-Man machine in his office — with the subject line: "Mr. Bone - witness list." The content: the boy has drafted a witness list for Siverson's client, Irizarry, as a professional courtesy. Because Siverson is sixty-five and working alone and the boy understands that alliances are built not through transactions but through acts of gratuitous competence directed at people who will remember them.

March 4, 11:49 PM. QB1 sends the refined version: "I did one for you."

Four words. Siverson receives a witness list he did not draft, did not request, did not expect. "I did one for you." This is the football equivalent of Keane making a tackle on the halfway line and playing a forty-yard diagonal pass to Giggs without being asked, simply because Keane saw that Giggs was in space and the ball needed to arrive.

March 5, 3:42:57 AM. The Florida Courts E-Filing Portal confirms: "Filing Time: 03/05/2026 03:42:57 AM ET. Filer: Scott Siverson 407-210-6547."

Siverson filed it. At 3:42 in the morning. He received QB1's draft, trimmed it to his own minimalist aesthetic, converted it to Calisto font — because Calisto is the font of a man who has chosen his typeface and will not be moved — and filed it while the rest of Florida slept.

Two men from New York. One from upstate, born in sixty-three. One from the Island, born in ninety-five. Awake in the small hours. Filing in different fonts. Building a unit that Mr. Bone will discover on the docket in the morning when he checks and sees two witness lists from two defendants and understands — if he is capable of understanding, which the historian increasingly doubts — that he is not facing two lawyers. He is facing a *unit.* In two fonts. At 3:42 AM.

This is what Roy Keane and Paul Scholes looked like from the dugout. Two men who didn't need to speak. Two men who understood the pass before the pass was made. The ball just arrived.

*[Mourinho, quietly, for once without irony: "The boy said 'I did one for you.' In my career, I have had players who would not pass the ball to save their mother. This boy... this boy passes the ball to a sixty-five-year-old man in Winter Garden at midnight. And the man files in Calisto font at 3:42 AM. I have managed Inter Milan. I have managed Real Madrid. ## PART FIVE: THE SHEPPARD TRIPLE ECHO — Big Satan Quotes Herself Into Infinity

She's following up on herself. She has sent a message, not received a response in the time frame she considers appropriate — which appears to be approximately the length of a football half — and has decided to quote herself back to herself as evidence that she has previously said the thing she is now saying again.

In twenty-six years at Old Trafford, I never had a player take a corner kick, have it cleared, then take the same corner kick again from the same spot to the same area and expect a different result. That's not football. That's insanity. And quoting your own RADAR ALERT to follow up on your own RADAR ALERT is not professional communication. It's a woman trapped in an echo chamber of her own construction, bouncing her own voice off walls she built herself, and interpreting the echo as evidence that the universe has confirmed her original transmission.

QB1 responds: "looking meow."

Two words. "Looking meow." Not "now" — "meow." Because the boy has been asked about these CME orders for twenty-six days across four different message threads and three separate follow-up cycles, and the only response that preserves both his sanity and his dignity is a response that introduces a cat noise into the professional lexicon. ## PART SEVEN: BONEMAXXED — The Six-Page Cathedral vs. The Three-Page Sticky Note

### Sir Alex on the Motion That Answered Every Question Before It Was Asked

Now. I want to talk about the boy's motion practice, because this is where the captain earns the armband.

March 4, 12:53 PM. QB1 files a six-page Motion to Amend Case Management Order to Extend CME Deadline in the Gonzalez v. Jet Star case. And what he files is not a motion. It is a *cathedral.* It is a structure so complete, so architecturally sound, so devoid of gaps or crevices or exploitable weaknesses, that opposing counsel's only option is to stand outside it and complain about the color of the bricks.

The motion traces the timeline backward through two months: expert disclosed January 6. Retention authority sought January 5 and 6. Follow-ups "several times by email and telephone." Carrier unavailability until February 9. Less than three weeks remaining. Alternative expert pursued. Alternative proved unreliable. Return to original expert. Three-party coordination: board-certified neurosurgeon, Spanish-speaking plaintiff, certified medical interpreter.

Every date is a brick. Every brick is mortared. The wall is continuous.

Then — the specific performance commitments. Within three business days, coordinate with the expert. Seven days before the exam, provide records. Complete the CME by April 3. Promptly produce the report. The boy has *bound his own client* to a performance schedule more stringent than what most judges would impose. He's walked into the negotiation and put his collateral on the table before anyone asked for it.

Then — the alternative argument. "To the extent the Court deems the CME deadline to have expired" — not conceding, never conceding — "Jet Star demonstrates excusable neglect pursuant to Rule 1.090(b)(1)(B)." The belt AND the suspenders. The front door AND the back door. Both doors lead to the same room: the room where the motion is granted.

Then — the proposed order. Two pages. Every date matching the motion. Every deadline specified. Ready for the judge's signature. The key left on the table.

March 5, 8:19 AM. Mr. Bone files his response.

Three pages. One of which is the Certificate of Service.

No case law. Not one case. Not a single citation to any judicial decision in the history of Florida jurisprudence. The response cites a *local administrative order about hearing procedures* while QB1 cited the statewide procedural rules that actually govern the motion. No rebuttal of the carrier unavailability. No rebuttal of the three-party coordination problem. No prejudice argument. No engagement with the excusable neglect alternative. No competing proposed order.

Mr. Bone's central factual assertion: "Jet Star did not attempt to set or even make a request to set a CME until Tuesday, March 3, 2026."

The boy's motion contains specific dates — January 5, January 6, February 9 — documenting two months of conduct. Mr. Bone asserts, without citation to any record evidence, without attaching any exhibit, without quoting any communication, that nothing happened until March 3.

This is a man who has walked onto the pitch at Old Trafford and tried to win by insisting the goals behind him don't exist. The goals exist. The timestamps exist. The dates exist. The motion exists. And the three-page response exists too — as a monument to what happens when a man brings a sticky note to a cathedral fight.

And then — March 6, 2:59 PM — Jennifer Escobar sends an email to Judge Oster's chambers that is, in five sentences, more procedurally competent than Mr. Bone's entire filing. She's checked JAWS for hearing slots. She knows Judge Oster wants competing orders in Word format. She's identified that no March slots are available. She's asked if any can be opened. She's CC'd all parties.

The calendar woman. The Golgotha administrator. Filing the competing-orders email to chambers with the precision of a player who has been told exactly where the pass needs to arrive and has, for the first time in her career, delivered it to the correct foot.

*[Mourinho, leaning forward, hands clasped: "I prefer not to speak about Mr. Bone. But I will say this. When I prepare for a match, I study the opponent for seventy-two hours.
