## I. THE CANCELLATION GODS

The historian has observed, across a career spanning Mississippi porches and bourbon-soaked evenings with the ghosts of better men, that the universe occasionally rewards competence with the thing that competence made possible. Not luck. Not coincidence. The thing that would not have happened if the competent man had not set the conditions for it to happen. The cancellation gods do not visit empty courtrooms. They visit courtrooms where a man has already laid the foundation — the precisely worded email, the correct deadline cited, the motion framed for fifteen minutes of judicial attention — so that when the slot opens, the slot has somewhere to land.

Let us trace the Gonzalez-Jet Star CME hearing chain, because the chain is the proof.

Jennifer Escobar — Little Satan, the Post-Golgotha Alliance's operational wing — opened the correspondence with Carol Brite, Judicial Assistant to the Honorable Cynthia S. Oster, Circuit Civil Division E, on March 4, 2026. The motion to amend the case management order was submitted. The competing orders were attached in Word format per Judge Oster's procedure. Jen had done the thing. Jen had read the procedure. Jen had complied. This is the Post-Golgotha Alliance functioning: when assigned a task within her operational scope, Jen executes.

On March 13, Judge Oster requested a hearing be set. Jen checked JAWS — the Judicial Automated Workflow System, the digital ledger through which Hillsborough County's civil calendar breathes. No slots. She emailed Brite on March 16: "There are no hearing slots on JAWS showing. I will resend that email requesting hearing dates before April 25th as that is the deadline we are trying to resolve." On March 19 at 4:31 PM, Brite responded with the institutional equivalent of a locked door: "Unfortunately, due to our exceedingly full court calendar, we do not currently have any additional dates available other than what is in JAWS. Please feel free to check JAWS periodically for openings due to cancellations."

Exceedingly full. No dates. Check JAWS periodically. The door had closed. A lesser team — the kind of team that posts Michael Scott GIFs and bills .10 for pressing "download" — would have waited. Checked JAWS once a week. Let the April 3 CME deadline pass. Told the client the court was full. Billed .10 for the explanation.

Jen did not wait. At 5:12 PM on this same Thursday — the Thursday of the Blitzkrieg — she escalated with professional clarity: "Thank you for making us aware of the court's full calendar. However, if we wait for the beginning of April for the new hearing slots, we are concerned with the window for setting the motion that there won't be enough time as the deadline we need to extend is currently set for April 25th."

Here Jen made a small error. The deadline she cited was April 25th. The actual CME deadline at issue was April 3rd. The difference matters. April 25th communicates "we have a month, but we're concerned." April 3rd communicates *the house is on fire and the fire truck needs to arrive in two weeks.*

Fifteen minutes later — at 5:27 PM, at the tail end of a seven-front Blitzkrieg day, after converting a motion to compel into a mediation with Barnacle Boy jokes, after releasing a vehicle with three sentences of Learned Hand prose, after giving The Ice in nine words, after approving proposed orders and delivering executed releases and fielding Negrette Editorials from a woman who was remote with allergies and opinions — QB1 intervened.

"Ms. Brite, Good evening. Following up on Ms. Escobar's email below."

The sentence is surgical. He does not displace Jen. He does not suggest she was wrong. He *follows up.* He builds on her foundation. He amplifies her escalation with the precision that converts a judicial assistant's "unfortunately" into a judicial assistant's "we had a cancellation this morning."

Three facts. Three sentences. Each one a load-bearing wall:

"To clarify, the CME deadline at issue in our pending motion is April 3, 2026." Not April 25. April 3. The urgency is *imminent.*

"The case is set for jury trial the week of June 15–19, 2026." The trial exists. The CME must precede it. The arithmetic is visible.

"The earliest hearing availability currently showing in JAWS is a UMC on May 14, 2026 — well after the relief requested would need to take effect." The gap between May 14 and April 3 is the chasm through which the client's interests will hemorrhage if the court does not act. QB1 has held the gap up to the light and shown Carol Brite exactly where the bleeding starts.

"If the Court has any earlier availability, even on shortened notice, we would be grateful for the opportunity to be heard. The motion should require no more than 15 minutes."

*Even on shortened notice.* Three things simultaneously. Flexibility — we will take anything. Urgency — we are not asking for convenience, we are asking for mercy. Professionalism — we know what shortened notice means, we know the rules, we will comply. Fifteen minutes. A quarter of an hour. The time it takes a paralegal to type "Good Afternoon" and press send, seven times, for .10 each.

The email was sent at 5:27 PM on a Thursday evening. The building was emptying. The Meme Queen had gone remote. And QB1, at the end of a day that would have broken a lesser practitioner into component parts, sat down and wrote three paragraphs that would rearrange the court's April calendar.
