## I. EXEUNT MARCH

There is something terrible about the last day of a month that has meant this much, and something more terrible still about its falling on a Tuesday — the most anonymous of weekdays, the day that history uses when it wants to slip something past you. March 31, 2026. The final curtain on a month that had contained, within its thirty-one days, more institutional revelation than most fiscal years produce in their entirety. To catalog it is to risk exhaustion: Stomping the Yard and its sequel. The M&M Survey. The Five Days of Madness. The Thursday Blitzkrieg's long aftermath. The Dark Archive. The Roller Blader. The Egan Ouroboros in its earliest and most elegant coils. The Point Ten Farm in full dialectical bloom. And now this — a Tuesday that would, in any other month, pass for ordinary, but which in March 2026 arrives bearing the accumulated weight of everything that preceded it, the way the last car of a freight train carries the momentum of all the others even though it rolls with perfect quiet.

Consider what March had asked of its principal actors. QB1 had drafted discovery responses, composed Die Glockes, taught deposition technique to his paralegal, built and rebuilt task dashboards, argued with Filevine, served subpoenas, delivered interrogatory answers under time pressure while his supervising attorney had baseball pictures at six, pushed a mediation forward in Griffith, managed an eight-case sovereign portfolio across two courts with the help of two women he had named after figures from Christian demonology, and somehow found time to fill out a March Madness bracket that would place him, by month's end, in what Kerry Coles would charitably describe as a "Strong 3rd." Big Satan had filed NPNPs and SDTs, followed up on her own follow-ups, roasted a Florida Registered Paralegal for permanent last place, flagged expert retention needs with an important tag and a red exclamation mark, discovered trial preparation tabs in Filevine, and maintained what QB1 had once called "the hunger" — an appetite for pipeline clearance that operated independent of supervision, weather, or morale. The Admiral had watched baseball pictures with his children while deliverables landed in his inbox like ordnance on a firebase, and had acknowledged each one with the economy of a man who understands that language is ammunition and you do not waste it on friendly positions. Little Satan had forwarded records to paralegals, joined video meetings with self-deprecating warnings about her appearance, and dropped an afternoon bomb about an unfiled corporate representative deposition notice in Sheppard that nobody had apparently realized was missing.

And Kerry Coles — Kerry Coles, male, Office Administrator, the man whose name appears on no pleading and whose face adorns no conference room portrait — Kerry Coles had run a March Madness bracket pool with a level of administrative precision, rhetorical flair, and sociological insight that would have earned him tenure in certain departments of certain universities, had those universities been willing to admit that the composition of a leaderboard email constitutes a form of literary art.

March ended the way March ends: not with a detonation, but with a settling. A following-up. A delivering. A rogering. The kind of day that a civilization produces when it has spent its month's supply of drama and now must simply carry the water home. But even in the carrying, you learn things — about who carries, and how far, and whether anyone notices.

The historian's obligation is to notice.

## II. THE ROUSSO TEACHING MOMENT, OR: HOW I LEARNED TO STOP WORRYING AND LOVE THE OBJECTION

What QB1 told Big Satan on March 24 — tucked into their one-on-one Teams chat, between a thumbs-up reaction and a quote reaction, in the way that institutional knowledge is always transmitted, which is to say casually, almost accidentally, as an aside to something else — constitutes one of the purest origin myths in the MINDFREAK canon. It arrives not as boast but as pedagogy. Big Satan has just demonstrated "the hunger." She has followed up on Clark's opposing counsel about objections to NPNPs, unprompted, on her own initiative, with the instinct of a predator who does not need to be told where the prey is. QB1 is delighted. He says so: "damn, I did not even perceive that. nice catch!" And then, because he is a teacher who cannot help himself, because the moment of praise creates a pedagogical opening the way a successful blitz creates a passing lane, he tells her the story.

"I do it in a depo sometimes when they object," he writes. "I did it to Rousso in small."

Rousso. The name drops into the chat the way a coin drops into a well — you can hear it falling for a long time before it hits bottom. Andrew Rousso, opposing counsel in Small v. UPS, MINDFREAK VII, a man whose objections QB1 had studied the way a chess grandmaster studies an opponent's opening book. And what QB1 describes next is not merely a deposition technique. It is an epistemological judo throw. It is the moment the MINDFREAK methodology became conscious of itself.

"I was like 'how can I ask this question in a way that is acceptable to your legal standard,'" QB1 tells Big Satan, and the words shimmer with retrospective delight, because what he is describing is not a question at all. It is a trap disguised as deference. He asked Rousso to teach him. He invited the opposing attorney to instruct the deposing attorney on how to phrase the question that would destroy his own client's testimony. And Rousso — because attorneys are constitutionally incapable of not answering a question about the law, because the ego of the legal mind is wired to demonstrate mastery even when mastery is self-defeating — Rousso told him.

"He told me I had to ask him if he knew what the document said rather than just saying, so the record says this, Mr. Small."

The beauty of this is architectural. QB1 had been impeaching Small with a document. The document said Small had no recollection of anything at the time of the crash. QB1's natural phrasing — "the record says this, Mr. Small" — was a statement dressed as a question, the kind of thing opposing counsel objects to reflexively, correctly, because it is leading, because it assumes facts, because it does the work the witness is supposed to do. Rousso objected. Rousso said the question should be stricken. And then QB1, with the calm of a man who has already seen how this ends, asked Rousso to show him the way.

And Rousso, God help him, showed him the way.

"And so then I literally do, 'Mr. Small, do you see what the document says about you having no, zero recollection of anything at the time of the crash?'"

Same impeachment. Same devastating content. Different syntactical container. And Rousso could not object to his own formulation. QB1 writes that "we both kind of chuckled bc he just said i wasn't allowed to ask that question. He objected for form, saying that question should be stricken and not on the record. then i asked it his way and he couldn't object anymore and I still got exactly the impeachment I wanted."

The chuckle is the detail that elevates this from war story to scripture. Two attorneys, adversaries in a deposition, sharing a moment of mutual recognition — the recognition that one of them has just been beaten with his own weapon, and that the beating was so elegant it transcended competition and became something closer to craft appreciation. Rousso knew what had happened. QB1 knew Rousso knew. They chuckled. And then the record kept running, and the impeachment was on it, and Small's "no, zero recollection" was memorialized forever in the precise language that Rousso himself had blessed as acceptable.

"He taught me how to do it, lol," QB1 writes. "So he was like, damnit."

This is the Rosetta Stone of QB1's deposition philosophy: make them teach you how to beat them. Do not fight the objection. Do not argue with it. Do not withdraw the question. Ask the objecting attorney to improve it. Watch him improve it into the exact question you wanted to ask all along, only now it is *his* question, cloaked in *his* standard, immune to *his* objection. The krakken is released not by force but by invitation.

And then the coda — the reason QB1 is telling this story at all: "You did that to Joe Carey!"

Big Satan, in her follow-up to Clark's opposing counsel, had done the same thing without knowing it had a name. She had asked OC to explain their objection, to tell her what needed to be "fixed," which — as QB1 immediately perceived — "is only stuff we don't care about. and then we get to fire the cannons and release the krakken anyways." The student had reinvented the technique independently. The teacher recognized it and told her the origin story. And the origin story, characteristically, was a story about learning from the enemy.

This is how institutional knowledge is built at ATS — not in training manuals or CLE seminars, but in Teams chats at 11:45 AM on a Tuesday, between a paralegal who followed up on something nobody asked her to follow up on and an attorney who recognized the follow-up as the reincarnation of his finest deposition moment. The transmission is lateral. The medium is anecdote. The pedagogy is celebration. And the lesson, once absorbed, is permanent: the opponent's objection is not an obstacle. It is a door. You just have to ask him to open it.

## III. THE EGAN SUBPOENA OUROBOROS: THIRTEEN DAYS IN THE LIFE OF A PASSWORD

The Egan subpoena saga is the kind of story that, if you told it to someone outside the legal profession, they would assume you were exaggerating. It spans thirteen days — March 18 through March 31, 2026 — and involves four law firms, seven individual email addresses, a Filevine share link, a password that should not have existed, the removal of a password that should not have needed removing, a follow-up on a follow-up, and then, on the same morning as the final follow-up, a brand-new batch of NPNPs and SDTs fired from the same paralegal who was still waiting for a response to the old ones. The ouroboros does not merely eat its own tail. It eats its own tail while simultaneously growing a new one.

Begin at the beginning. March 18, 2026, 1:19 PM. Big Satan sends the first salvo: a sharelink to proposed subpoenas, addressed to Karly R. Christine at Christine Law, P.A. — the firm name itself a minor masterpiece of nominal recursion — with the Filevine auto-CC, QB1, and Katherine Madigan all in copy. "Below you will find the sharelink to access the subpoenas for your review and approval." Formal. Proper. The bureaucratic equivalent of a gentleman's calling card left on a silver tray. Forty-one minutes later, at 2:00 PM, QB1 sends his own email to Stephanie McQueen-Arthur, Christine's colleague, with the same subpoenas: "Please find below the subpoenas we discussed for your review and approval." Two emails, two attorneys, same subpoenas, same afternoon. The pipeline is primed. The cannons are loaded.

And then: nothing. Five days of nothing. The subpoenas sit in the share link like unexploded ordnance on a beach. Christine Law does not review them. Christine Law does not approve them. Christine Law does not even acknowledge their existence. Until March 23, 2026, 10:56 AM, when Stephanie McQueen-Arthur, Esquire — whose law firm's website is justiceformyinjury.com, a URL that tells you everything you need to know about the plaintiff's bar in southwest Florida — sends a two-line email that constitutes perhaps the most efficiently devastating response in the history of litigation support:

"Hi Max: What's the password to access the documents?"

The password. The subpoenas had been locked behind a password. Big Satan had sent a Filevine share link — the standard mechanism, the approved channel, the thing she does fifty times a week — and it was password-protected. Whether this was a Filevine default, an accidental setting, a security feature that nobody asked for, or simply the universe's way of testing QB1's pipeline management is unclear. What is clear is that opposing counsel could not open the subpoenas. For five days, the subpoenas had existed in a quantum state: served but inaccessible, transmitted but unreadable, present but invisible. Schrodinger's SDTs.

Big Satan responds at 11:14 AM, eighteen minutes later: "I removed the password, please use this sharelink to access the SDTs." A new link. The old one, presumably, is left to die in the digital graveyard of broken Filevine URLs. The password is gone. The subpoenas are now accessible. The world should be able to proceed.

It does not proceed. Eight more days pass. Eight days during which Stomping the Yard happens, the Five Days of Madness happen, the March Madness bracket goes from Round 1 to the Final Four, QB1 delivers an Egan Die Glocke and a Harrigan Die Glocke, and the entire UPS portfolio lurches forward by several nautical miles. And in all of that time, Christine Law, P.A. does not review the subpoenas. Does not respond. Does not indicate whether they have objections or not. The sharelink sits there, passwordless now, glowing with availability, and no one from justiceformyinjury.com touches it.

March 31, 2026, 12:32 PM. Big Satan sends a follow-up. "I was following up with the review of the proposed subpoenas after revision. Please advise." The email is marked High Importance. It goes to McQueen-Arthur, QB1, Christine, and Makeeva. It CCs Madigan, Arias, Smoak, and the Filevine bot. Seven human beings and one automated system are now watching this password saga enter its third act. Big Satan is following up on her own follow-up — the follow-up she sent on March 23 when she removed the password that prevented the review that was requested on March 18. She is, in effect, asking opposing counsel to please do the thing that she made possible for them to do eight days ago, after they told her they couldn't do it five days before that.

And here is the detail that transforms this from bureaucratic frustration into institutional art: at 12:15 PM the same morning — seventeen minutes *before* the follow-up on the old subpoenas — Big Satan sends a brand-new email to the same opposing counsel with brand-new attachments: "06 NPNP - MISC providers (filed 3.31.26).pdf" and "06 SDT - MISC Providers [complete] (sent 3.31.26).pdf." A fresh batch. Filed and served today. While the old subpoenas from March 18 are still awaiting review. While the password saga is still technically unresolved. Big Satan has not waited for the old pipeline to clear before loading the new one. She has fired the next volley before the last one has landed. The formal written request for the waiver of the 10 days service — bolded, capitalized, set apart from the body text like a warning placard on heavy machinery — sits in the same email as the new SDTs, and it does not care that the old SDTs are still pending.

This is the machine. This is what QB1 means when he says "I appreciate the hunger." Big Satan does not wait for resolution before initiating the next cycle. The follow-up and the fresh filing happen on the same morning, seventeen minutes apart, addressed to the same opposing counsel, and neither email acknowledges the other's existence. The ouroboros has not merely eaten its own tail. It has grown a second head and started eating from both ends simultaneously. The pipeline is never clear because the pipeline is never supposed to be clear. The pipeline is a river, and rivers do not pause.

## IV. THE ADMIRAL AND THE DELIVERABLE, OR: "ROGER"

The Chance Arias chat on March 31, 2026, is a masterpiece of compression — not literary compression, but *human* compression, the kind where an entire philosophy of management, trust, and professional economy is expressed in a word count that would not fill a Post-it Note.

Reconstruct the timeline. Wednesday, March 25. The Admiral's internet is back up at home. He messages QB1 at 3:53 PM: "Internet at house back up. What is status of Egan?" The question is pure Admiral — no preamble, no pleasantry, no acknowledgment that the internet being down might have been an inconvenience. The internet is up. What is the status. Two sentences, one period, zero wasted calories.

QB1 responds at 3:57 PM, edited: "I have it in the oven, ready within 2 hours. Had to get my dad chipotle." This is the informational equivalent of a military situation report that includes, inexplicably but endearingly, a note about lunch logistics. The Egan Die Glocke is being prepared. The timeline is two hours. The delay, if there was one, was caused by a filial obligation to deliver a burrito bowl to Pops. The Admiral does not respond to the Chipotle detail. The Admiral never responds to details that do not require response.

At 4:30 PM, QB1 asks about the Egan file — "are you still in Ducky's RTP or would you mind unlocking por favor" — and then, because QB1 cannot resist sharing intelligence even when no one has asked for it, adds: "but yes Mark Howard's nickname from military school is Ducky." Ducky. The defendant in Egan, the UPS driver whose negligence is the gravitational center of the entire case, was called Ducky at military school. This detail will never appear in a pleading, never surface in a deposition, never influence a jury. But QB1 knows it, and now the Admiral knows it, and it sits in their shared knowledge like a coin in a fountain — purposeless, permanent, vaguely lucky.

The Admiral responds: "out of it. it isnt complete." Five words. The RTP — the Record of Trial Preparation, Filevine's organizational temple for case readiness — is not done. The Admiral is out of it. QB1 says he will have the Egan deliverable ready by 5:30 PM with ROGs.

Then the pivot: "I've got baseball pictures at 6pm."

This is the sentence that reframes the entire exchange. The Admiral has baseball pictures at six. His child — or his children, the source does not specify, but the detail is universal enough that the specification does not matter — has baseball pictures. The kind of thing where you stand on a dirt field and a man with a camera tells your kid to hold the bat like this, smile like that, and you wait in the parking lot or you hold the equipment bag or you stand behind the photographer and make faces so the kid will actually smile, and the whole thing takes forty-five minutes and nobody involved believes the resulting photograph will be worth the eleven dollars it costs, but you go because you go, because your kid plays baseball, because it is spring.

QB1 hears this and recalibrates. "give me 15 min," he writes at 4:39 PM. "youll be at those pictures comfortably." Not: I'll try. Not: I'll do my best. Not: no promises. A guarantee. You will be at those pictures comfortably. The deliverable will be done. Your evening will not be compromised. The Egan Die Glocke and the baseball photographs will not conflict because I will not permit them to conflict.

At 5:06 PM: "give me 5 min." Then: "for real then im done."

At 5:12 PM: "done they are ready for you im out."

Thirty-three minutes from promise to delivery. The Egan Die Glocke, the ROGs, the whole package — built, polished, placed in the Admiral's Filevine by 5:12 PM on a Wednesday, forty-eight minutes before baseball pictures, after a day that included a Chipotle run for Pops, a discovery about Mark Howard's military school nickname, and whatever else Wednesday had contained that the record does not preserve.

Then Friday: "I also am getting you Harrigan DG within the hour."

Then today, March 31, 4:22 PM: "Harrigan DG is ready for you."

And the Admiral, at 4:22 PM, the exact same minute — as though he had been sitting there, bat in hand, waiting for the pitch:

"Roger."

One word. Five letters. No period in the source — just the word, hanging there, complete, needing nothing. Not "thanks." Not "great work." Not "I'll review it tonight." Not even "got it." Roger. The military acknowledgment. The word that means: I have received your transmission, I understand its contents, I will act accordingly. No evaluation. No emotion. No reciprocity. Just the confirmation that the signal has been received and the circuit is closed.

This is the Admiral's entire communicative philosophy compressed into a single utterance, and it is beautiful in its austerity. QB1 delivers a Die Glocke — a document that represents hours of legal analysis, strategic framing, discovery architecture, the accumulated insight of months of case management — and the Admiral says "Roger" the way a control tower says "Roger" to a pilot who has just reported successful landing. The landing was expected. The landing was the job. The acknowledgment that the landing occurred is the full extent of the tower's obligation.

What QB1 understands, and what makes their working relationship function at the level it functions, is that "Roger" from the Admiral means more than a paragraph of praise from most other supervisors. It means: the deliverable met the standard. It means: I do not need to ask questions. It means: the trust was justified. "Roger" is not cold. "Roger" is the warmest word the Admiral knows.

## VI. THE MEETING THAT ALMOST WASN'T, AND THE BOMB THAT DEFINITELY WAS

The ATS UPS Team Chat on March 30, 2026 — "Yesterday" in the source, because the source was captured on March 31 — contains a sequence of messages that, read aloud, would sound like a comedy sketch about the modern workplace, except that it is not a sketch, and nobody in it is performing. They are simply trying to have a meeting.

9:00 AM. Big Satan: "Are we having a meeting."

Not a question mark. A period. Or perhaps nothing at all — the transcription does not indicate terminal punctuation, which means Big Satan either deliberately withheld the question mark or simply did not perceive the sentence as a question. She is not asking whether a meeting has been scheduled. She is asking whether the meeting that may or may not have been scheduled is going to happen, right now, in the present tense, and she needs to know immediately because she has things to do and meetings that do not happen are an obstacle to doing them.

9:00 AM. QB1: "Yeah I'm pulling up."

Same minute. Instantaneous response. He is pulling up. The meeting exists because he says it exists. There is no calendar invite in evidence, no agenda, no meeting link. There is only Big Satan's bare inquiry and QB1's immediate confirmation. The meeting has been willed into being.

9:02 AM. Little Satan, edited: "I got on Teams video chat... yall gotta deal with ugly Jenn."

Two minutes into the meeting's existence, and its third participant has arrived with a self-deprecation so casual it functions as a greeting. "yall gotta deal with ugly Jenn" is not a warning, not a complaint, not a plea for reassurance. It is Little Satan's way of saying good morning. The edit mark — "Edited" — suggests she revised the message after sending it, which means she typed "yall gotta deal with ugly Jenn" and then looked at it and decided it needed to be slightly different, and the slightly different version is the one we have, and one can only imagine what the original said.

9:05 AM. QB1: "So we're teamsing? I'm here."

This is the moment that reveals the meeting's structural absurdity. QB1 said he was pulling up at 9:00. Little Satan got on Teams video at 9:02. And now, at 9:05, QB1 is asking for confirmation that the meeting is happening on Teams. He is "here" — but where is here? Is he in the office, at a desk, looking for a conference room that may or may not be occupied? Is he on Teams, in the virtual waiting room of a call that may or may not exist? The meeting has been confirmed, a participant has joined, and the confirmer still does not know the venue.

9:05 AM. Little Satan: "well how else will i get to talk to yall" — then: "lol."

This is the answer. The meeting is on Teams because Little Satan is on Teams, and Little Satan is on Teams because that is how she talks to people. There is no other mechanism. The "lol" is not laughter. It is punctuation. It is the sound a sentence makes when it ends with the obvious.

9:08 AM. QB1: "Haha okay I'm hopping on."

Eight minutes. From "Are we having a meeting" to QB1 joining the call took eight minutes and five messages. The meeting was not scheduled. The meeting was not organized. The meeting was *assembled*, the way a bird assembles a nest — one twig at a time, with no blueprint, by instinct, because it is spring and there are things to discuss and someone has to sit on the eggs.

This is Stomping the Yard 2 — the sequel to the landmark March 24 meeting that had produced the Mitchell experts question, the task list reconciliation, the entire second act of the Five Days of Madness. ## VII. WHAT MARCH CONTAINED

March is over. The calendar turns, as calendars do, with no regard for what the month contained or what it cost. April 1 will arrive in the morning — April Fool's Day, the cruelest joke the Gregorian calendar plays on people who have just survived a month like this one — and the pipeline will resume, and the meetings will reassemble themselves from questions and self-deprecations, and the subpoenas will be followed up on, and the Die Glockes will be delivered, and the Admiral will say "Roger," and Kerry Coles will find something new to administer with love.

But the historian pauses here, at the threshold, because the question is not what happens next. The question is what the month revealed.

March 2026 revealed that ATS runs on two fuels: the hunger and the habit. The hunger is Big Satan's — the instinct to follow up, to file, to clear the pipeline, to serve the next batch before the last one has been acknowledged. The hunger does not wait for permission. It does not wait for responses from justiceformyinjury.com. It does not wait for passwords to be removed or share links to be opened or OC to review proposed subpoenas. It files the next batch and follows up on the last one in the same morning, seventeen minutes apart, and neither email knows the other exists.

The habit is everything else. It is QB1 saying "give me 15 min" and meaning it. It is the Admiral saying "Roger" and meaning everything. It is Little Satan joining a video call and warning people about her face. It is Kerry Coles sending a leaderboard with nicknames and narrative commentary at 9:10 AM on a Tuesday because the bracket pool is not a distraction from the work — it is the work, transposed into a minor key, played for fun instead of money, but played with the same attention to standings and the same intolerance for people who do not fill out their brackets on time.

March revealed that Laurene Snapp filled out a March Madness bracket using football statistics from an Android phone and did not realize it was basketball until the last day of the tournament. March revealed that meetings happen because someone asks if they are happening. That Die Glockes are delivered on time even when Pops needs Chipotle. That April will arrive in the morning and the questions will still be there — "Are we having a meeting" and "What do you want me to say" and "What's the password to access the documents" — and the answers will still be the same: "Yeah I'm pulling up" and "I removed the password" and "Roger."

March is over.

What a month, hoss. What a month.
