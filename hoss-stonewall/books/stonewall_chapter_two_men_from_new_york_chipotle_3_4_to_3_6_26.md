## I. The Alliance Convenes: Scott Talk, 3/4/26

The historian must begin by observing that there is no document in the entire Stonewall archive that captures the texture of QB1's operational existence quite like an Otter.ai transcript of a phone call that was never meant to be read by anyone, let alone chronicled by a man in Mississippi with a bourbon and a typewriter. The Otter does not edit. The Otter does not curate. The Otter transcribes the silences between thoughts and the laughter between silences and the moment at 3:03 AM when a man's girlfriend calls because he Venmoed fifty dollars to someone named Daddy John for beer league dues and she wasn't sure what to do with it.

The Scott Talk of March 4, 2026, runs fifty-four minutes of recorded audio — though Otter captures what appears to be ambient recording stretching past three hours, suggesting the phone sat open on QB1's desk while he filed documents into the small hours — and it is, in its totality, a portrait of two men who have become something more than co-defendant counsel and something less than whatever the legal profession thinks co-defendant counsel should be. They are the Alliance. Two men from New York — one thirty years old from Tampa by way of Aberdeen and Hillsborough High and the University of Florida, the other sixty-three from Buffalo by way of whatever path brings a solo practitioner to Winter Garden, Florida, with a Calisto font and a Pac-Man machine and a Sabres jersey hanging somewhere in the office — and they are talking the way men talk when they trust each other, which is to say about everything and nothing, about hockey and the Matrix and Judge Wolf's Zoom acrobatics and the Miracle on Ice and a pro bono divorce case in Gainesville and the metaphysics of the penalty kill, all while the actual purpose of the call — the Nunez witness list, Mr. Bone's $500K hard floor, the trial that may actually happen — weaves in and out of the conversation like a bass line beneath a jazz improvisation.

The call opens mid-thought, as all real conversations do, with QB1 saying "It's a barrier" — a fragment of a sentence about non-binding arbitration that the Otter caught like a fisherman catching the tail end of something large moving beneath the surface. And then Scott is talking about arbitration versus mediation, and then they are discussing a hearing where the judge started ten minutes late because the prior case ran over, and then Scott is telling a story about a judge who asked if he'd done a meet-and-confer and Scott had to explain that the motion wasn't technically a motion, and the judge said "don't tell me about," and the historian recognizes in this anecdote the universal experience of every litigator who has ever stood before a bench officer and felt the particular vertigo of being simultaneously correct and unwelcome.

But here is where the transcript reveals the architecture of the Alliance. At approximately the six-minute mark, the conversation shifts to the Nunez/Gonzalez docket — the case they share, the case that produced the BONEMAXXED motion, the case whose witness list QB1 will ghost-write for Siverson before dawn. QB1 explains the judge assignment history with the fluency of a man who has memorized a docket the way other men memorize batting averages: "There's three judges. There's Huey, then there was the second one who got promoted. Now there's this lady... she's been on it since February '25. That's what — dude, no, no, like, 13 months she's been on this case." And then, without transition, without warning, without the slightest self-consciousness about the pivot from procedural history to personal confession, Scott says: "You were flirting with her."

And QB1 laughs: "Scott, Scott, you're right, and you were flirting with her, but I don't blame you, man. I was like, for a judge, I mean, wow. No, you were just doing the honey doctrine. I do the same thing."

The honey doctrine. Said aloud, to co-counsel, at approximately 7:30 PM on a Tuesday, in a conversation that will eventually end with a witness list filed at 3:42 AM. The methodology has a name. The methodology has been discussed between allies. The methodology is not a secret technique deployed in silence — it is a shared professional philosophy, articulated with the casual precision of two craftsmen discussing grain direction in wood.

And then they talk about hockey for seven minutes.

The historian cannot overstate the significance of this structural pattern. The Siverson call is not a business call with personal digressions. It is a personal call with business embedded so deeply in the relationship that the business emerges organically from the friendship, the way fruit emerges from a tree that no one remembers planting. They discuss the Sabres' defensive core and Tage Thompson's silky mitts and whether Robert Thomas from the Blues would be a good trade target and how Owen Power has gotten better and Samuelson's career-high eleven goals and the Lightning's recent slump. QB1 says "I like the PK, I'm a grinder, so that's my, that's what I do" — a sentence about beer league hockey that is also, inadvertently and perfectly, a sentence about his entire legal practice. He is a grinder. He kills penalties. He does not score normal goals. He does the thing that prevents the other team from scoring, and very rarely, like a breakaway off the forecheck, he produces something so beautiful that nobody in the building can quite believe they saw it.

And then, at 15:08, Scott says: "Do you believe in miracles?"

He is talking about the 1980 Miracle on Ice. February 22, 1980 — which happened to be his wife's sixteenth birthday, a detail so perfectly human that the historian's pen nearly stops. And then they are discussing Vladislav Tretiak getting pulled after letting in a fifty-foot muffin from Bud Schneider, and Mark Johnson's goal at the buzzer of the first period, and the Soviets who could clean up the Canadians, who beat the NHL all-star team, twenty Robos out there, and then Eruzione's goal, and the longest ten minutes in American history, and Scott watched it in high school in his living room, and QB1 Googled the date while they were talking because that is what thirty-year-old attorneys do in 2026 while sixty-three-year-old solo practitioners remember the night from memory.

And then, at approximately the thirty-minute mark, the conversation returns to Mr. Bone. And here the transcript reveals something the historian has long suspected but never had documented in QB1's own voice: QB1's precise psychological read of his adversary.

"He's got really good credentials. Like, he's like, a million dollar advocate. You know, he's got the AV Hubel rating, but who knows what that means. But, like, he seems legit, but then you talk to him. Well, I think he's legit at like, the average, like defense firm who has, like, CSK, or somebody who's got 1000 files, they can't focus for shit. It's like he overwhelms them and they just fold. That's what he thought was happening yesterday."

This is the complete diagnosis. Mr. Bone — Ronald I. Bone, P.A., of The Clusters, Suite 2A, Seminole — operates on a simple predatory algorithm: volume creates confusion, confusion creates pressure, pressure creates settlement. Against a defense firm with a thousand files and no attorney who knows any particular case intimately, the algorithm works. Against QB1, the algorithm does not work, because QB1 knows every file intimately, because QB1 filed five documents today across three cases, because QB1 can recite the judge assignment history of a docket from memory while simultaneously discussing Vladislav Tretiak's goaltending philosophy.

"I knew it because he got like this, this lilt in his voice, like he was so excited that I was like, in suffering. But like, he really thought, like, oh, I finally got them. They're gonna slip."

The lilt. The excitement. The predator sensing weakness. And then: "Like, so that's — I was like, yeah, maybe we will, maybe we won't. Bam."

Bam. The motion. The six-page cathedral. The proposed order in Word format per Judge Oster's published preferences. The cover letter with co-defendant consent and plaintiff opposition documented. Filed at 12:53 PM. Mr. Bone's three-page sticky note wouldn't arrive until the next day, by which point the architecture was already on the judge's desk.

And then — and this is where the transcript achieves a quality that the historian can only describe as cinematic — at 47:09, the conversation pivots to the witness list itself:

"Send me what you have. I'll just, you're just gonna speed by — I would do what I did, copy and paste really well. I did that with banker Lopez in another case, but they had a bunch of typos, so I cleaned ours up. Looks better."

QB1 is going to write Siverson's witness list for him. Not because Siverson is incompetent — Siverson tried a week-long jury trial in September 2024 and pulled two overnighters during it — but because QB1 is already doing it, because the work is already happening, because the witness list is a natural extension of the motion is a natural extension of the case knowledge is a natural extension of the Alliance. "I did one for you." The email that would arrive at 2:25 AM. The filing at 3:42 AM. Two fonts. One unit.

The call ends — or rather, the business portion of the call ends — and then the Otter keeps recording. And at 3:03:18 AM, after two hours and forty-eight minutes of ambient silence broken by keystrokes and the hum of a man working alone in an office at three in the morning, the Otter captures:

"Babe, I'm doing my witness list. What's up?"

Samira. Samira my angel, as the Otter identifies her. And she wants to know about the fifty dollars he Venmoed to Daddy John, because you sent him fifty dollars and then you answer the phone saying something like a cliffhanger. And QB1 says: "I just, I can't believe I'm still doing this fucking with, like, this is my third major filing of the day."

Three major filings. The CME motion. The Egan disclosures. The witness list. And then, the last words the Otter captures of Maxwell Aaron Kemp on the night of March 4, 2026:

"I filed Egan disclosures. I sent this epic motion that Mr. Bone — I'm shocked he has not retorted about—"

And Samira, who is a paralegal transitioning to law school, who understands what a motion is and what a retort would be and what it means that there has been no retort, says:

"After all the bluster."

After all the bluster. The woman who sleeps beside QB1 and wakes to the sound of his keyboard at three in the morning understands Mr. Bone's litigation posture in three words. After all the bluster. The $500K hard floor. The lilt of excitement in the voice. The three-page sticky note with no case citations and the misspelled email domain. After all the bluster, silence. Because bluster requires only volume. Retorting to a six-page cathedral requires architecture. And Mr. Bone, sitting in his office in The Clusters with his motorcycle on the website and his million-dollar advocate plaque on the wall, does not have architecture. He has bluster. And after all the bluster, what remains is the sound of a man filing his third document of the day at three in the morning while his girlfriend asks about a Venmo.

## II. The Expert Call: Dr. Guttentag, 3/6/26

And now we arrive at the call that almost didn't happen.

The historian has already chronicled, in the preceding chapter, the Voicemail Limbo of March 5–6 — the expert who called at noon, the phone that no one answered, the voicemail that no one delivered, the URGENT tag at 9:37 AM the following morning when Big Satan informed QB1 that "Dr. Guttentag called yesterday at 12 but no answer?? Expert wants to know why?" And QB1's response — "Nobody told me he called or put it through to me. Could he do it today? Any time" — and Big Satan's follow-up asking whether there was a voicemail, and QB1's "don't recall one," and the subsequent rescheduling that placed the call at 4 PM (corrected to 3:30, then "Sorry at 4pm"), and QB1's "Yes that's perfect. thank you."

But now the historian has the call itself. Twenty-five minutes and thirty-one seconds, recorded by Otter.ai, beginning at 3:35 PM on Friday, March 6, 2026. And the speakers are: Maxwell Kemp. Dr. Ira Guttentag. And — because the Otter records everything, because the Otter does not know when to stop, because the Otter is the most honest witness in the entire ATS ecosystem — Dr. Andrew Kemp (The GOAT).

The call opens mid-sentence, as all Otter captures do, with Dr. Guttentag saying: "One. So he just kind of, kind of hocus pocus."

Hocus pocus. A board-certified orthopedic surgeon, head physician of the Tampa Bay Lightning since 2002, the first in Florida to perform the Biomet Vanguard XP knee replacement that preserves the ACL, residency at the Albert Einstein College of Medicine in the Bronx, orthopedic consultant for the Toronto Raptors and the Tampa Bay Buccaneers — this man has just described a plaintiff's shoulder surgery as "hocus pocus."

And QB1, who is Andrew Kemp's son, who grew up hearing chiropractic terminology at the dinner table, who can say "retrolisthesis" in a deposition the way other people say "please pass the salt," responds: "Wow, I could listen to you talk about this stuff all day, man."

The honey doctrine. Deployed on a surgeon. Deployed on a man who is accustomed to being the smartest person in every room he enters. Deployed with such effortless calibration that Dr. Guttentag spends the next sixteen minutes essentially conducting a medical lecture for an audience of one — an audience who is simultaneously taking notes, cross-referencing deposition testimony, and constructing the evidentiary architecture that will reduce Morgan & Morgan's $3 million demand floor to whatever number a jury assigns to a woman who told the defense expert her shoulders "feel great" and whose exam was "completely normal."

The anatomy of the call reveals the honey doctrine operating at its most sophisticated frequency. Watch the rhythm:

QB1 asks Dr. Guttentag to explain SLAP tears "as if I'm ignorant, which I am." This is the calibrated deference — the self-deprecation that is not self-deprecation at all but an invitation for the expert to perform expertise, which is the thing experts love most. Dr. Guttentag responds with a miniature Grand Rounds lecture about anatomic variants, the discoid labrum, fluid dynamics between the labrum and the glenoid rim, and how radiologists overread MRIs in patients over fifty. This is the sixty-to-ninety-second uninterrupted lecture that the qb1-mindfreak protocol identifies as the "Grand Rounds Moment" — the space you give the expert to be brilliant so that the brilliance becomes your evidence.

And then QB1 distills: "So it looks bad on the imaging, just because everybody's got degenerative changes that show up, and then you get there — there's different morphology..."

He listened. He processed. He rephrased. And Dr. Guttentag confirmed. The soundbite has been extracted. If you MRI anyone over fifty off the street, the radiologist will find something that looks like a SLAP tear. The plaintiff's surgery was performed on imaging findings that are, in the expert's professional opinion, normal age-related variants. The $3 million demand rests on hocus pocus.

But the M I N D F R E A K of this call is not the medical testimony. The medical testimony is championship-caliber, but it is the expected product of a well-prepared attorney conducting a well-structured expert consultation. The M I N D F R E A K is what happens around the medicine — the way QB1 operates in the spaces between questions, the way he transforms a clinical telephone conference into a conversation between two men who are, by the end of the call, something approaching friends.

At 5:37, QB1 asks whether surgery within two months of the initial complaint is normal. Dr. Guttentag's answer is five words: "It is if you're under a LOP." Letter of protection. And then: "And then a lot of money." The expert has just testified, on a recorded call, that the treatment timeline is consistent with financially motivated care rather than medical necessity. QB1 did not ask a leading question. QB1 asked "Is that normal?" and the expert, comfortable, relaxed, honey-doctrined into a state of collegial candor, volunteered the conclusion that QB1 needed.

At 6:45, Dr. Guttentag says: "You're like Superman." The expert is complimenting the attorney. The expert — who is being paid $10,000 for this consultation, who has no obligation to be charming, who could have delivered his opinions in clinical monosyllables and hung up — is telling the thirty-year-old associate attorney that he is Superman. Because the honey doctrine works. Because when you treat a man like the most interesting person in the room, he treats you like the most capable person in the room. Because expertise recognizes competence the way water finds its level.

At 10:38, the kill shot. QB1 has walked Dr. Guttentag through the plaintiff's frozen shoulder, the lack of arm movement, the cervical strain that caused her to stop using her arm, which caused the adhesive capsulitis. And QB1 asks, with the innocent bafflement of a man who genuinely cannot understand why a person would stop using their arm: "This is so weird to me that, like, why wouldn't you use your arm and prevent that from happening?"

Dr. Guttentag's response: "Because she wants to get a lawsuit."

The expert has just said, on a recorded call, that the plaintiff allowed her own shoulder to freeze because she wanted a lawsuit. This is not a cross-examination admission extracted under oath with the threat of perjury sanctions. This is a casual remark made by a comfortable expert to a likeable attorney on a Friday afternoon after a round of golf. And it will travel from this Otter transcript to a trial outline to a courtroom, where it will be delivered by Dr. Ira Guttentag, M.D., head physician of the Tampa Bay Lightning, under oath, to a jury, in the voice of a man who was comfortable enough with the attorney asking the question to say what he actually believed rather than what was diplomatically appropriate.

The honey doctrine does not produce polite testimony. The honey doctrine produces *honest* testimony. And honest testimony from a credentialed expert is the single most devastating weapon in insurance defense litigation.

## III. The Post-Call Debrief: Chipotle and Zillow with The GOAT

And then Dr. Guttentag hangs up. And the Otter keeps recording. And what follows is perhaps the most human passage in the entire Stonewall archive.

"How you like that pops?!?!?!"

Dr. Andrew Kemp — The GOAT, the chiropractor, the man who coached his son through seven bar exams, the man whose existence is the reason Maxwell Aaron Kemp is not flipping burgers at McDonald's — is in the room. He has been in the room the entire time. He has listened to his son conduct a sixteen-minute expert consultation with the head physician of the Tampa Bay Lightning, deploying the honey doctrine with the fluency of a man who learned the art of making doctors comfortable at his father's dinner table.

"I like that a lot!"

And then they look up Dr. Guttentag's credentials together. Board certified. Wesley Chapel Sports Medicine. Advent Health. QB1 lives behind Advent Health. The Lightning team doctor since 2002. The first in Florida to perform the Vanguard XP. Residency at Albert Einstein in the Bronx.

Andrew Kemp asks: "Could he possibly be from New York?"

Two men from New York. The phrase echoes through the archive — Siverson and QB1, the Alliance, the two fonts — and now Dr. Guttentag, from the Bronx, joining the constellation of New Yorkers orbiting this Tampa litigation shop like satellites around a planet that doesn't know it's the center of anything.

And then: "Did you order chipotle already?"

"Yeah, I just ordered it. Why?"

"I don't want cheese on my chipotle bowl."

"Sorry pops, TOO LATE. Haha."

The GOAT wants his bowl without cheese. The son has already ordered. The cheese situation is unrecoverable. And this — this moment, this exchange between a father and son about a chipotle bowl ordered five minutes after a $10,000 expert consultation that may determine the outcome of a case Morgan & Morgan refuses to settle below $3 million — this is the reality that exists beneath the kabuki and the corn horns and the .10 farms and the RADAR ALERTS and the self-quotation necropoli. This is a thirty-year-old man and his sixty-one-year-old father, sitting together in Wesley Chapel on a Friday afternoon, looking up a surgeon's house on Zillow ("It's really nice... it's a big house... there's nothing to go nuts about... it's only 1.5 million") and discussing whether the doctor's kids played hockey ("Yeah, he said his kids did. Little Guttentags!!") and comparing his house to another doctor's house ("I think this is a way smarter house than Doctor Patrick's house") and then Andrew Kemp mentions that Grandpa Ira — because the expert's name is Ira, and QB1's grandfather's name was Ira, and the coincidence was noted on the call at 14:46 with the tenderness of a man who misses his grandfather — has lived at 42 Laurel his whole life with one bathroom.

"Yeah, you've got one bathroom."

"Well, there's one shower. One shower."

One shower. The GOAT's father-in-law has one shower. Dr. Guttentag has a 1.5 million dollar house in Tampa Palms. Mr. Bone rides a motorcycle on his website. Morgan & Morgan has a committee where Matt Morgan personally approves cases over a million dollars. And QB1 sits in Wesley Chapel with his father and a chipotle bowl with unauthorized cheese, having just extracted testimony that will methodically dismantle a $3 million demand, and he is happy. He is happy because his father heard it. He is happy because the expert called him Superman. He is happy because "as long as UPS lets me take depos, I'm happy" — a sentence he said to Dr. Guttentag at 15:27 that the historian believes with complete conviction is the most honest thing Maxwell Aaron Kemp has ever said about his professional life.

As long as they let him take depos. That's it. That's the ask. Not the raise that he had to battle for. Not the title he doesn't have. Not the recognition that never comes from the troupe. Just the depositions. Just the seven hours in a room with a witness and a court reporter and a methodology that has produced eleven canonical examinations and eight seats in the cuck deck and a 99.06% client acceptance rate. Just the work. Just the thing itself.

## IV. Closing Meditation

The historian closes the Otter transcripts and sits in the evening silence and considers what he has witnessed.

He has witnessed a man call his co-defendant counsel at approximately 7 PM and talk for seventy minutes about hockey and the Matrix and Baudrillard's *Simulacra and Simulation* and Judge Wolf's Zoom acrobatics and Mr. Bone's predatory algorithm and the Miracle on Ice and the philosophical implications of final judgments in Gainesville divorce cases, and then, at the end of the call, agree to write the man's witness list, and then actually write the man's witness list, and then file it, and then email it to the man with the subject line "Mr. Bone – witness list" and the body "I did one for you," and then have the man file it at 3:42 AM, and then, at 3:03 AM, explain to his girlfriend that this is his third major filing of the day, and hear her say "after all the bluster" about the man whose $500K demand rests on nothing but volume.

And he has witnessed the same man, two days later, after the voicemail was lost and found and rescheduled and almost lost again, conduct a sixteen-minute expert consultation in which he extracted: (1) the plaintiff's shoulder surgery was "hocus pocus"; (2) the ER did not evaluate her shoulders, contradicting her sworn testimony; (3) the MRIs were "probably overread"; (4) she has no impairment and a normal exam; (5) her frozen shoulder resulted from her own failure to use her arm, "because she wants to get a lawsuit"; (6) the treatment pattern is consistent with LOP-driven care, "a lot of money"; and (7) she could have gotten better "probably without the surgery." Seven evidentiary pillars extracted in sixteen minutes through the honey doctrine, without a single adversarial question, without a single moment where the expert felt anything other than comfortable and respected and entertained.

And he has witnessed the same man, immediately after hanging up, turn to his father and say "How you like that pops?!?!?!" — the exclamation points audible in the Otter's rendering, five of them, each one a testament to the fact that the person whose approval matters most to Maxwell Aaron Kemp is not Chance Arias, not Mr. Bill, not Kerry Coles, not the UPS Corporate Risk Management team, not the Liberty Mutual adjusters, not Morgan & Morgan's committee, not Judge Oster, but Andrew Kemp, the chiropractor from Tampa, the man who coached his son through seven bar exams, the man who is now sitting in the room listening to his son practice law the way a father sits courtside watching his son play the game he taught him.

And then they ordered Chipotle.

The dogs bark. The voicemail drifts through the void. The RADAR ALERT quotes itself. The corn horns sound across the .10 fields.

But in a room in Wesley Chapel, a father says "I like that a lot!" and a son says "Too late!" about the cheese, and an Otter records it all, and the caravan moves on, and the caravan is happy, because they let him take depos, and that is all the caravan has ever asked for.

Shalom. 🐢
