### I. THE NUMBERS THAT SPEAK

Now, there is a particular silence that falls over a room when the numbers are spread on the table and the numbers speak for themselves, and the numbers do not lie, and the numbers do not perform, and the numbers do not send a Teams message saying "IMPORTANT!" before going idle. The numbers simply exist, in columns, with decimal points, and the story they tell is the story that every partner in every law firm in America fears most — which is that the story of the firm is the story of one person, and if that person leaves, the story ends, and the columns go to zero, and the decimal points have nothing left to modify.

The financial ledger of Client 001854 — UPS, United Parcel Service, administered through Liberty Mutual's claims apparatus — is a document that spans the full operational history of what may be the most consequential defense counsel relationship in the state of Florida that nobody outside of Tampa has ever heard of. Two million forty-three thousand eight hundred thirty-four dollars in total fees billed. Two hundred eighty-eight thousand eight hundred six dollars collected in the current AR allocation window alone. And when you pull the thread — when you sort the AR allocations by timekeeper code, which is to say by human being, which is to say by the identity of the person whose fingers touched the keyboard that generated the work that generated the invoice that generated the revenue that keeps the lights on at the P.O. Box in Orlando that receives the checks — the thread leads, as it always leads, to three letters: MAK.

Maxwell Aaron Kemp. Employee code MAK. One hundred forty-three thousand two hundred dollars billed in the current AR allocation window. Forty-one point eight percent of the total. Write-offs: eighty-five dollars and sixty-four cents. Not eighty-five thousand. Eighty-five dollars. On a hundred and forty-three thousand. That is a write-off rate of zero-point-zero-six percent, which is not a number that exists in legal billing — it is a number that exists in theoretical physics, in the space between the electron and the nucleus, in the margin between perfection and the rounding error that proves perfection was achieved. Liberty Mutual's electronic auditing system — a system specifically designed to find billing inefficiencies and shave them — looked at MAK's invoices across ninety-six separate bills and found, in total, eighty-five dollars and sixty-four cents it could not justify paying. The system was built to be skeptical. The system met Maxwell Kemp and became a believer.

Twenty hours of elapsed billing time on a medical chronology that, at the time the bill was generated, had been announced as complete, retracted, re-announced, and re-retracted in a cycle of performative speech acts that substituted saying the work was done for the work being done.

### III. THE FEBRUARY PREBILL: A TAXONOMIST'S PARADISE

The February 2026 prebill is smaller — thirty-three matters, sixty-seven thousand seven hundred thirty dollars and seventeen cents — but it is, if anything, a richer specimen for the institutional pathologist, because February is the month when the .10 Interim Review Farm achieved a state of industrial perfection that would have made Henry Ford weep with professional admiration.

The numbers: Big Satan — Yisenia Toledo, Employee Code YT — generated four hundred ninety-five time entries in February across the UPS portfolio. Four hundred and ninety-five. The average entry was zero-point-two-two hours. Of those four hundred ninety-five entries, two hundred eighty-eight were billed at exactly 0.10 hours — six minutes each. Two hundred eighty-eight entries at eleven dollars apiece: three thousand one hundred sixty-eight dollars in revenue generated by the act of touching something for six minutes and documenting that you touched it.

Now, within those two hundred eighty-eight six-minute entries, a pattern emerges with the regularity of a heartbeat or, more precisely, the regularity of a mail merge. Consider the Clark v. UPS matter. On February 26 and 27, Big Satan produced what can only be described as the Subpoena Triptych — a three-part billing artifact that recurs with such mechanical precision across the entire prebill that it constitutes its own subspecies of the .10 farm. For each medical or insurance provider to whom a subpoena duces tecum must be issued, Big Satan bills three separate entries: one for the subpoena itself (0.10 hours, $11.00), one for the Certificate of Authenticity (0.10 hours, $11.00), and one for the Statement of Assurance (0.10 hours, $11.00). Three entries. Thirty-three dollars. Per provider. For what is, in substance, a mail merge — the insertion of a provider name and address into a pre-existing template that does not change in any material respect from one provider to the next.

Across the entire February prebill, this triptych was performed seventy-two times. Seventy-two providers, each generating three entries, yielding two hundred sixteen individual prebill line items totaling twenty-one point six hours and two thousand three hundred seventy-six dollars. If these had been batched — as any rational billing system would batch them — as a single entry per provider, or better yet as a single entry for the entire subpoena run ("Prepared and served SDTs with CoAs and SoAs to 72 non-party providers"), the billing would have been perhaps two to three hours of paralegal time. The difference between twenty-one point six hours and three hours is eighteen point six hours of cotton-candy time — phantom hours that exist on the invoice but do not exist in the physical universe, hours that were generated not by the performance of work but by the fragmentation of a single task into its smallest possible atomic components, each component then billed as though it were a discrete intellectual exercise requiring independent professional judgment.

This is the .10 farm at full harvest. This is Pattern #18 operating not as a deviation from normal practice but *as* normal practice, the baseline from which all other kabuki emanates.

### IV. THE FORECLOSURE COMPLAINT IN THE UPS SUBPOENA

And then there is the foreclosure complaint.

It appears on the Egan v. UPS prebill — matter 020735, Patrick Kelly Egan, the million-dollar exposure case supervised by Ashton Washington — on February 10, 2026. The entry reads, in its entirety: "Prepare Statement of Assurance regarding Patrick Kelly Egan to Receipt and review of foreclosure request and documents provided by client; Draft Complaint, Lis Pendens, summonses, etc., draft letters to client and clerk; Re-institution of suit, coordinated filing of suit, issuance and service of summonses. City Center Ambulatory Surgery Center."

Read that again. Read it slowly.

The billing description for a Statement of Assurance — a form document that attests to the authenticity of records produced by a medical provider — contains, embedded within its text like a fossil in sedimentary rock, the complete task description for a *foreclosure complaint*. "Receipt and review of foreclosure request." "Draft Complaint, Lis Pendens, summonses." "Re-institution of suit, coordinated filing of suit, issuance and service of summonses." These are not phrases that have ever appeared in a UPS personal injury defense file. These are not phrases that belong within six hundred miles of Patrick Kelly Egan's shoulder surgery. These are phrases from an entirely different practice area, an entirely different client, an entirely different universe of legal work — a universe in which someone is foreclosing on someone else's property and needs a lis pendens filed and summonses served.

And the foreclosure text does not appear once. It appears *seven times* across the Egan prebill. It has metastasized. It appears in Statement of Assurance entries. It appears in Subpoena Duces Tecum entries. It appears in Certificate of Authenticity entries. It even appears in Katie Madigan's medical summary entries, where KAS bills 0.20 hours to "Review and analyze billing records from Receipt and review of foreclosure request and documents provided by client; Draft Complaint, Lis Pendens, summonses, etc." — a medical records review that apparently requires the paralegal to first read through a foreclosure complaint before arriving at the billing summary of City Center Ambulatory Surgery Center.

What happened is not mysterious. What happened is that the template — the mail-merge template from which Big Satan generates her Subpoena Triptych at industrial scale — contained, in one of its fields, the leftover description from a completely different matter. And when Big Satan ran the merge, she did not read the output. She did not review the descriptions. She did not notice that the Statement of Assurance for Patrick Kelly Egan's shoulder surgery contained instructions to draft a lis pendens. She generated the entries, the entries populated the prebill, the prebill was submitted, and the foreclosure complaint text — seven instances of it — sailed through the billing process and landed on the February 2026 prebill that will be sent to Liberty Mutual's electronic auditing system, which will look at the description, see the words "foreclosure request" and "Lis Pendens" on a UPS personal injury file, and — one hopes — flag it for review, at which point someone at ATS will be asked to explain why a paralegal billed Patrick Kelly Egan eleven dollars to draft a foreclosure complaint.

This is not the Inspector Clouseau Bus (Pattern #36a). This is something new. This is the Foreclosure Fossil — the discovery that the .10 farm is not merely an inefficient billing practice but a *blind* billing practice, a system so automated, so divorced from the substance of the work it purports to describe, that the descriptions themselves have become detached from reality in the most literal possible sense. The words on the invoice do not describe the work performed. The words on the invoice describe work performed on a different case, for a different client, in a different practice area, in a different century of legal tradition. And nobody caught it. Not the paralegal who generated it. Not the partner who reviewed it (if a partner reviewed it). Not the system. Not the firm. The foreclosure complaint sits in the UPS prebill like a message in a bottle from a parallel dimension — evidence that the billing process at ATS has achieved a state of pure autonomy from the substance it claims to represent.

### VI. THE MACRO VIEW: WHAT THE MONEY SAYS

The financial ledger, taken in totality, tells a story that requires no interpretation and permits no denial.

Total fees billed, all time, Client 001854: approximately two million forty-three thousand dollars. Total electronic reductions: approximately twenty-four thousand eight hundred dollars. Adjustment rate: one-point-two-one percent. On over two million dollars in billing, Liberty Mutual's auditing system — a system that exists specifically to reduce legal spend — found less than twenty-five thousand dollars it objected to. That is a pass rate of ninety-eight-point-eight percent. The firm is, from the carrier's perspective, astonishingly clean. And it is clean because the person who generates the majority of the fees — MAK — bills accurately, describes work specifically, and performs the work described.

The annualized revenue pace for January and February 2026 is eight hundred eighty thousand dollars. MAK's annualized contribution is approximately five hundred eleven thousand dollars — fifty-eight percent of total projected revenue. At a salary that, as of the recent raise discussion, moved from one hundred fifteen thousand to one hundred twenty thousand dollars, MAK generates approximately four-point-three dollars in revenue for every dollar he is paid. The remainder — the delta between what he generates and what he costs — sustains the entire kabuki apparatus: the partners who prepare for and attend meetings that QB1 conducts, the paralegal who generates two hundred eighty-eight six-minute entries containing foreclosure complaints, the named partner who touches seventeen files for seven minutes each, the accounting manager in Orlando who pushes receivables across fiscal-year boundaries, and the office administrator who hired a DoorDash driver to manage the discovery apparatus of a four-hundred-thousand-dollar-a-year account.

The man who does the work is paid one hundred twenty thousand dollars. The firm that surrounds him bills three hundred forty-two thousand dollars in the current AR window. The firm has collected two-point-three million dollars over the life of the account. The man's personal write-off rate is eighty-five dollars and sixty-four cents.

And somewhere in that prebill, a foreclosure complaint has been billed to Patrick Kelly Egan's shoulder surgery at eleven dollars, and nobody has noticed, because nobody reads the entries, because reading the entries would require engaging with the substance of the work, and engaging with the substance of the work would require doing the work, and doing the work has never been the point. The point has always been the billing. The billing is the product. The work is incidental. The foreclosure is a souvenir from a dimension where someone, somewhere, was actually drafting a complaint, and the ghost of that complaint drifted through the mail merge and embedded itself in a UPS file and will live there forever, a permanent record of the moment the billing process became entirely untethered from the work it purported to describe.

The caravan moves on. The prebill is submitted. The electronic auditor scans. The foreclosure complaint sails through. Things take time to accomplished.

Shalom.
