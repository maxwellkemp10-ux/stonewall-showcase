## I. THE DASHBOARD

There is a moment in every financial forensic examination when the investigator stops scrolling through individual entries and steps back far enough to see the shape of the whole — the way a satellite photograph reveals the geometry of a city that no pedestrian ever perceives at street level. The UPS Financial Ledger Complete, assembled in its totality across five source files comprising 5,962 lines of merciless numerical specificity, is such a satellite photograph. And what it reveals, from this altitude, is not a city so much as a plantation. One man works the fields. Several others occupy the main house. The arithmetic is unambiguous.

The Combined Dashboard speaks first, and what it says is this: between July and December of 2025, across Client 001854 — UPS, United Parcel Service — Alvarez, Thompson & Smoak billed a total of $342,390.50. Of that sum, $288,806.00 has been collected. The write-offs total a negligible $253.00. The outstanding balance sits at $53,331.50, most of it December entries awaiting the ordinary course of CounselLink's automated payment processing. The collection rate stands at 84.3%, which sounds unremarkable until one considers that this figure includes dozens of December bills that simply have not yet cycled through Liberty Mutual's payment apparatus.

But the dashboard's true revelation is the Timekeeper Billing Summary, which reads like a class photograph in which one student is standing and everyone else is sitting:

Maxwell A. Kemp: $143,200 billed. Ninety-six bills. 84.5% collection rate.

Chance C. Arias: $84,693 billed. One hundred and twenty-five bills. 77.9% collection rate.

Yisenia Toledo: $63,041 billed. One hundred and thirty-three bills. 83.2% collection rate.

Katherine A. Sagel-Madigan: $35,784 billed. Forty-nine bills. 99.4% collection rate.

William G.K. Smoak: $11,633 billed. Ninety-two bills. 84.1% collection rate.

Pause here. Sit with these numbers as the historian must sit with casualty reports.

QB1 generates 41.8% of the total revenue across the UPS vertical with ninety-six billing entries. The Admiral generates 24.7% with a hundred and twenty-five entries — meaning he generates *less* revenue across *more* entries, which means his average bill is significantly smaller. This is the mathematical signature of a man who bills 0.20 hours for attending a quarterly review he did not prepare for and 0.20 hours for planning a review he will not attend to, then retreats to the chrysanthemum chamber until the next ceremonial appearance is required.

Big Satan generates $63,041 with one hundred and thirty-three billing entries — the most entries of any timekeeper on the account, and the third-highest revenue. But $63,041 across 133 entries yields an average of $474 per bill. QB1's $143,200 across 96 entries yields an average of $1,492 per bill. The ratio is approximately three-to-one. For every dollar Big Satan produces in a billing event, QB1 produces three. And the composition of those dollars — but we shall get to that. We shall very much get to that.

Katie Sagel-Madigan, meanwhile, logged $35,784 at a 99.4% collection rate across forty-nine bills before disappearing into maternity leave and the permanent exemption from accountability that institutional protection affords. One wonders what those entries contained. One does not need to wonder long — the historian recalls the phantom discectomies, the BLANK MedChron.docx, the "I don't feel like it" — but the collection rate is immaculate. 99.4%. CounselLink's automated system almost never flags her entries. Why would it? Each one is individually small, individually defensible, individually invisible. The quantum foam does not trigger the metal detector.

And Mr. Bill — William G.K. Smoak, named partner, Marine Monarch, the man who said "Fuck that — he is running UPS" — contributes $11,633 across ninety-two entries. Ninety-two entries at $11,633 is an average of $126.45 per entry. At his rate of $275 per hour, that is an average of 0.46 hours per entry — roughly twenty-seven minutes. But the historian suspects that the modal entry is not 0.46 hours but rather 0.10 or 0.20, with a handful of more substantial entries pulling the average upward. Mr. Bill's billing pattern is that of a man who reads the subject line of a filing, notes that it has been filed, and bills six to twelve minutes for the awareness that something has occurred in a case he has no operational involvement in. This is what institutional oversight looks like when the institution has surrendered oversight to the man on the field. You bill twelve minutes to confirm the field still exists.

## II. THE ADJUSTMENTS — OR, THE AUTOPSY OF EVERY DOLLAR THAT DIED

The Ledger History 2 — the adjustment table — is 178 entries long and spans from 2022 through January 2026. Total fee adjustments across four years: negative $24,796.80. Total expense adjustments: negative $200.78. These are Liberty Mutual's electronic auditing systems flagging and reducing entries that fail to pass automated review.

Twenty-four thousand, seven hundred and ninety-six dollars across four years of billing on an account that generates roughly $400,000 annually. That is a reduction rate of approximately 1.5% per year. The industry average for automated billing reductions on insurance defense files runs between 3% and 8%. QB1's vertical is being reduced at roughly one-third to one-fifth the industry rate.

And what is being reduced? The largest single adjustment is $2,797.50 on Matter 019538 from November 2024. The next largest is $2,166.80 on Matter 018960 from October 2024. The next is $1,880.00 on Matter 018897 from September 2022. These are pre-QB1-sovereignty reductions, mostly. The reductions from the current era — the CHAIRMANMAXXED epoch — are almost comically small. Twenty-two dollars here. Twenty-nine dollars and ninety cents there. Forty-six dollars. These are not substantive billing challenges. These are rounding errors that CounselLink's algorithm catches because a paralegal's six-minute entry didn't quite match the automated tolerance for that task code. The machine catches a $22 YT entry on the Adams matter. The machine catches a $29.90 YT entry on the Egan matter. The machine, in other words, catches Big Satan. Not often. Not with consequence. But the machine sees.

## III. THE JANUARY REVELATION — $81,333.50 AND THE QUESTION OF WHO EARNED IT

The January 2026 prebills encompass thirty-seven matters totaling $81,333.50 in professional services plus $2,989.12 in disbursements. Eighty-one thousand dollars in a single month. This is championship-level revenue, the kind of number that makes named partners take notice, the kind of number that causes Mr. Bill to say, from whatever Japanese ryokan he is currently occupying, the words that echo through the chronicle: "Fuck that — he is running UPS."

Let us examine what produced that number.

The Smalls case — Matter 020144, Samuel v. UPS — is the January leviathan: $10,430 in total charges, 63.10 hours. And within those 63.10 hours, a tale of two timesheets. MAK billed 29.40 hours at $200. KAS billed 4.40 hours at $110. YT billed 25.50 hours at a blended rate of $107.41 — the reduced rate indicating that some of her entries were already cut during prebill review. CCA billed 0.40 hours at $230. WGKS billed 3.40 hours at $275.

MAK's 29.40 hours on Smalls in January represent the Small Trilogy deposition campaign — the December 4th Samuel deposition, the preparation, the post-deposition analysis, the DataGavel, the settlement architecture. These are hours that correlate to a specific human being sitting in a room asking questions under oath, reviewing medical records by lamplight, drafting evaluative reports that reduce million-dollar demands to five-figure settlements. Big Satan's 25.50 hours on the same case represent — and the historian knows this with the certainty of a paleontologist who has catalogued a thousand specimens of the same species — the SDT preparation triplets, the Certificate of Authenticity ceremonies, the Statement of Assurance rituals, the "Received and categorized" notifications, and the internet research to identify proper service addresses that could be accomplished in a single batch but are instead atomized into individual billing events like a physicist splitting atoms to generate the maximum number of individually countable particles.

Across the January prebills as a whole: 117 instances of "Received and categorized." Sixty Certificates of Authenticity. Forty-six Statements of Assurance. Sixty-six Subpoena Duces Tecum preparations. These are Big Satan's four horsemen — the four template operations that constitute the core production line of the .10 farm. Each one billing six minutes. Each one individually defensible. Collectively constituting a billing fiction so pervasive that it has become the background radiation of the UPS account's financial universe — always present, always detectable if you know where to point the instrument, never quite rising to the threshold that triggers institutional intervention.

## IV. THE FEBRUARY SPECIMEN — "REGARDING UNDEFINED"

The February 2026 prebills total $67,730.17 across thirty-three matters. Fourteen thousand dollars less than January. The dip is unremarkable — case volumes fluctuate, deposition schedules shift, some matters settle and others ramp up. What is remarkable is the internal composition.

Seventy-three Certificates of Authenticity. Seventy-two Statements of Assurance. Seventy-two Subpoena Duces Tecum preparations. Twenty-eight "Received and categorized" entries. The assembly line has not merely persisted from January into February — it has *intensified*. The ratio of template operations to substantive work has shifted further toward template operations. The Henry Ford Assembly Line is now running a night shift.

The historian pauses. He counts. On the Clark case alone, across two days, Big Satan generated approximately fifty separate billing entries. Each one billed at 0.10 hours — six minutes. Each one $11.00. Approximately $550 in professional services for what is, in its entirety, the act of opening a template document, changing the provider name in the address field, saving the document, and repeating the process fifty times.

But it is not the volume that arrests the historian's attention. It is the entries that read: *"Prepare Subpoena Duces Tecum (re: undefined) to Gainesville Open MRI Center, LLC."* And: *"Prepare Statement of Assurance regarding undefined to Gainesville Open MRI Center, LLC."* And: *"Prepare Certificate of Authenticity regarding undefined to Central Florida Family Health Center, Inc."*

*Regarding undefined.*

She did not fill in the plaintiff's name. The template field that should read "Quenella Renee Clark" reads, instead, "undefined." The word that appears when a mail merge encounters a blank data field. The word that means: I did not populate the variable. I did not complete the operation for which I am billing six minutes. I ran the assembly line so fast that the product came off the conveyor belt missing its primary component — the identity of the human being on whose behalf the subpoena is theoretically being prepared — and I billed for it anyway.

And she billed it at $110 per hour. And it went on the prebill. And it will be reviewed by the Admiral, who does not read prebills so much as approve them in the way a man approves the sunrise — acknowledging its occurrence without inspecting its photons.

*Regarding undefined.* The historian could write a dissertation on those two words alone. They are the .10 farm's confession. They are the moment the assembly line's product label says "Contents: Nothing." They are the Henry Ford paradigm's logical terminus — the point at which the factory has so thoroughly disaggregated the act of producing something that it has begun billing for the production of nothing. Not the absence of a thing. The active production of nothing. Six minutes. Eleven dollars. Regarding undefined.

## V. THE CONTRAST — OR, THE TWO LANGUAGES OF WORK

Now let us read what QB1 was doing while Big Satan was preparing Certificates of Authenticity regarding undefined.

The Bowen case — Ron v. UPS, Matter 021000, February 2026. MAK billed 20.70 hours. The entries read like dispatches from a functioning litigation practice:

*Reconcile specials calculator against PIP summary; categorize provider-level billing; isolate $50,902 single-encounter ER charge representing 62% of gross specials; document PIP denial patterns.* That is 1.10 hours.

*Detailed review of medical chronology and source records: Orlando Health ER, Beach Medical (43 visits), Encore Imaging MRIs, Premier Orthopedic intake, ESI procedure notes, and final evaluation with MMI, VAS, PDQ, and impairment rating.* That is 1.90 hours.

*Review and analysis of three prior MVA crash reports (2008, 2023, 2024); isolate mirror-mechanism citation; cross-reference Willits intake against crash records and demand representations.* That is 1.40 hours.

*Draft DataGavel occurrence summary integrating FHP report with UPS documentation; draft injury treatment summary incorporating ER presentation, prior MVA history, mirror-mechanism analysis, MRI findings, ESI procedures, and clinical resolution through MMI.* That is 1.80 hours.

*DataGavel injury toggle analysis (13 claimed injuries): designate 9 disc herniations and headache as pre-existing aggravated based on prior MVA mechanisms, absence of comparison imaging, and aggravation doctrine; retain whiplash, thoracic sprain, and lower leg pain as new.* That is 1.30 hours.

The historian does not need to explain the difference between these entries and "Prepare Certificate of Authenticity regarding undefined." The difference explains itself. One set of entries describes a lawyer practicing law. The other describes a template being run through a mail merge that didn't finish executing. One set generates the analytical architecture that reduces million-dollar demands to five-figure settlements. The other generates fifty separate $11 invoices for documents missing the plaintiff's name.

But here is the obscenity that the financial ledger makes visible in a way that no individual prebill can: across the entire July-December 2025 period, QB1 billed $143,200 and was reduced $85.64. Big Satan billed $63,041 and was reduced $62.31. The reduction rates are nearly identical in percentage terms — both hovering at that sub-one-percent level that makes CounselLink's algorithms shrug and wave the entries through. But the *work* beneath those numbers is categorically different. QB1's $143,200 represents depositions taken, motions filed, DataGavels produced, cases settled. Big Satan's $63,041 represents approximately 573 entries at an average of $110 each, which at her 0.10-to-0.40-hour range represents somewhere between 573 and 2,300 individual billing events — the overwhelming majority of which are template operations, portal downloads, and document categorization tasks that a functioning paralegal performs in batch, not in individually billed atoms.

The machine cannot tell the difference between a $6,840 entry for a multi-week deposition campaign and sixty-two $110 entries for Certificates of Authenticity. Both add up. Both pass through the system. Both get paid. And both contribute to the dashboard's $342,390.50 total in a way that makes it appear, to anyone reading only the summary, as though this is a team effort.

It is not a team effort.

## VI. THE AUDIT — OR, 3,813 CLICKS IN THE DARK

And then there is the audit_domo, that neurological scan of the FileVine system, and its 3,813 rows of ytoledo activity. Three thousand eight hundred and thirteen actions across the UPS account, each timestamped to the second, each representing a mouse click, a document view, a tag update, a billing timer adjustment.

The historian has already noted, in the previous chapter, the three-second intervals between billing timer adjustments — the five modifications in fifteen seconds that constitute the sculpting of time entries rather than the recording of work performed. But the audit_domo reveals something else when cross-referenced against the prebill entries: the sheer volume of FileVine activity required to produce the .10 farm's output.

On a single project — 12724468, on a single afternoon — Big Satan generated the following sequence: view Document, save Document, update tags, update tags, save Document, view Document, save Document, update tags, update tags, save Document, update time entry for billing timer, update time entry for billing timer, update time entry for billing timer, update time entry for billing timer, update time entry for billing timer, save Document, view Document, update tags, update tags, update time entry for billing timer, update time entry for billing timer, update time entry for billing timer. Forty-some actions on a single case in a single sitting.

And what did that sitting produce? One 0.10-hour entry. Eleven dollars. A Certificate of Authenticity, or a categorized document, or a Statement of Assurance. The ratio of FileVine clicks to billable output is approximately forty-to-one. Forty mouse clicks to produce eleven dollars of professional services. The factory requires forty mechanical operations to stamp a single commemorative coin.

Meanwhile, Chance Arias's audit trail across the same period consists primarily of calendar invite updates and document previews. Kerry Coles is deep in expense request management. Joyce Loeblich makes her lone surveillance appearance at 5:12 PM. And QB1 — well, QB1 is not in the audit_domo at all, because QB1 is not generating his work product through FileVine template operations. He is generating it through legal analysis performed in his mind, drafted in Word documents, refined through AI-integrated workflows, and transmitted to clients via email. His work leaves traces in prebills, not in FileVine activity logs. The man who produces 41.8% of the revenue produces approximately 0% of the FileVine click volume.

## VII. THE SHAPE OF THE WHOLE

So here, assembled from five source files and nearly six thousand lines of financial data, is the shape of the thing:

A $400,000-per-year account in which one associate attorney generates 42% of the billings while a paralegal generates 18% of the billings through template operations so repetitive that she occasionally forgets to fill in the plaintiff's name. A partner who generates 25% of the billings through entries so small that his average bill is one-third the associate's. A named partner who contributes 3.4% while receiving standing ovations for presentations ghost-written by the associate. A former paralegal on maternity leave who contributed 10.5% through mechanisms that included phantom discectomies and the statement "I don't feel like it." An automated billing review system that flags approximately $25,000 across four years — less than 2% annually — because the individual entries are too small to trigger alarm thresholds even when they describe documents prepared "regarding undefined."

A collections ledger showing $2.29 million in cash receipts flowing into the firm's accounts, almost entirely generated by work products bearing the initials MAK. An audit trail showing 3,813 FileVine actions by a paralegal whose principal professional output is the repetitive execution of mail merge templates at six-minute intervals. And a quarterly claim review schedule that allocates five minutes per case to the only person in the building who has actually read the case files, while the people who are paid to read the case files update document tags at three-second intervals and bill for the experience of having been proximate to information.

The ledger of souls, hoss. Every number in its proper column. Every dollar accounted for. Every Certificate of Authenticity regarding undefined preserved in amber for all eternity.

The dogs bark. The caravan generates the revenue. The dogs bill for hearing the caravan. The Admiral approves it all without reading. Mr. Bill forwards and fades. And CounselLink's algorithm, that blind machine, sees nothing that rises to the threshold of concern — because the threshold was designed for a world in which paralegals do not bill six minutes for documents missing the plaintiff's name.

The machine was not built for this particular species of kabuki.

Nothing was.
