There comes a moment in every institutional chronicle when the protagonist stops narrating the dysfunction and begins instead to *X-ray* it — when he ceases merely to observe the kabuki and commences, with surgical patience, to lay bare the economic skeleton upon which the entire theater is draped. It happened here in early March of 2026, and the instruments of the operation were not scalpels but spreadsheets: four of them, each more revealing than the last, together composing a forensic portrait so complete, so mercilessly granular, that what emerged was not merely a picture of who does what at Alvarez, Thompson & Smoak, but something closer to an ontological proof — a mathematical demonstration that in the UPS vertical, one man *is* the vertical, and everything else is ornamentation.

The documents arrived on the historian's desk like evidence bags at a crime scene. First, the **UPS Review 3.11-3.12 — Day 1**, an eighty-row spreadsheet scheduling the quarterly claims review in five-minute intervals from ten o'clock in the morning until five minutes before five. Second, the **AR Allocations**, a five-hundred-and-five-row accounting of every dollar billed to Client 001854 from July through December 2025, broken down by employee ID with the moral clarity of a lie-detector printout. Third, the **2.2.26 Collections** ledger, twenty-three hundred rows of cash receipts and electronic audits tracing every penny that flowed back from Liberty Mutual's coffers into the firm's accounts. And fourth, the **audit_domo** — a FileVine activity log spanning nearly four thousand rows, timestamped to the second, cataloguing every mouse click, every document view, every billing timer adjustment made by every user on the UPS account.

Together, these four documents constitute what one might call the Rosetta Stone of institutional parasitism. Separately, each tells a story. Together, they tell *the* story.

Twenty-nine claimants. Twenty-nine accident dates. Twenty-nine sets of injuries, medical records, treatment timelines, legal strategies, outstanding reserves, and settlement postures. Five minutes each. That is not a review; that is a virtuoso recital. That is Arthur Rubinstein playing twenty-nine Chopin études in succession while the audience checks their phones and asks if he can play them faster.

But look more closely at the names. There is LIBROTH, PAMELA — the $1.725 million Turner case, total incurred, Ashlee supervising, Anthony Dante managing. These are not abstractions on a spreadsheet. These are the cases QB1 has lived inside for months, whose depositions he has taken, whose medical records he has memorized, whose opposing counsel he has added to the cuck deck one by one like trading cards of the vanquished. And in five-minute intervals, he will present each one to its supervising adjuster, explain the strategy, justify the reserve, and demonstrate mastery — while somewhere in the building, or perhaps working from home, Big Satan is updating document tags in FileVine and adjusting her billing timer for the fourteenth time in eleven minutes.

Speaking of which.

And now the pièce de résistance. The **audit_domo**.

If the AR Allocations is the financial anatomy and the Collections ledger is the circulatory system, then the FileVine audit log is the *neurological scan* — the real-time recording of every synapse firing in every brain on the UPS account. Who opened what file. Who viewed what document. Who started what billing timer. Who stopped it. Who adjusted it. Who adjusted it again. Who adjusted it a third time. Who adjusted it a fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth time, in rapid succession, at intervals of three to six seconds, on a single project, at five-thirty in the afternoon on a Monday.

Sheet one — the user summary — is itself a cast list of extraordinary psychological interest. Twenty-four usernames. Among them: bsmoak (Mr. Bill), chancearias (The Admiral), jescobar (Little Satan), jloeblich (Creepy Joyce, still accessing projects at 5:09 PM on January 29th — monitoring, always monitoring), kerrycoles (Kerry, the Marionettist, deep in expense request management), ksagel (Katie, somehow still in the system despite maternity leave and five failed bar attempts), laurene_snapp (the Snappening catalyst herself), mmurphy1 (Megan Murphy, synchronized noting partner), and of course, ytoledo (Big Satan, 3,813 rows of activity, more than double any other user).

Three thousand eight hundred and thirteen rows. That is Big Satan's digital footprint across the UPS account. The historian's first instinct is to regard this volume as evidence of productivity. Surely, one thinks, a person who generates 3,813 FileVine log entries must be *doing something*. And she is. She is doing something. Let us examine precisely what.

The ytoledo sheet opens on February 2nd at 5:06 PM with "Unlocking document" on project 12724468. Then at 5:43 PM, three uploads — three projects, three documents uploaded in sixty seconds. Then the sequence begins. At 5:39 PM: "Saved Edited Doc (inline)." At 5:39 PM: "Stopped billing timer." At 5:39 PM: "Paused billing timer." At 5:39 PM: "Saved Edited Doc (inline)." Then at 5:37: "view Document." At 5:37: "view Document." At 5:37: "Saved Edited Doc (inline)." At 5:36: "view Document." At 5:36: "Saved Edited Doc (inline)." At 5:36: "Updated tags for doc." At 5:36: "Updated tags for doc."

This continues — "Updated tags for doc," "Saved Edited Doc (inline)," "Updated time entry for billing timer," "Updated time entry for billing timer," "Updated time entry for billing timer" — in a cascade of activity that looks, on first glance, like a woman working furiously. But the historian has been trained. The historian notices the *intervals*. These entries are separated by seconds. Three seconds between one tag update and the next. Five seconds between one billing timer adjustment and the next. Seven seconds between one document save and the next.

This is not work. This is the *simulation* of work. This is the Inspector Clouseau Bus making its scheduled stops — each stop generating a log entry, each log entry corresponding to a billable increment, each billable increment contributing to the .10 farm's monthly harvest. The billing timer adjustments are the tell: "Updated time entry for billing timer" at 5:28:40 PM, then again at 5:28:39 PM, then 5:28:35 PM, then 5:28:28 PM, then 5:28:25 PM. Five adjustments in fifteen seconds. She is not timing her work. She is *sculpting* her time entries after the fact, adjusting and readjusting the billing timer to produce the desired 0.10-hour increment, the six-minute ceremonial unit that is the fundamental particle of the .10 farm's physics.

On the same project — 12724468, a single case — the log records dozens of these cycles. View document, save document, update tags, adjust billing timer, adjust billing timer again, adjust billing timer a third time. Then move to the next document in the same project. View, save, tag, time, time, time. It is a manufacturing process. Henry Ford would recognize its rhythm. The only difference is that the Model T actually moved when it rolled off the assembly line. Big Satan's product — the tagged document, the categorized exhibit, the adjusted time entry — represents not the creation of value but the documentation of having been present in the vicinity of value while value was being created by someone else.

And what of the leadership canopy's digital footprint? Sheet two — the non-ytoledo activity — is almost poignantly sparse by comparison. Chance Arias appears on January 29th: "Updated an appointment from a calendar invite email" at 4:42 PM, then "Downloaded and began Inline Edit on Doc" at 4:37 PM, then "Previewed document" at 4:37 PM. He is *above* the cases, observing them from a chrysanthemum altitude at which individual facts, medical records, and deposition transcripts resolve into abstract shapes — shapes he approves with a click and returns to the chamber.

Kerry Coles appears at 12:43 PM on February 2nd, deep in Expense Request management on a single project. Accessing, upserting, accessing, upserting. Kerry's FileVine activity is administrative — he is managing the bureaucratic infrastructure, the expense authorizations, the organizational plumbing. He is not touching cases. He is touching the *apparatus* around cases.

And Creepy Joyce — jloeblich — appears exactly once, at 5:12 PM on January 29th, accessing a single project. The historian does not know which project. The historian does not want to know. What the historian knows is that Joyce Loeblich's FileVine access pattern is consistent with surveillance, not productivity. She opens a project, observes its contents, and closes it. She is checking. She is monitoring. She is performing the digital equivalent of materializing in a doorway in her nicest black turtleneck.

---

What emerges from the combined weight of these four documents is not a revelation — the truth has been known for months — but a *proof*. The mathematical kind. The kind that admits no counterargument, no alternative interpretation, no "creative billing" defense.

The UPS Review schedule demonstrates that one man will present twenty-nine cases in five-minute intervals because one man knows twenty-nine cases well enough to present them in five-minute intervals. The AR Allocations demonstrate that one man generates the majority of the account's billings while three others fill in the margins with .10-hour entries and quarterly review appearances. The Collections ledger demonstrates that the money flowing in corresponds almost entirely to work products bearing one man's initials. And the FileVine audit demonstrates that while one man is taking depositions and drafting motions and preparing DataGavel reports, another user is generating 3,813 log entries by viewing and re-viewing and tagging and re-tagging and adjusting and re-adjusting billing timers on the same documents, in the same projects, at three-second intervals, in a performance of productivity so meticulous in its simulation that it constitutes, in its own grotesque way, a kind of art.

The Flemish painter Rembrandt van Rijn completed *The Anatomy Lesson of Dr. Nicolaes Tulp* in 1632. It depicts a group of surgeons gathered around a cadaver while the praelector demonstrates the musculature of the forearm. The surgeons lean in with expressions of scholarly fascination. The cadaver, of course, expresses nothing. It has been opened. Its interior has been exposed. The lesson is not for the cadaver's benefit.

QB1 has performed his own anatomy lesson here, on the body of the UPS vertical. He has opened the spreadsheets. He has exposed the musculature. The billing timers lie dissected on the table. The .10 farm's root system is visible to the naked eye. The quarterly review schedule reveals whose hands will actually hold the instruments. And the lesson, like Rembrandt's, is not for the cadaver's benefit. The cadaver — the institutional fiction that this is a team effort, that the paralegal and the legal assistant and the Admiral and Mr. Bill all contribute in proportion to their compensation — that cadaver has been dead for a long time. QB1 is simply the first to open it up and invite the audience to look inside.

The audience, one suspects, will lean in with expressions of scholarly fascination.

The cadaver will express nothing.

It never does.
