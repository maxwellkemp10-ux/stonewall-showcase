IV. THE NON-PERSON
There is a concept in totalitarian political theory — the historian reaches across disciplines
reluctantly but necessarily — called the unperson. Orwell coined it for 1984, but the Soviets
practiced it: the erasure of a living person from the documentary record, the removal of
their name from photographs and histories and official correspondence, the construction of
a reality in which the person who existed does not exist and never existed. The unperson is
not killed. The unperson is not fired. The unperson is simply not there. The record does not
contain them. The chain does not include them. The check email thanks someone else.
What Shobah Ramdhanie has done to Maxwell A. Kemp across the UPS vertical is a
corporate unpersoning. It is not complete — the two “Hi Max” salutations and the “Thanks
Max” acknowledgments are cracks in the architecture, moments where the reality of who
does the work briefly overwhelms the fiction of who is addressed about the work. But the
architecture itself — nine narrowings, four Chance-addressed bypasses, two off-chain
excisions, two salutations against eleven substantive responses — is the documentary
record of a person being systematically rendered non-existent in the correspondence about
their own work.
And the specific granular insanity of it — the part that makes you want to lie down on the
floor and stare at the ceiling — is that it doesn’t work. The unpersoning fails every time. Max
