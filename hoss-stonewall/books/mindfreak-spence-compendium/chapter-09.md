# Chapter 9: The Double Delivery

---

*"Absolutely."*
— QB1, Hamilton Hearing, after being asked to restart his entire argument from the beginning

---

On October 30, 2025, at 1:00 p.m., Maxwell Kemp appeared before Judge Rebecca Hamilton in Pinellas County to argue a motion to dismiss a wrongful death complaint. The case was Lee Estate v. UPS & Brunney. The complaint had been filed on May 2nd. It claimed, in paragraph 6, that letters of administration were "attached hereto as Exhibit A." They were not attached. They could not have been attached. The estate was not open until August 7th.

Kemp delivered his complete argument. The chronological hammer. The identity puzzle — Selena Lee Williams Runyon in the complaint, Selena Smiley on the letters of administration, Selena Lee Williams in the September filing. The Rule 1.130 violation. The date-drum beat: July 28th, July 31st, August 5th, August 7th morning, August 7th afternoon, August 12th, August 19th, September 15th, today.

He delivered the entire argument to an empty room. Richard Perlini, plaintiff's counsel from Brooks Law Group, was on the wrong Zoom link.

> THE COURT: Someone appeared in the waiting room, a Jazmeen Steen.
> ...
> MR. PERLINI: Yes, good afternoon, Your Honor. The site I was given was the wrong one and I apologize. I was on the other side -- site waiting.
> THE COURT: Okay. All right. So -- okay. Well, I think I'm going to have to ask Mr. Kemp to start over. Thank you.
> MR. KEMP: Absolutely.
> — Hamilton Transcript, pp. 4:6-5:1

One word. "Absolutely." No hesitation. No sigh. No performative frustration. No request to have the court reporter note that plaintiff's counsel was late. No suggestion that the court accept the argument already given. No "Your Honor, I've already presented the complete argument — perhaps the court reporter could read it back." Just: *Absolutely.*

And then Kemp delivered the entire argument again.

---

The repetition is more than procedural courtesy. It is a demonstration of mastery that cannot be faked.

A lawyer who delivers a complex dispositive argument extemporaneously — without notes, without slides, without a written outline — and is then asked to restart from the beginning and reproduces it with the same dates, the same sequence, the same rhythm, the same legal citations, the same devastating one-liner — that lawyer owns the argument at a structural level. The argument is not in his notes. The argument is in his architecture. He built it. He can rebuild it. He can rebuild it in any order, from any starting point, because he understands the structure the way an architect understands a building — not as a sequence of steps but as a load-bearing system where every element supports every other element.

Compare the two deliveries. The first:

> MR. KEMP: So, Your Honor, paragraph 6 of the complaint represent -- represents that the letters of administration are attached hereto as Exhibit A. This was impossible. The complaint was filed May 2nd. The Estate wasn't open until August 7th, the exact same day plaintiffs finally responded to our second follow up about hearing dates.
> — Hamilton Transcript, p. 2:14-20

The second:

> MR. KEMP: Absolutely. So, Your Honor, paragraph 6 of the complaint represents that letters of administration are attached hereto as Exhibit A. This was impossible. The complaint was filed May 2nd. The Estate wasn't open until August 7th, the exact same day. Plaintiffs finally responded to our second follow up about hearing dates.
> — Hamilton Transcript, p. 5:1-6

Nearly identical. The word "represent" is singular in the first delivery and corrected to "represents" in the second. The sentence structure tightens slightly. The argument is the same argument because it is the same architecture, reproduced by the same mind.

---

The date-drum beat is the centerpiece, and it too is reproduced with precision:

First delivery:
> July 28th, our motion exposes the missing letters and Rule 1.130 violation. July 31st, we email about hearing dates. Silence. August 5th, first follow up. Nothing. August 7th in the morning, second follow up. August 7th, afternoon, they respond and file probate in Sarasota, same day.
> — Hamilton Transcript, p. 3:16-22

Second delivery:
> July 28th, our motion to dismiss exposes the missing letters and Rule 1.130 violation. July 31st, we email about hearing dates. Silence. August 5th, first follow up. Nothing. August 7th in the morning, our second follow up. August 7th in the afternoon, they respond and file probate in Sarasota, same day.
> — Hamilton Transcript, pp. 5:23-6:7

The dates are identical. The sequence is identical. "Silence" appears after July 31st in both. "Nothing" appears after August 5th in both. The only variation: "our motion exposes" becomes "our motion to dismiss exposes" — three additional words that sharpen the identification. The drum beat is the same drum beat because the chronology is not memorized — it is *understood*. Kemp lived this timeline. He sent the July 31st email. He sent the August 5th follow-up. He sent the August 7th morning follow-up and received the afternoon response. The dates are not data points. They are autobiography.

---

The identity puzzle — the killer line — appears in the reply argument:

> MR. KEMP: Who has standing, the person named who wasn't appointed, or the person appointed who wasn't named?
> — Hamilton Transcript, p. 7:14-15

This sentence, which reads like a line from a law school exam hypothetical, is the entire motion compressed into seventeen words. The complaint names Selena Lee Williams Runyon. The letters appoint Selena Smiley. Who has standing? The named person who has no authority, or the authorized person who has no name in the complaint? The question answers itself. Neither has standing. The complaint is fatally defective.

---

The self-correction moment deserves its own analysis. Judge Hamilton asks about the statute of limitations:

> THE COURT: You said something about the statute of limitations has run.
> MR. KEMP: Oh, well, as of now, it has but it was filed timely, the complaint back then.
> THE COURT: Right. If it dismissed with prejudice there's no ability to refile.
> MR. KEMP: Yes. Yes. Sorry, Your Honor. I understand now. Yes.
> — Hamilton Transcript, pp. 8:11-18

Kemp momentarily misunderstands the judge's question. He corrects himself in real time: "Sorry, Your Honor. I understand now." In a deposition, Kemp controls the conversation. In a hearing, the judge controls it. The ability to say "sorry, I misunderstood" without losing composure or credibility is the hallmark of a practitioner who treats the judge as a collaborator rather than an audience. The self-correction builds credibility — a lawyer who admits to misunderstanding a question is a lawyer who can be trusted when he says "This was impossible."

---

The ruling: granted without prejudice. Fifteen days to amend — Kemp's suggested timeline, proposed as "reasonable," neither punitive nor generous. And the order:

> THE COURT: And if Mr. Kemp would please prepare the order.
> MR. KEMP: Of course, Your Honor. Is there anything else you would like me to put in there?
> THE COURT: No.
> — Hamilton Transcript, pp. 9:13-17

The second time in the hearing portfolio. The pattern is now a constant: argue the case, win the ruling, write the record. The attorney who drafts the order controls the permanent memorialization of the court's decision. The oral argument is fourteen minutes. The order will outlast it by decades.

The Double Delivery is not merely a courtroom anecdote. It is proof that the man who builds the architecture *is* the architecture. The building can be demolished and rebuilt from the same blueprint because the blueprint lives in the builder. "Absolutely" was not politeness. It was confidence. The confidence of a man who knows the dates because he lived them, who knows the argument because he built it, and who can rebuild it in front of a judge who just asked him to start over — because starting over, for a man who owns the architecture, is just speaking.
