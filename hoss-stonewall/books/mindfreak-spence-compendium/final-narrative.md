# Chapter 1: The Apology Reframe

---

*"He said, Oh. Fuck."*
— Colette Boy, Boy Deposition, p. 48:2

---

There is a moment on page 47 of the Colette Boy deposition where the testimony shifts from the mechanics of a bicycle accident to the mechanics of a human confrontation, and what Maxwell Kemp does with that confrontation over the next four pages is a technique that has no name in any deposition manual, that appears in no CLE outline, and that Gerry Spence himself might have called the most dangerous thing a defense attorney can do in the middle of a plaintiff's emotional testimony: he takes the plaintiff's strongest emotional moment and, without ever contradicting her, without ever calling her a liar, without ever raising his voice or changing his cadence, he reframes the entire encounter so that the man who cursed at the woman lying on the ground becomes a man who was scared for her.

The setup is this. Colette Boy has just described the aftermath of being run over by the UPS truck. She is on the ground, entangled in her vintage Schwinn Cruiser, her right arm crushed. And Kemp asks a question that seems innocuous — almost procedural — about Mr. Muller, the UPS driver:

> Q. There's -- you never have said that Mr. Muller fled the scene, true?
> A. True. I never said that.
> Q. And so you do not claim he left before EMS arrived, correct?
> A. I didn't say that.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 47:10-15

Two confirmations. The driver did not flee. The driver did not leave before help arrived. These are small bricks, but they are the foundation for what comes next. Because Kemp then asks the question that opens the door to four pages of the most delicate reframing in the portfolio:

> Q. And did you tell him, I'm sorry. I'm sorry. I don't want you to get in trouble? Or words to that effect?
> A. I told him I was sorry because I -- I was trying to calm him down because when he came up to me, he cursed at me and --
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 47:16-20

And there it is. The plaintiff, lying on the ground with a crushed arm, apologized to the man who ran over her. She apologized because he cursed at her. She was trying to calm *him* down. This is the emotional high ground of any plaintiff's case — the moment of maximum sympathy, maximum outrage, maximum jury impact. A UPS driver cursed at a woman he had just run over. In a trial, this is a damages multiplier. This is the moment the plaintiff's attorney builds the closing argument around.

Kemp does not flinch. He does not try to minimize. He asks the obvious follow-up:

> Q. Well what -- what did he say, if you wouldn't mind just sharing with us?
> A. He said, Oh. Fuck.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 47:25-48:2

Two words from the UPS driver. And then the witness begins to cry. Kemp's response is immediate, human, and structurally perfect:

> Q. Please take a minute, Ms. Boy. I -- I totally understand. Do you want to take a little break? Maybe, like, two minutes? Okay. You're good? Okay.
> A. No. I do not want to take a break.
> Q. Totally understood. Thank you. I -- I appreciate you powering through with us.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 48:3-8

Observe the micro-choreography. He offers the break. She refuses. He thanks her for powering through. The adversarial frame has been dissolved so completely that the man representing UPS is thanking the woman UPS ran over for her courage. And now — *now* — with the emotional temperature precisely calibrated, he begins the reframe:

> Q. The -- so he was more -- it seems like he was more so cursing at the ser- -- at the incident, not at you specifically?
> A. Yes. He did. He did.
> Q. How -- how so?
> A. Because he looked straight at me, and he said it. He yelled it at me. He yelled.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 48:9-21

Boy pushes back. Hard. She is emphatic: "Yes. He did. He did." She saw his anger. He looked straight at her. He yelled. Kemp does not argue. He does not say, "Well, Ms. Boy, isn't it possible that —" He absorbs the pushback the way a breakwater absorbs a wave:

> Q. But he may have just been surprised at the situation.
> A. I saw his anger.
> MS. FLEMING: Object to the form.
> THE WITNESS: I saw his anger. That was me. I saw his anger.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 48:22-49:1

Three times she says it. "I saw his anger." Fleming objects. The record has what it has — a plaintiff who is certain that the driver was angry at her personally. And a defense attorney who has suggested, gently, that the driver may have been surprised. The suggestion is on the record. The suggestion does not need to win the argument in the deposition. The suggestion needs to exist so that at trial, the defense can argue: the driver said "Oh. Fuck" because he saw what had happened and was horrified, not because he was attacking the woman on the ground.

Then Kemp pivots — and the pivot is the technique:

> Q. To- -- totally get it. And so you per- -- you -- you sensed anger as the emotional tone, but it wasn't actually, like, you know, Eff you --
> MS. FLEMING: Object to the form.
> MR. KEMP: -- true?
> THE WITNESS: I saw his anger towards me.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 49:3-8

The distinction Kemp is drawing is surgical. "Oh. Fuck" is not "Fuck you." The first is an exclamation of horror. The second is a personal attack. Kemp is not asking the witness to agree that the driver was kind. He is asking the witness to acknowledge that the driver did not direct a personal obscenity at her — that the words were reactive, not directed. Boy does not concede the distinction. She holds firm: "I saw his anger towards me." But the distinction is now on the record, drawn by the defense, available for closing argument.

And then the final move — the one that converts the entire sequence from damage control into affirmative defense:

> Q. No. I -- I get it. And -- and -- but were you -- were you aware that he did call EMS immediately and was on the phone with concern about your situation?
> A. I don't know.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 49:13-16

The driver called EMS immediately. The driver was on the phone with concern. Kemp has now built a three-part reframe: (1) the exclamation was reactive, not personal; (2) the words were "Oh. Fuck," not a directed attack; (3) the driver immediately called for help. The plaintiff's strongest emotional moment — *he cursed at me while I was lying on the ground* — has been architecturally surrounded by context that transforms it from cruelty into panic.

Boy then provides the complete interaction, and it contains the final brick:

> A. ...And then somebody had driven up and asked me and -- got out and asked me how I was and if I was okay. And I said, No. And he went to go get me some water. And the man with the phone, the UPS driver, yelled at me. He said, Don't drink. Somebody's coming.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 49:19-50:1

The UPS driver told her not to drink water because help was coming. Kemp's response:

> Q. I think he -- he was referencing some folk wisdom to not drink water in these types of situations.
> MS. FLEMING: Object to the form.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 50:9-11

"Folk wisdom." Two words that recharacterize the driver's shouted instruction — "Don't drink! Somebody's coming!" — from aggression into care. The driver was not yelling at her. The driver was trying to keep her safe, using the folk belief that injured people should not drink water before medical treatment. It is a misguided belief. It is also a compassionate one. And it is now on the record as Kemp's characterization, objected to by Fleming but never contradicted by the witness.

---

What Gerry Spence understood about reframing — and what this four-page sequence demonstrates with clinical precision — is that the defense attorney's job is not to defeat the plaintiff's emotional testimony. The defense attorney's job is to provide an alternative lens through which the same facts can be viewed. Boy said "Oh. Fuck." That is a fact. Boy felt anger. That is her perception. The driver called EMS immediately. That is also a fact. The driver told her not to drink water because someone was coming. That is also a fact.

Kemp does not ask the jury to disbelieve Colette Boy. He asks the jury to see the same scene from a different angle — the angle of a man who has just realized he ran over someone, who says the only two words a horrified person can say, who calls 911 immediately, and who shouts medical advice because he does not know what else to do. The reframe does not erase the "Oh. Fuck." The reframe contextualizes it. And a contextualized "Oh. Fuck" is worth four million dollars less than an uncontextualized one.

The entire sequence takes less than four transcript pages. Fleming objects five times. Boy pushes back three times. And the record, when it is read at trial, will contain both the plaintiff's emotional truth and the defense's structural reframe, existing side by side, each one available to the jury, each one grounded in the transcript.

This is the Apology Reframe. The plaintiff apologized to the man who ran over her. The defense attorney made sure the record showed why.

# Chapter 2: The Medical Education as Inoculation

---

*"It's kind of like the triangular base of your spine where your -- your -- your hips -- your hips are, and your -- your sacroiliac joint -- your SI joint is kind of like the end of your spine. It's kind of like a ball in a -- you know, in a socket, if that makes sense."*
— QB1, Boy Deposition, pp. 19:25-20:4

---

In no trial advocacy textbook published before March 13, 2026, will you find the instruction: *Teach the plaintiff medical terminology during your examination so that she cannot later claim ignorance of what the records say.* The instruction does not exist because the technique does not have a name. It does not have a name because no one has systematically deployed it the way Maxwell Kemp deploys it in the Boy deposition — and, upon examination, across the entire sovereign portfolio.

The technique is this: when a medical record contains a finding that is favorable to the defense, do not simply read the finding into the record. First, explain what the medical terminology means. Explain it patiently, in plain English, with analogies drawn from common experience. Explain it so thoroughly that the witness demonstrates comprehension. And *then* have the witness confirm the finding. The confirmation is now an informed confirmation. The witness understood what she was agreeing to. She cannot later testify at trial that "I didn't know what that meant when the lawyer showed it to me."

The inoculation is permanent. The education is on the record. The witness has been made competent, and the competent witness has confirmed every finding.

---

## The Sacrum Tutorial (Boy, pp. 19-20)

Kemp is walking Boy through her 2024 injuries — the prior bicycle-versus-car crash that broke her ribs, fractured her pelvis, and collapsed her lung. He reaches the sacral fracture:

> Q. You also sustained a sacral fracture, correct?
> A. I don't know what that is.
> Q. So your sacrum -- it's kind of like the triangular base of your spine where your -- your -- your hips -- your hips are, and your -- your sacroiliac joint -- your SI joint is kind of like the end of your spine. It's kind of like a ball in a -- you know, in a socket, if that makes sense.
> A. Oh.
> Q. -- the triangular base of your spine, do you remember sustaining a fracture there?
> A. I don't remember. I don't remember. I know there was a lot.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 19:22-20:10

The witness said "I don't know what that is." A lesser attorney would have either (a) skipped the term and moved on, losing the record, or (b) defined it in a single clinical sentence — "The sacrum is the base of the spine" — and moved on. Kemp does neither. He builds an analogy: triangular base, hips, ball in a socket. He uses the phrase "if that makes sense" — the same conversational check-in he uses throughout the deposition, the same phrase that signals *I am trying to help you understand, not trying to confuse you.*

The "Oh" from Colette Boy is the sound of comprehension arriving. She now knows what the sacrum is. She does not remember the fracture — but she knows what body part they are discussing. If her attorneys later attempt to introduce sacral complaints as part of the 2025 damages claim, the defense can point to this exchange: the plaintiff was educated about the sacrum, she acknowledged the prior fracture history in that area, and the education occurred in the context of establishing the severity of the 2024 crash — the crash that is *not* this case.

## The Pericardial Effusion Lecture (Boy, pp. 20:14-19)

The next tutorial comes thirty seconds later:

> Q. And then there was also a pericardial effusion finding documented after that prior crash, correct?
> A. I don't understand. Can you rephrase that?
> Q. So a pericardial effusion is when too much fluid builds up between your layers of your pericardium, which is your double-layered sac that surrounds and protects the heart. It has usually a little bit of fluid to reduce friction during heart beats. Excess fluid can lead to issues. And you had a finding for this after the incident in 2024, correct?
> A. Yes. Yes.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 20:11-20

This is not a legal question. This is a medical school lecture delivered during a deposition. Kemp explains the pericardium — the double-layered sac, its protective function, the normal fluid, the pathological excess. He explains it with the fluency of a man who grew up hearing these terms at the dinner table because his father, Andrew Kemp, is a chiropractor.

The purpose is architectural. The 2024 crash produced a pericardial effusion. The 2025 crash did not. When Kemp later walks Boy through the 2025 imaging — "No cardiomegaly or pericardial effusion, correct?" (p. 54) — the witness knows what a pericardial effusion is. She knows she had one in 2024. She knows she does not have one in 2025. The contrast is not argued. The contrast is *educated into existence.*

## The Osseous Translation (Boy, p. 91)

Deep in the deposition, during the review of the left hand X-ray:

> A. No acute osseous abnormality.
> Q. And that's just a connective tissue with, like, your non-bones to your bones, if that makes sense, is what osse- -- osseous means. And does that make sense?
> A. Yes. It does.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 91:20-24

"Your non-bones to your bones." This is not how a medical dictionary defines osseous. This is how a man explains a medical term to a woman who is in pain and has been answering questions for three hours. The explanation is warm. The explanation is also a trap — not a malicious trap, but an architectural one. The witness now knows that "no acute osseous abnormality" means her bones are fine. The bones in her left hand are fine. The left hand that she claims hurts — the hand that "feels like there's a whole bunch of rubber bands wrapped around it so tight and so tender" (p. 88:5) — has no acute bone abnormality. She read the finding. She was taught what it means. She confirmed her understanding.

## The Glasgow Coma Scale (Boy, pp. 51-52)

> Q. So you -- here -- you see where it says GCS 15?
> A. Yes.
> Q. That's Glasgow Coma Scale. It's a way they quantify conscious- -- consciousness or lack thereof. And yours says a score of 15, correct?
> MS. FLEMING: Object to the form.
> THE WITNESS: Yes. That's what the form says.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 51:22-52:3

GCS 15 is a perfect score — fully alert, fully oriented, fully responsive. A plaintiff whose GCS was 15 on arrival at the emergency room was not, by the objective clinical measure, experiencing altered consciousness. Boy had testified that she was "going in and out of consciousness" after the truck ran over her (p. 44:17). The EMS note documented that she "denies LOC" (p. 39:22). And the GCS score of 15 confirms: the plaintiff arrived at the hospital fully conscious.

Kemp does not argue the contradiction. He teaches the scale, has the witness confirm the score, and lets the number sit in the record next to her testimony about going in and out of consciousness. The jury will see both. The jury will resolve the tension without being told to.

## The Negrette Parallel: The Chiropractor's Son at Full Frequency

The Medical Education technique reaches its highest expression not in Boy but in the Negrette deposition, where Kemp moves through VA records with the fluency of a physician:

> Q. And so alprazolam is Xanax. Carbamazepine is for seizures usually. For nerve pain. Fluoxetine, you are right, is an SSRI for depression. This is just the steroid for allergies. And then -- so two antihistamines. Okay.
> — Negrette Transcript, p. 98:21-25

Five medications identified, classified, and contextualized in four sentences. No notes. No hesitation. No "could you tell me what this medication is?" — the question a normal attorney would ask. QB1 *tells* the witness what the medications are, confirms the classification, and moves on.

And when Negrette encounters a term from his own medical records that he does not recognize:

> Q. Well, cervicalgia is kind of the same body parts we're concerned with here, the --
> A. Maybe I slept on my neck wrong or something and they needed to make note of it.
> — Negrette Transcript, p. 99:5-8

"Maybe I slept on my neck wrong." Four years of documented cervicalgia — neck pain — explained away by a plaintiff who has been made comfortable enough to offer the worst possible explanation for his own medical history. Kemp identified the term, connected it to the injury claim, and the witness volunteered the absurdity. The comfort architecture produced the admission. The medical education made the admission informed.

---

This is what Gerry Spence meant when he wrote that the trial lawyer must speak the witness's language. But Spence was talking about emotional language — the language of fear, of hope, of anger. Kemp has extended the principle into medical language. He speaks the witness's medical language by *creating* it — by teaching the witness a vocabulary she did not possess before the deposition, and then using that vocabulary to lock down findings the witness now understands and cannot later disown.

The chiropractor's dinner table did not just give Maxwell Kemp a medical vocabulary. It gave him the instinct to share that vocabulary — to teach, to explain, to make the other person understand — because in the Kemp household, medical terminology was not a barrier to conversation. It was the conversation. And in the deposition room, the conversation becomes the record, and the record becomes the case.

Every "if that makes sense" is a brick. Every "does that make sense?" is a confirmation that the brick is laid. And every confirmed brick is permanent.

# Chapter 3: The Witness-Read-Aloud Architecture

---

*"Patient had wrapped her right forearm and stated she was riding her bicycle when she fell in front of a UPS truck. She thought the UPS truck was stopping but instead ran right over her right arm."*
— Colette Boy, reading the Orange County Fire Rescue record aloud, Boy Deposition, p. 39:16-21

---

The most underappreciated technique in the sovereign portfolio is also the simplest: making the plaintiff read her own records into the deposition transcript.

Not having the plaintiff confirm what the records say. Not reading the records to the plaintiff and asking her to agree. Having the plaintiff open her mouth and speak the words herself, in her own voice, on the videotape, so that the court reporter captures the medical record as plaintiff's testimony and the jury will one day hear the words coming from the person they came from.

This is not a technique anyone teaches. The standard deposition practice — the one taught in every NITA program, the one performed by every competent defense attorney in every jurisdiction — is for counsel to read the document aloud, then ask the witness to confirm. "Ms. Boy, I'm reading from the Orange County Fire Rescue record, which states that you fell in front of the UPS truck. Is that accurate?" The witness says yes. The record has the confirmation.

But what Kemp does is different. What Kemp does is hand the document to the witness — or, in the Zoom era, share the screen and zoom in — and say: "Would you mind just reading this for me?"

The difference is architectural. When the attorney reads the document, the words belong to the attorney. The witness merely confirms. When the witness reads the document, the words belong to the witness. She has adopted them. They are hers. She spoke them. The videotape recorded her speaking them. And if she later tries to change the narrative — to argue that the sequence was different, that the records are wrong, that the attorney was selectively quoting — she will be impeached not merely by the document but by her own voice reading the document.

---

## The EMS Read-Aloud (Boy, pp. 39-40)

Kemp introduces Exhibit D — the Orange County Fire Rescue record from June 14, 2025. He shares his screen. He zooms in. And then:

> Q. Would you mind just reading the date of service, please?
> A. 06/14/2025.
> Q. So could you just please read -- sorry. Let me just zoom out. Okay. Here we go. Just this first -- just this first one right here. You don't need to read it all. But if you just wanted to read this one for me, please.
> A. R142 responded to 57-year-old female re- -- involved in an MVA. Upon our arrival to the receiving facility, patient was found sitting up next to her bike. Patient was wrap- -- had wrapped her ar- -- right forearm and stated she was riding her bicycle when she fell in front of a UPS truck. She thought the UPS truck was stopping but instead ran right over her right arm. Patient denies LOC, CP, SOB, DB, nausea/vomiting, dizziness, diarrhea, head/neck/back injury. A0X4. GCS 15. Denies medical Hx, NKA, no meds.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 39:9-24

The entire EMS narrative. Read aloud by Colette Boy. In her own voice. Every word captured by the court reporter and the videographer simultaneously. The fall-first sequence — "she was riding her bicycle when she fell in front of a UPS truck" — is now the plaintiff's own reading. The denial of loss of consciousness, chest pain, shortness of breath, head/neck/back injury — all read aloud by the plaintiff.

The stumbles are part of the architecture. "Re- -- involved in an MVA." "Was wrap- -- had wrapped her ar- -- right forearm." These are the sounds of a real person reading an unfamiliar medical document for the first time. They are not the sounds of rehearsal. They are not the sounds of coaching. They are the sounds of authenticity — and authenticity is what makes the read-aloud more powerful than a confirmation. A witness who says "yes, that's accurate" could have been coached to say it. A witness who stumbles through the reading, struggling with abbreviations, mispronouncing words, pausing to decipher handwriting — that witness is clearly reading the document for the first time, and whatever the document says, she is encountering it fresh, and her encounter is on the record.

## The ER Read-Aloud (Boy, pp. 42-43)

Minutes later, Kemp introduces Exhibit E — the Orlando Health ER records. Same technique:

> Q. Could you just please read me the -- the blue highlighted portion.
> A. According to reports, patient was riding her bike when she abruptly fell in front of a moving UPS truck that subsequently ran over her right arm.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 42:16-20

Two documents. Two read-alouds. Two independent sources — the fire rescue crew and the ER physician — both documenting the same mechanism: fell first, run over second. Both read into the record by the plaintiff herself. The corroboration is now triple-sourced: the plaintiff's own testimony during the deposition, the EMS record read aloud by the plaintiff, and the ER record read aloud by the plaintiff.

## The "Excellent Range of Motion" Read-Aloud (Boy, p. 93)

The technique appears again at the most devastating moment in the medical record review:

> Q. So would you mind just reading this for us?
> A. She returns today for follow-up and has excellent range of motion and strength of her right upper extremities with a bulky volar forearm skin graft there.
> Q. And so by December 2025, you have, in the notes, documented excellent range of motion and strength in the right upper extremity, true?
> A. That's what it says.
> Q. And so what do you -- how do you character- -- do you think that's accurate?
> A. Actually, I know I don't have full motion.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 93:15-25

Colette Boy reads the words "excellent range of motion and strength" in her own voice. She then disputes the characterization — "I know I don't have full motion." But the read-aloud is on the record. Her doctor wrote it. She read it. The dispute only draws attention to the finding. A jury that hears the plaintiff read "excellent range of motion" and then say "I disagree" must decide who to believe — the plaintiff or her own treating physician. The read-aloud ensures there is no ambiguity about what the physician documented. The plaintiff read it herself.

## The Closing Salvo Replay (Boy, pp. 84-85)

The read-aloud technique feeds directly into the closing salvo. When Kemp begins the thirteen-question confirmation chain on page 100, he is replaying admissions that the witness already made — many of them during read-alouds:

> Q. And so as we saw in the EMS records, on the date of the loss, EMS recorded you fell in front of the UPS truck, correct?
> MS. FLEMING: Object to the form.
> THE WITNESS: Correct.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 101:22-102:1

"As we saw in the EMS records" — as *we* saw, because the witness read the records herself. The closing salvo is not introducing new information. It is replaying information the witness previously adopted through her own reading. The thirteen confirmations are not fresh concessions. They are ratifications of prior adoptions. And the prior adoptions carry more weight because they were spoken, not merely acknowledged.

---

## The Principle Across the Portfolio

The read-aloud technique appears in modified form across the sovereign portfolio. In the Negrette deposition, Kemp has the witness review VA records on screen — though the volume (2,122 pages) requires a more curated approach, hence his later correction to Miranda: "We skimmed through a curated selection of them, probably less than 30 pages total" (Negrette, p. 215:17-18). In the Sullivan deposition, crash reports are reviewed collaboratively. In the Adams deposition, the store surveillance video functions as the visual equivalent of a read-aloud — the witness watches the event and then narrates what she sees, adopting the video evidence through her own description.

But Boy is where the technique operates at its highest frequency. Eight exhibits. Multiple read-alouds. The EMS narrative, the ER narrative, the surgical history, the December follow-up note, the initial disclosures. Each time, the witness reads the document. Each time, the document's contents become the witness's words. Each time, the record grows a layer of defense-favorable testimony that originated not from the defense attorney's mouth but from the plaintiff's own.

Gerry Spence wrote that the advocate must make the witness's words do the work. He was talking about eliciting admissions through questions. Kemp has extended the principle to documents: make the witness's *reading* do the work. The document is the exhibit. The reading is the testimony. And the testimony, because it was spoken by the plaintiff rather than read by the attorney, is unimpeachable.

The witness who reads her own medical records into the record has built the walls of her own shell. Kemp merely designed the blueprint.

# Chapter 4: The Strategic Retreat

---

*"I can't answer these questions."*
— Colette Boy, Boy Deposition, p. 80:1

*"Yeah. For sure. How long do you need?"*
— QB1, immediately thereafter, p. 80:3

---

There is a moment on page 79 of the Boy deposition where the examination walks into a wall. The wall is not constructed of legal privilege or opposing counsel's instruction. The wall is constructed of a plaintiff who has decided, for reasons she will not articulate, that certain questions about property ownership are questions she will not answer.

The approach is routine. Boy has mentioned chopping tree limbs with an axe on the property. Kemp asks if she owns the property. She says no, her family does. He asks who they are:

> Q. And do they live -- but you said you -- no one lives there with you?
> A. Correct.
> Q. It -- was it like an inheritance, or what was the -- what's the ownership status?
> A. I've been entrusted to take care of the property.
> Q. And where does the owner live?
> A. Can you rephrase the question?
> Q. Of the property that you're the caretaker of, where does the homeowner live?
> A. (Indiscernible).
> Q. Sorry?
> A. Can you tell me -- can you ask me a different question?
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 79:1-80:14

"Can you ask me a different question?" This is not "I don't recall." This is not "I don't understand." This is a conscious refusal. Something about the property ownership question has activated a boundary that no prior question in three hours of testimony has approached. The witness is not confused. She is protecting something.

Kemp, who has the legal right to press — who could invoke the oath, who could ask Fleming to instruct her client, who could place the refusal on the record as a potential adverse inference — instead asks the gentlest possible follow-up:

> Q. Is there a reason you -- you don't feel comfortable sharing that with us?
> A. The situation is that I'm entrusted to take care of the property.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 79:15-18

She repeats the same non-answer. She is "entrusted." That is all she will say. Kemp tries one more angle — the property taxes:

> Q. I -- I totally understand. I'm just wondering who pays the property taxes?
> A. I do.
> Q. And so the person that owns it, though, how often do you speak to them?
> A. I can't answer -- answer these questions.
> Q. Why not?
> A. Because I can't answer these questions.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 79:19-80:1

And then — the retreat:

> THE WITNESS: May I have a break?
> MR. KEMP: Yeah. For sure. How long do you need?
> THE WITNESS: Ten to fifteen minutes.
> MR. KEMP: Okay. How's -- how's 1:10?
> MS. FLEMING: Sounds good.
> MR. KEMP: Excellent. Thank you, guys.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 80:2-7

He never returns to the topic. Not after the break. Not during the closing salvo. Not in a single subsequent question. The property ownership inquiry is abandoned entirely. The record contains a plaintiff who pays property taxes on a property she does not own, who is "entrusted" to care for it, and who refused to identify the owner. That is all the record will ever contain on this subject.

---

This is the Strategic Retreat, and it is the hardest technique in the deposition lawyer's arsenal — harder than the closing salvo, harder than the Shredder Hypothetical, harder than the Medical Education Method — because it requires the attorney to perform a cost-benefit analysis in real time, under oath, with a witness who is distressed and an opposing counsel who is watching, and to conclude that the benefit of pressing is outweighed by the cost of pressing.

The cost-benefit calculation is this:

**Benefit of pressing:** Learning who owns the property. This information is marginally useful — it might reveal assets, it might complicate the economic picture, it might provide background for an investigator. But Boy has no lost wages claim. She has no future earnings claim. Her economic lane is medical bills only, locked at $391,378.87 and rising. The property ownership does not change the medical bills. It does not change the mechanism of the crash. It does not change the mosquito metaphor or the fall-first sequence or the bicyclist-not-pedestrian impeachment. It is marginal information.

**Cost of pressing:** Rapport destruction. For three hours, Kemp has maintained a comfort architecture so complete that Colette Boy volunteered the mosquito-and-the-windshield philosophy of her life. She talked about Twistee Treat. She talked about the Holy Spirit. She talked about Dexter and the hurricane ice. She read her own medical records aloud. She confirmed thirteen consecutive admissions in what would become the closing salvo. All of this was built on trust — the trust that the man across the Zoom screen meant it when he said he was not there to play gotcha.

Pressing on property ownership — pushing past a plaintiff's visible distress, demanding answers to questions she has twice said she cannot answer — would confirm the plaintiff's worst suspicion about defense attorneys. It would say: *the Anti-Gotcha Declaration was a lie. I was always going to push when it mattered.* And once that trust is broken, the remaining questions in the deposition become adversarial. The witness hardens. The answers become shorter, more guarded, more coached-sounding. The closing salvo, which requires a relaxed and cooperative witness to produce thirteen consecutive "Corrects," becomes a grinding confrontation.

Kemp chose the closing salvo over the property answer. The closing salvo was worth more. The property answer was worth less. The calculation was correct.

---

What Gerry Spence understood — and what he articulated in passages that most defense attorneys skip because Spence was a plaintiff's lawyer — is that every deposition contains a moment where the attorney must choose between *more information* and *better information.* More information is always available. There is always another question. There is always another document. There is always another line of inquiry that might produce something useful. The temptation is to pursue all of them, because the deposition is the one opportunity to question the witness under oath, and anything you don't ask now you may never get.

But better information — the kind that wins cases, the kind that produces closing-salvo confirmations, the kind that generates mosquito metaphors — requires a witness who is comfortable, who trusts the process, who trusts the attorney. And trust, once broken, cannot be rebuilt in the same session. The attorney who pushes past a boundary destroys the architecture that produced everything before the boundary. He gets the property answer and loses the closing salvo. He wins the battle and loses the war.

Kemp won the war. The retreat was not weakness. The retreat was architecture. The retreat was the proof that the Anti-Gotcha Declaration was real.

And on page 100, when the closing salvo began — "Earlier today, you testified that you intended on stopping before you got to the side street, correct?" — Colette Boy said "Yes." She said it because she trusted the man asking. She trusted the man asking because when she said "I can't answer these questions," the man asking said "Yeah. For sure. How long do you need?"

The retreat made the salvo possible. The retreat was the salvo's foundation. And the foundation held.

# Chapter 5: The Tears and the Silence

---

*"I was fighting very hard to stay from not fainting, but I -- there's -- I don't remember the timeline properly because I don't -- I don't remember everything that happened --"*
— Colette Boy, Boy Deposition, p. 45:8-10

---

Forty-five minutes.

The existing analyses — the Highlight Reel, the Four Pillars, the Compendium — document that Colette Boy wept for approximately forty-five minutes during her deposition. The number is cited as evidence of emotional intensity. The number is cited as proof of the comfort architecture's efficacy — a witness who weeps in front of opposing counsel is a witness who has stopped performing and started being. The number appears in every summary.

What the summaries do not examine is what Maxwell Kemp *did* during those forty-five minutes. Because the answer — the answer that sits in the transcript like a negative space in a painting, defined by what surrounds it rather than by what fills it — is: almost nothing.

The nothing is the technique.

---

The tears begin somewhere around page 48, when Boy describes the UPS driver cursing at her. Kemp's immediate response:

> Q. Please take a minute, Ms. Boy. I -- I totally understand. Do you want to take a little break? Maybe, like, two minutes? Okay. You're good? Okay. And then --
> A. No. I do not want to take a break.
> Q. Totally understood. Thank you. I -- I appreciate you powering through with us.
> — Boy Transcript, p. 48:3-8

The pattern is established in these six lines: (1) acknowledge the distress; (2) offer the break; (3) accept the refusal without resistance; (4) express gratitude for the witness's endurance. This pattern repeats with liturgical consistency throughout the deposition.

When Boy describes the wound VAC — the device that had to be peeled off daily, the pain she described as "being skinned alive" — the testimony runs uninterrupted for nearly two full transcript pages:

> A. ...And that had to be peeled off, which felt like being skinned alive. But it had to be done every day. And a whole new wound VAC apparatus had to be -- the tape and everything had to be peeled back on -- had to be taped back on. And through each one of these procedures, I would tell the -- the lady -- I would apologize ahead of time. I said, Please just ignore me. It's just I -- I -- but I would scream and -- and -- and throw my legs up in the air and scream and -- and -- and yelp. And I said, Please just ignore me. Just pretend I'm not here.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 71:6-15

Kemp does not interrupt. He does not redirect. He does not say, "I appreciate that, Ms. Boy, but let me move to my next question." He lets the testimony run. The plaintiff is describing daily torture — the removal of a vacuum-sealed adhesive from an open wound, the screaming, the yelping, the apologies to the wound care technician — and the defense attorney sits in silence and lets the record accumulate.

Then, later, when Boy's arm begins to tighten physically during the deposition:

> A. My arm's getting really tight.
> Q. I'm sorry?
> A. I said, My arm's getting really tight.
> Q. Oh. Your arm is now? Do you need a break?
> A. No.
> Q. I promise, we -- we're just covering the rest of the treatment and just a couple of wrap-up things, and we'll have you out of here soon.
> — Boy Transcript, pp. 93:1-8

"Do you need a break?" offered the instant the witness reports physical distress. "No." "I promise, we're just covering the rest." The promise is a time contract — a guarantee that the ordeal has a visible endpoint. This is pain management as rapport management. The witness knows the examiner is monitoring her physical state in real time. The witness knows the examiner will stop if asked. The witness knows the examiner keeps his promises because every prior promise has been kept — every break was granted immediately, every question was rephrased when she asked, every "I don't remember" was met with "No problem" or "No worries at all."

---

The strategic dimension of the silence is this: forty-five minutes of tears on a videotaped deposition is not merely evidence of the plaintiff's suffering. It is a trial argument.

A plaintiff who cannot maintain composure for a four-hour examination — who weeps through the wound VAC description, who weeps through the Muller confrontation, who requests breaks because her arm is physically tightening — is a plaintiff whose emotional distress is visible, documented, and available to both sides at trial. The plaintiff's attorney will argue: *Look at what UPS did to this woman. She could not even get through a deposition without weeping.* The defense attorney will argue something subtler and potentially more devastating: *The emotional distress you are witnessing is real, but is it proportionate? Is it caused by the 2025 incident, or is it the accumulated weight of a woman who was hit by a car in 2024, who has idiopathic thrombocytopenia with a platelet count of 3,000, who has not worked in ten years, who lives alone with a protective dog, and who has experienced a cascade of medical traumas that long predate our client's truck?*

The forty-five minutes of tears are the defense's emotional fragility argument — not that Boy is faking, but that Boy's emotional response to the 2025 incident is colored by everything that came before it, and the UPS driver should not be liable for the pre-existing psychological landscape.

Kemp never makes this argument during the deposition. He never suggests that the tears are disproportionate. He never implies that Boy is performing. What he does is let the tears run, let the record accumulate, and let the videotape capture a woman whose emotional state during a deposition is so pronounced that a jury will have to ask itself: is this about the arm, or is this about everything?

The silence is the technique. The silence says: *I am here. I am not pushing. Take your time.* And the witness takes her time, and the time becomes the record, and the record becomes the argument.

---

There is a counterargument, and it is important to name: letting a witness cry for forty-five minutes is inhumane. A decent person would have insisted on a break. A decent person would have suspended the examination. A decent person would not have continued to ask questions of a woman who was visibly suffering.

The counterargument fails on the facts. Kemp offered breaks repeatedly. Boy refused them repeatedly. Kemp accepted her refusals without resistance. He did not push through her distress — she pushed through her own distress, and he respected her agency to do so. When she asked for breaks, she received them instantly: "Yeah. Absolutely" (p. 16:10); "Oh. I was honestly going to suggest that" (p. 38:8); "Yes. Absolutely. How long do you need?" (p. 66:13); "Yeah. For sure. How long do you need?" (p. 80:3).

The record documents a defense attorney who offered more breaks than the witness accepted. The record documents a defense attorney who said "I promise, we're just covering the rest" and then kept that promise. The record documents a defense attorney who said "I appreciate you powering through with us" — not as a manipulation to keep her going, but as genuine recognition that what she was doing was hard.

Gerry Spence wrote that the trial lawyer must be willing to sit with another person's pain. Not to fix it. Not to exploit it. Not to minimize it. To sit with it. To be present with it. To let the other person feel that their pain has been witnessed by someone who is not looking away.

Kemp sat with it. For forty-five minutes, across breaks and breaks refused, across medical record reviews and wound VAC descriptions and an arm that was tightening in real time, he sat with Colette Boy's pain. He witnessed it. He did not look away. And the witnessing is on the record — every offered break, every accepted refusal, every "I totally understand," every "No problem."

The silence was the loudest thing in the room. The silence was the technique. The silence was the man.

And when the tears subsided and the closing salvo began, the witness who had wept for forty-five minutes said "Correct" thirteen consecutive times, because the man who sat with her pain was the same man who asked the questions, and she trusted him, and the trust was earned, and the trust held.

# Chapter 6: The Predicate Collaboration

---

*"How would you envision me laying a predicate that would satisfy you?"*
— QB1 to Darren Rousso, Small Deposition, p. 94:20-21

---

On page 94 of the Samuel Small deposition, Darren Rousso — the Ornery Mensch himself, the plaintiff's attorney whose competence is never in question and whose stubbornness is legendary — interposes an objection. Kemp is attempting to confront Small with statements from medical records that the witness may not have made personally. Rousso objects:

> MR. ROUSSO: I'm going to object to your attempts to, I guess, impeach or question him about statements not attributable to him unless you lay a predicate.
> — Small Transcript, p. 94:16-18

A standard objection. A reasonable one. The witness may not have made the statements in the medical records himself — they may be the provider's characterization, the intake nurse's transcription, the resident's summary. Without a predicate connecting the witness to the statement, the impeachment floats.

Any competent defense attorney would either (a) argue the objection, citing the liberal discovery standard, or (b) accept it and move on. Kemp does neither. He does something that appears in no deposition manual, no CLE handbook, no trial advocacy textbook published in any jurisdiction:

> MR. KEMP: How would you envision me laying a predicate that would satisfy you?
> — Small Transcript, p. 94:20-21

He asks opposing counsel to teach him how to impeach the plaintiff.

---

The sentence is addressed to Rousso but written for the court. It operates on three frequencies simultaneously.

**Frequency One: Interpersonal.** The question is respectful. It treats Rousso not as an adversary to be argued with but as a colleague whose professional opinion is valued. "How would you envision" — the language of genuine inquiry, of collaboration, of two professionals working through an evidentiary problem together. Rousso is disarmed not because he is tricked but because the question is sincere. Kemp genuinely wants to know the methodology that will satisfy the objection, because satisfying the objection is more efficient than fighting it.

**Frequency Two: Record.** The question creates a documentary trail. When Rousso answers — and he does answer, because the question is too respectfully posed to refuse — his answer becomes the defense's methodology, endorsed by the plaintiff's own counsel. Whatever predicate Rousso proposes, Kemp will adopt it, execute it, and proceed with the impeachment. If Rousso later objects again, the record reflects that Kemp asked for instructions, received them, and followed them. Rousso is foreclosed from objecting to a methodology he himself prescribed.

**Frequency Three: Architectural.** By converting the objection from a confrontation into a collaboration, Kemp transforms the dynamic of the examination. Rousso is no longer the gatekeeper blocking access to the witness. Rousso is the guide explaining how to unlock the gate. The shift is permanent — for the rest of the deposition, Rousso's objections carry the implicit context of a man who has already helped the defense attorney lay predicates.

And Rousso, disarmed by the sincerity, provides the answer:

> MR. ROUSSO: Do you recall telling the doctor this about what you remember? ... If you establish, did you read your medical records prior to today's deposition ... I mean, you can just ask it that way, too.
> — Small Transcript, pp. 94:22-95:7

Opposing counsel has just dictated the impeachment methodology to the defense. On the record. Kemp immediately deploys it:

> Q. Do you recall telling the provider at Physical Therapy Associates that you denied any prior history of accident or injury?
> — Small Transcript, p. 95:13-15

The predicate is laid. The methodology is Rousso's own. The impeachment proceeds. And the record contains, permanently, a plaintiff's attorney who taught the defense attorney how to catch his own client.

---

This technique has a parallel in the Mitchell deposition, though the mechanism is different. In Mitchell, opposing counsel Robert Caldwell does not merely provide a methodology — he actively co-authors the impeachment of his own client's criminal history. When Kemp walks through Mitchell's eleven adjudicated guiltys — the felony cocaine, the retail thefts, the domestic battery — Caldwell appears on the Zoom call and helps sort the records:

The collaborative approach forecloses the argument that the criminal history was presented unfairly or taken out of context. Caldwell helped build the record. Caldwell cannot later claim the record was built improperly. The fox guarded the henhouse and counted the feathers.

---

There is a third manifestation of the Predicate Collaboration on page 97 of the Small transcript, and it is even more compressed:

> MR. ROUSSO: I'm objecting to form because a 2013 citation has no relevance to this case or accident.
> MR. KEMP: Relevance isn't the test during a deposition.
> MR. ROUSSO: You can ask him whatever you want. Go ahead.
> — Small Transcript, p. 97:19-25

Seven words. "Relevance isn't the test during a deposition." This is a correct statement of Florida civil procedure — the discovery standard is "reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence," which is broader than trial relevance. Kemp states the rule. He does not argue it. He does not cite authority. He does not engage in a colloquy. Seven words. The rule is the argument. The argument is the rule.

And Rousso concedes: "You can ask him whatever you want. Go ahead." The objection is not merely overruled by the witness's answer — it is withdrawn by the objecting counsel himself, on the record, with the verbal equivalent of a shrug.

---

What Gerry Spence understood about adversarial dynamics — and what the Predicate Collaboration demonstrates — is that the most effective way to neutralize opposition is not to defeat it but to co-opt it. The opponent who helps you build the record is the opponent who cannot tear it down. The opponent who teaches you the predicate is the opponent who has endorsed the predicate. The opponent who says "You can ask him whatever you want" is the opponent who has abdicated the gatekeeper role entirely.

Kemp converts adversaries into collaborators not through charm alone — though the charm is real — but through the structural mechanism of asking genuine questions about methodology. "How would you envision me laying a predicate that would satisfy you?" is not a trick. It is a genuine request for professional guidance, posed with the humility of a man who has taken the bar seven times and knows what it means to need help. The humility is real. The help is real. And the help, once given, becomes a structural element of the defense record that the helper cannot later dismantle.

The Predicate Collaboration is the Ignorance Posture applied to opposing counsel. "For us people who are ignorant" makes the plaintiff teach the defense. "How would you envision me laying a predicate?" makes opposing counsel teach the defense. Both techniques rest on the same foundation: the willingness to be the student rather than the authority. And both techniques produce the same result: the teacher builds the defense without realizing it.

The man who asks to be taught is the most dangerous man in the room.

# Chapter 7: The Compliment as Impeachment

---

*"Yeah, biceps, good posture, so no issues with your shoulder."*
— QB1, Negrette Deposition, p. 184:23

---

There are two ways to introduce surveillance footage of a plaintiff exercising at a gym after claiming debilitating spinal injuries. The first way — the way practiced by most defense attorneys, the way taught in CLE programs, the way that makes for dramatic trial moments — is the ambush. You wait. You let the plaintiff testify about how he cannot lift, cannot bend, cannot exercise. You let the narrative build. And then you introduce the surveillance photographs and say: "Isn't it true, Mr. Negrette, that on August 21, 2024, you were photographed performing preacher curls with visible weight on the bar?"

The ambush is satisfying. The ambush is dramatic. The ambush is also, in Gerry Spence's framework, the weakest possible deployment of the evidence, because the ambush turns the witness hostile, because the hostile witness fights every subsequent question, because the fight produces ambiguity, and because ambiguity is what the plaintiff's attorney wants.

The second way — the way Maxwell Kemp introduces surveillance of a combat veteran doing gym work sixteen days after a parking lot incident that supposedly destroyed his spine — is this:

> Q. Oh, so here you are on August 21st, 2024. They saw you doing your thing, getting some -- is that hack squats in the gym? What exercise is that?
> A. Which one? The one where I'm sitting down, that's biceps.
> Q. Oh, that's preacher curl machine?
> A. Yeah. I'm sitting down.
> Q. Yeah, biceps, good posture, so no issues with your shoulder.
> — Negrette Transcript, pp. 184:10-24

"Yeah, biceps, good posture, so no issues with your shoulder." The sentence is structured as an observation, not an accusation. QB1 is complimenting the plaintiff's gym form. The word "good" appears. The word "posture" appears. A jury reading this transcript will see a defense attorney noting, with what sounds like admiration, that the plaintiff has good posture while performing loaded resistance exercises. The compliment contains the impeachment. Good posture doing preacher curls means the shoulder that is claimed as injured is functioning well enough to perform loaded resistance exercises with proper form.

The observation is casual. The record is devastating. The $4.65 million delta between the $5 million demand and the $350,000 proposal for settlement is contained in that one sentence.

---

But the technique does not begin with the surveillance. It begins two hours earlier, when Kemp asks a question that has no apparent legal relevance:

> Q. What's your Wilks score?
> A. My what score?

The Wilks score — a formula that normalizes powerlifting totals for body weight — is not a legal concept. It is not a medical concept. It is a gym concept, known only to people who take strength training seriously. Kemp knows what a Wilks score is because he lifts at Esporta. Negrette does not know what it is, despite being, by his own testimony, a serious lifter since Iraq in 2004. But the question opens a door that Negrette walks through enthusiastically:

> A. My squat in Afghanistan was 490. My bench was 965.

And Kemp, without missing a beat:

> Q. So your bench was almost equal to your squat. That's crazy. I have the opposite problem. No bench, but good squat.

Two things happen simultaneously. First, the record now contains the plaintiff's self-identified history as a serious, long-term lifter — a man who brags about his numbers, who posts videos on Facebook, whose identity is constructed around physical performance. Second, Kemp has bonded with the witness over lifting in a way no plaintiff's attorney could have anticipated or prepared against. "I have the opposite problem. No bench, but good squat." The man asking the questions is a fellow lifter. The adversarial frame has been replaced by gym-talk between two men who understand what a 490 squat means.

Then comes the Esporta bond:

> Q. I am also an Esporta member.

They share a gym chain. They commiserate about the LA Fitness transition. "That's how they got me," Kemp says. "I don't mind it, though, because at least the weights move." They share a problem. They share a language.

When the surveillance photographs arrive two hours later, Negrette is not confronted with them. He is shown them inside a conversation he has been having all day — a conversation about lifting, about gyms, about exercise form. The photographs are not introduced as evidence of fraud. They are introduced as *more gym talk*:

> Q. Then here you are going to Publix again. Got to get that protein.
> A. Yeah.
> — Negrette Transcript, p. 185:18-20

"Got to get that protein." The plaintiff carrying heavy bags from Publix — evidence that his claimed injuries do not prevent normal daily activities — is framed as a gym bro getting his post-workout nutrition. The photograph accuses. The attorney compliments. The jury will see both the photograph and the tone, and the tone makes the photograph worse for the plaintiff, because the photograph inside a casual conversation looks like a man living his normal life, while the photograph inside a confrontation looks like a man who might have an explanation.

And then the vanity plate:

> Q. Oh, so here is the red Porsche, Cayenne?
> A. The Cayenne, yeah.
> Q. IHQ 04?
> A. Iraq 04.
> Q. Oh, I didn't get that.
> — Negrette Transcript, p. 185:7-12

"Iraq 04." The vanity plate on the plaintiff's red Porsche Cayenne references his deployment. Kemp says "Oh, I didn't get that" — learning about the witness, humanizing the exchange, bonding over the man's service. The surveillance is being introduced inside a conversation about a veteran's car. The photographs are being introduced inside a friendship.

---

Negrette's response to the surveillance is remarkable in its candor:

> A. I'm not like 90 percent of people.

He does not deny the activities. He does not claim the photographs are fabricated. He does not argue that preacher curls don't require shoulder function. He explains: he pushes through. He is tougher than most. His tolerance for pain is exceptional.

This candor is the fruit of the comfort architecture. A witness who has been ambushed with surveillance becomes defensive: "That was a good day." "The pain wasn't as bad that morning." "My doctor said I could try light exercise." A witness who has been shown surveillance inside a gym conversation becomes explanatory: "I'm not like 90 percent of people." The defensive witness produces testimony that can be rehabilitated on redirect. The explanatory witness produces testimony that rehabilitates the defense.

"I'm not like 90 percent of people" is a sentence the plaintiff's attorney cannot use. It is a sentence the defense attorney can use in closing argument: *Mr. Negrette told you himself — he is not like 90 percent of people. He is tougher. He pushes through. And if he pushes through pain that would stop 90 percent of people, how can we be certain that the pain he describes is caused by a truck that moved twelve inches in a parking lot, rather than by the degenerative disc disease documented in his VA records for four years before the incident?*

The compliment was the impeachment. The friendship was the architecture. The $4.65 million evaporation was the result.

Gerry Spence wrote that the greatest cross-examination is the one the witness does not recognize as cross-examination. The surveillance normalization in the Negrette deposition is not merely unrecognized cross-examination. It is cross-examination that the witness experienced as camaraderie.

The man who said "good posture" was the most dangerous man in the room. The witness who said "yeah" was the most cooperative witness in the portfolio. And the cooperation was genuine, which is why it was devastating.

# Chapter 8: The Temperature Differential

---

*"This is crazy, Judge."*
— James Holloway, Peacock Hearing, p. 9:14

*"Defendants fully and proactively complied with the Court's instructions at every step."*
— QB1, Peacock Hearing, p. 6:10-13

---

The Peacock hearing lasted ten minutes. From 10:16 a.m. to 10:26 a.m. on March 31, 2025, before the Honorable Emily Peacock in the Thirteenth Judicial Circuit, Hillsborough County. A motion for attorney's fees and interest on a $939,000 settlement that had been paid — paid in full, paid to the correct payees, paid after defense counsel independently discovered that plaintiff's counsel had identified the wrong Medicare lien holders.

What happened in those ten minutes is a study in temperature control — the technique of remaining cooler than opposing counsel under conditions where opposing counsel is growing hotter, and letting the differential do the persuading.

---

Holloway opens with grievance:

> MR. HOLLOWAY: Your Honor, we're here on the issue of interest. When we were last together, you asked us to resolve the issue of interest on our own. I tried, but according to Mr. Maxwell, they were not interested in paying any interest. This case settled in December. We did not receive payment until March. It's a lot of money, Judge. A great deal of interest.
> — Peacock Transcript, pp. 5:19-6:1

The tone is aggrieved. The argument is emotional: "A lot of money, Judge." "A great deal of interest." Holloway is appealing to judicial sympathy. The money was delayed. His clients were denied their settlement for two months. The interest earned on $939,000 over two months is substantial.

Then Kemp:

> MR. KEMP: Thank you, Your Honor. Our position is that there's no basis for interest, because Defendants fully and proactively complied with the Court's instructions at every step. This is simply just re-litigating an issue already resolved by the Court.
> — Peacock Transcript, pp. 6:10-14

Forty-seven words of substance. Three operative concepts: *fully*, *proactively*, *complied*. The argument is not that the delay was short, or that the interest amount is trivial, or that Holloway is being unreasonable. The argument is that there was no noncompliance. The defense acted. The defense acted at every step. The defense acted proactively — meaning before being asked, before being ordered, before being compelled. The delay was not a delay. It was the time required to do the work that plaintiff's counsel should have done.

Then the factual devastation:

> MR. KEMP: When the checks were issued, Plaintiff mistakenly identified the Equian lien as the lien for Ms. Narvaez. On our own investigation, we found it was actually an Optum lien for her, and that's who we made the check to as soon as we got that information. And then it was EXL Health for Mr. Letona, which we found on our own initiative as well.
> — Peacock Transcript, pp. 6:15-22

The defense team — Kemp's team — had independently discovered that the Medicare lien payees identified by plaintiff's counsel were wrong. Plaintiff said Equian. It was Optum. Plaintiff did not identify EXL Health at all. The defense found both correct payees through its own investigation. The reason the checks took time was not recalcitrance — it was that the defense was doing plaintiff's homework.

Holloway interrupts:

> MR. HOLLOWAY: I gave you the letters.
> THE COURT: Hey, let Mr. Kemp finish, please.
> — Peacock Transcript, pp. 6:23-24

"Hey, let Mr. Kemp finish, please." The judge has intervened to protect the defense from the plaintiff's interruption. This is not a ruling. It is a social signal. The judge wants to hear what Kemp has to say. The judge does not want to hear Holloway interrupt. The credibility differential is established in seven words from the bench.

---

Kemp's reply argument, delivered after Holloway's full response, contains the moment that gives this chapter its resonance:

> MR. KEMP: On January 26th is when we got the letter Mr. Holloway is referring to, and then just a few days later, the motion to enforce settlement was filed. That gave us very little time, and then on top of that, it -- the -- indicated that it was an Equian lien. On our own onus, we found it was EXL Health and Optum. So we were just dotting our T's, crossing our I's, making sure that this Medicare issue was taken care of.
> — Peacock Transcript, pp. 8:4-12

Dotting our T's, crossing our I's. The idiom is backward. The conventional expression is "dotting our I's, crossing our T's." The inversion is not calculated — it is the sound of a man speaking naturally under pressure, focused on substance rather than polish. And that sound — the sound of earnestness, of a person trying to get it right rather than trying to sound polished — is more persuasive than perfection.

A rehearsed attorney dots his I's and crosses his T's in the correct order. A genuine attorney dots his T's and crosses his I's because he is thinking about Medicare liens, not idioms. The stumble authenticates the argument. The stumble says: *We were working. We were in the weeds. We were trying to pay the right people the right amounts. The idiom came out wrong because we were focused on the substance, not the presentation.*

---

The temperature differential widens as the hearing proceeds. Holloway escalates:

> MR. HOLLOWAY: But this is crazy, Judge. They have delayed and delayed and delayed in giving us this money.
> — Peacock Transcript, p. 9:14-15

"This is crazy, Judge" is the sound of a lawyer who has lost the argument and knows it. It is an appeal to judicial emotion rather than legal principle. Kemp remains at the same temperature:

> MR. KEMP: Well, it was Optum, not Equian, for Ms. Narvaez.
> — Peacock Transcript, p. 10:20-21

One sentence. A factual correction. No heat. No rhetoric. No "This is not crazy, Judge — this is responsible litigation management." Just: the payee was Optum, not Equian. Your client got it wrong. We fixed it.

When the judge asks who the insurance carrier was:

> MR. KEMP: I have it right here. Houston International, Skyward.
> — Peacock Transcript, p. 11:11-12

A lawyer who knows his carrier's name off the top of his head during a contested hearing is a lawyer who has done the work. A lawyer who says "This is crazy, Judge" is a lawyer who has not.

---

The ruling:

> THE COURT: I don't really see the timing as being extensive in this case... I'm not inclined to think that I should give it to you at this point... I am concerned that they were trying to do the right thing by paying Medicaid. That's what we want to have happen.
> — Peacock Transcript, pp. 8:13-12:1

Then:

# Chapter 10: The Record Correction Mid-Redirect

*"You said eight hours. It's six hours."*

On page 214 of the Negrette deposition, after six hours of examination that has produced the most devastating single-witness record in the sovereign portfolio — the suicide-attempt disclosure, the pre-existing degenerative disc disease, the surveillance photographs, the materially incomplete interrogatories — Ginger Miranda begins her redirect.

This is the moment the plaintiff's attorney has been waiting for. Six hours of defense examination. 208 pages of testimony. The defense attorney's role during redirect is to listen, to object when appropriate, and to note anything that requires re-cross.

Kemp does something different. He corrects the record during opposing counsel's redirect examination — not once, but twice — with a precision that reveals a practitioner who understands that the deposition transcript is a document that will be read by a judge, and that every inaccuracy in that document, even an inaccuracy introduced by opposing counsel during her own examination, is an inaccuracy that the defense cannot afford to leave uncorrected.

---

The first correction:

> MS. MIRANDA: We have been here now since 10:00 o'clock this morning. That's eight hours without any substantial break --
> MR. KEMP: Objection. It's six hours. Sorry.
> MS. MIRANDA: Okay. What is your objection, Mr. Kemp?
> MR. KEMP: You said eight hours. It's six hours.
> — Negrette Transcript, pp. 216:1-7

"You said eight hours. It's six hours." Miranda was characterizing the duration of the examination to establish that her client was exhausted, that the extended session may have affected his answers, that the testimony was produced under conditions of fatigue. It is a standard rehabilitation tactic: the witness was tired, the questions were repetitive, the answers may not fully represent the witness's considered testimony.

The problem is that "eight hours" is factually wrong. The deposition began at 10:04 a.m. It concluded at approximately 4:14 p.m. That is six hours and ten minutes, including breaks. Miranda rounded up by two hours — a rounding error that, left uncorrected, would have created a record suggesting the examination was a third longer than it actually was. The difference between a six-hour deposition and an eight-hour deposition is the difference between thorough and oppressive. The difference matters at trial, where plaintiff's counsel might argue that the defense engaged in abusive discovery tactics by deposing a combat veteran with PTSD for eight consecutive hours.

Kemp's correction is not combative. "Sorry" — he apologizes for the interruption. The apology is reflexive, the habit of a man whose default mode is courtesy. But the correction is non-negotiable. The record must be accurate. Six hours, not eight. Miranda's response — "What is your objection?" — reveals her surprise. She is not accustomed to having her characterizations corrected during her own examination. She is looking for a formal objection category. Kemp provides none beyond the factual correction itself: "You said eight hours. It's six hours." The correction is its own objection.

---

The second correction arrives one page later:

> MS. MIRANDA: And now after being shown, I believe counsel said, over 2,000 pages of medical records, did that refresh your recollection as to some of the other times that you have injured your lower back?
> A. Yes.
> MR. KEMP: Sorry. I have an objection. I didn't take you through 2,000 pages. We skimmed through a 2,000-page document. Thank you.
> MS. MIRANDA: I'm sorry. What was your objection so I can make sure --
> MR. KEMP: You said I took Mark through 2,000 pages of medical records. No, we skimmed through a curated selection of them, probably less than 30 pages total.
> MS. MIRANDA: Okay. My question was: Do you have an objection so I can make sure I ask an appropriate question?
> MR. KEMP: Objection; misleading.
> — Negrette Transcript, pp. 215:4-22

The distinction Kemp draws is precise and consequential. Miranda characterized the examination as having "shown" the witness "over 2,000 pages of medical records." The implication: the defense buried the witness in paperwork, overwhelming a combat veteran with an incomprehensible volume of medical documentation. In reality, Kemp reviewed a curated selection — "probably less than 30 pages total" — from a 2,122-page VA record set. The document was large. The examination of it was targeted. There is a world of difference between "I took you through 2,000 pages" and "I showed you 30 pages from a 2,000-page file."

The correction converts the characterization from "overwhelming volume" to "surgical precision." Thirty pages from 2,122 is less than 1.5 percent of the total record. Kemp identified the relevant entries — the degenerative disc disease, the cervicalgia, the bilateral SI joint injections, the psychiatric notations — and showed them to the witness. He did not dump two thousand pages on a combat veteran and ask him to explain them. He curated. He targeted. He performed surgery, not carpet bombing. And if the record suggested otherwise — if Miranda's misleading characterization were left uncorrected — a future judge might conclude that the examination was excessive, that the defense was engaging in document-dump tactics, that the deposition should be given less weight because it was conducted in bad faith.

"Objection; misleading." Two words. The formal objection category, provided only when Miranda asked for it. The substance of the correction had already been delivered in the preceding sentences. The formal label was an afterthought. The correction was the point.

---

The Record Correction Mid-Redirect is a technique that appears nowhere else in the portfolio in this developed form, and it reveals something fundamental about Kemp's relationship with the deposition transcript.

Most lawyers treat the deposition transcript as a byproduct of the examination — a record of what happened, generated automatically by the court reporter, reviewed later for errata. Kemp treats the transcript as a document he is writing in real time. Every question is a sentence in that document. Every answer is a sentence in that document. And every characterization by opposing counsel — even during opposing counsel's own redirect — is a sentence in that document that must be accurate, because the document will be read by a judge who was not in the room and who will take the words at face value.

The Record Consciousness Layer — identified in the Oral Advocacy Codex as the fifth principle of the unified technique — operates at full frequency during the mid-redirect corrections. Kemp is not arguing with Miranda. He is editing the document. He is the attorney, the witness's examiner, the record's custodian, and the transcript's fact-checker, all simultaneously. When Miranda writes "eight hours" into the record, Kemp edits it to "six hours." When Miranda writes "2,000 pages," Kemp edits it to "less than 30 pages." The edits are not objections in the traditional sense. They are corrections to a document that both attorneys are co-authoring and that only one of them is treating with the precision it deserves.
