# Chapter 8: Damages & Anchoring

***

_"This is crazy, Judge."_ — James Holloway, Peacock Hearing, p. 9:14

_"Defendants fully and proactively complied with the Court's instructions at every step."_ — QB1, Peacock Hearing, p. 6:10-13

***

The Peacock hearing lasted ten minutes. From 10:16 a.m. to 10:26 a.m. on March 31, 2025, before the Honorable Emily Peacock in the Thirteenth Judicial Circuit, Hillsborough County. A motion for attorney's fees and interest on a $939,000 settlement that had been paid — paid in full, paid to the correct payees, paid after defense counsel independently discovered that plaintiff's counsel had identified the wrong Medicare lien holders.

What happened in those ten minutes is a study in temperature control — the technique of remaining cooler than opposing counsel under conditions where opposing counsel is growing hotter, and letting the differential do the persuading.

***

Holloway opens with grievance:

> MR. HOLLOWAY: Your Honor, we're here on the issue of interest. When we were last together, you asked us to resolve the issue of interest on our own. I tried, but according to Mr. Maxwell, they were not interested in paying any interest. This case settled in December. We did not receive payment until March. It's a lot of money, Judge. A great deal of interest. — Peacock Transcript, pp. 5:19-6:1

The tone is aggrieved. The argument is emotional: "A lot of money, Judge." "A great deal of interest." Holloway is appealing to judicial sympathy. The money was delayed. His clients were denied their settlement for two months. The interest earned on $939,000 over two months is substantial.

Then Kemp:

> MR. KEMP: Thank you, Your Honor. Our position is that there's no basis for interest, because Defendants fully and proactively complied with the Court's instructions at every step. This is simply just re-litigating an issue already resolved by the Court. — Peacock Transcript, pp. 6:10-14

Forty-seven words of substance. Three operative concepts: _fully_, _proactively_, _complied_. The argument is not that the delay was short, or that the interest amount is trivial, or that Holloway is being unreasonable. The argument is that there was no noncompliance. The defense acted. The defense acted at every step. The defense acted proactively — meaning before being asked, before being ordered, before being compelled. The delay was not a delay. It was the time required to do the work that plaintiff's counsel should have done.

Then the factual devastation:

> MR. KEMP: When the checks were issued, Plaintiff mistakenly identified the Equian lien as the lien for Ms. Narvaez. On our own investigation, we found it was actually an Optum lien for her, and that's who we made the check to as soon as we got that information. And then it was EXL Health for Mr. Letona, which we found on our own initiative as well. — Peacock Transcript, pp. 6:15-22

The defense team — Kemp's team — had independently discovered that the Medicare lien payees identified by plaintiff's counsel were wrong. Plaintiff said Equian. It was Optum. Plaintiff did not identify EXL Health at all. The defense found both correct payees through its own investigation. The reason the checks took time was not recalcitrance — it was that the defense was doing plaintiff's homework.

Holloway interrupts:

> MR. HOLLOWAY: I gave you the letters. THE COURT: Hey, let Mr. Kemp finish, please. — Peacock Transcript, pp. 6:23-24

"Hey, let Mr. Kemp finish, please." The judge has intervened to protect the defense from the plaintiff's interruption. This is not a ruling. It is a social signal. The judge wants to hear what Kemp has to say. The judge does not want to hear Holloway interrupt. The credibility differential is established in seven words from the bench.

***

Kemp's reply argument, delivered after Holloway's full response, contains the moment that gives this chapter its resonance:

> MR. KEMP: On January 26th is when we got the letter Mr. Holloway is referring to, and then just a few days later, the motion to enforce settlement was filed. That gave us very little time, and then on top of that, it -- the -- indicated that it was an Equian lien. On our own onus, we found it was EXL Health and Optum. So we were just dotting our T's, crossing our I's, making sure that this Medicare issue was taken care of. — Peacock Transcript, pp. 8:4-12

Dotting our T's, crossing our I's. The idiom is backward. The conventional expression is "dotting our I's, crossing our T's." The inversion is not calculated — it is the sound of a man speaking naturally under pressure, focused on substance rather than polish. And that sound — the sound of earnestness, of a person trying to get it right rather than trying to sound polished — is more persuasive than perfection.

A rehearsed attorney dots his I's and crosses his T's in the correct order. A genuine attorney dots his T's and crosses his I's because he is thinking about Medicare liens, not idioms. The stumble authenticates the argument. The stumble says: _We were working. We were in the weeds. We were trying to pay the right people the right amounts. The idiom came out wrong because we were focused on the substance, not the presentation._

***

The temperature differential widens as the hearing proceeds. Holloway escalates:

> MR. HOLLOWAY: But this is crazy, Judge. They have delayed and delayed and delayed in giving us this money. — Peacock Transcript, p. 9:14-15

"This is crazy, Judge" is the sound of a lawyer who has lost the argument and knows it. It is an appeal to judicial emotion rather than legal principle. Kemp remains at the same temperature:

> MR. KEMP: Well, it was Optum, not Equian, for Ms. Narvaez. — Peacock Transcript, p. 10:20-21

One sentence. A factual correction. No heat. No rhetoric. No "This is not crazy, Judge — this is responsible litigation management." Just: the payee was Optum, not Equian. Your client got it wrong. We fixed it.

When the judge asks who the insurance carrier was:

> MR. KEMP: I have it right here. Houston International, Skyward. — Peacock Transcript, p. 11:11-12

A lawyer who knows his carrier's name off the top of his head during a contested hearing is a lawyer who has done the work. A lawyer who says "This is crazy, Judge" is a lawyer who has not.

***

The ruling:

> THE COURT: I don't really see the timing as being extensive in this case... I'm not inclined to think that I should give it to you at this point... I am concerned that they were trying to do the right thing by paying Medicaid. That's what we want to have happen. — Peacock Transcript, pp. 8:13-12:1

Then:

> THE COURT: -- deny it. — Peacock Transcript, p. 12:5

Two syllables. Motion denied. And:

> THE COURT: Can you do an order, please, Mr. Kemp? MR. KEMP: Absolutely. Is there anything specific you'd like me to say? — Peacock Transcript, pp. 12:11-17

Order control. For the second time in the hearing portfolio — Hamilton being the first — the judge asks Kemp to prepare the order. Kemp accepts with "Absolutely" and follows up with "Is there anything specific you'd like me to say?" The question is not procedural. It is strategic: the attorney who drafts the order controls the written record. The written record outlasts the oral argument by years.

***

The temperature differential is the hearing equivalent of the comfort architecture. In the depositions, Kemp is warmer than the witness expects. In the hearings, Kemp is cooler than opposing counsel delivers. Both differentials produce the same result: the person controlling the temperature controls the room.

Holloway lost because he was hot. Kemp won because he was cool. The judge heard a man who was working — dotting T's, crossing I's, finding Optum, finding EXL Health — and a man who was complaining. The judge chose the man who was working.

Gerry Spence wrote about the power of quiet confidence — the advocate who does not need to raise his voice because the facts are doing the speaking. In the Peacock hearing, twelve words — "Defendants fully and proactively complied with the Court's instructions at every step" — did all the speaking. Everything else was temperature.
