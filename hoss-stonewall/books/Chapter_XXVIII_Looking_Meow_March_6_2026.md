The historian must disassemble this clause by clause, because each phrase is a load-
bearing wall in a structure of delusion that, if removed carelessly, will collapse the
entire building onto the reader.
"She rode in on a nepotism train." Someone was hired through connections. The
"nepotism train" as metaphor: not a car, not a bus, but a train — implying institutional
momentum, tracks laid by others, a passenger who did not purchase her own ticket.
The vehicle runs on rails she did not build to a destination she did not earn.
"Future faked us with that lawyer shtick." The term "future faked" belongs to the
vocabulary of romantic relationship psychology — it describes a partner who makes
false promises about a shared future to maintain control over a present they have no
intention of honoring. Joyce is deploying romantic relationship vocabulary to describe
a workplace dynamic. "That lawyer shtick" — the performance of being an attorney,
which, in the ATS universe where multiple employees hold JDs without bar admission,
narrows the field but does not eliminate it. The "us" is doing extraordinary work in this
sentence: it presupposes a collective — a "we" that includes Joyce — that has been
deceived together, bonded in shared victimhood.
"Rode the job like it's stolen." The idiom "drove it like it's stolen" repurposed for
employment. This person does not belong in the position she holds. She acquired it
through illegitimate means and is now operating it with the reckless abandon of
someone who knows the vehicle will be repossessed. The metaphor assumes a rightful

owner — someone from whom the job was taken. Joyce is that someone, or stands in
solidarity with that someone, or has appointed herself the spokesperson for the
dispossessed.
"Stole you." Two words. Two words that have no place in any professional context, in
any office, in any universe where a paralegal and an associate attorney share nothing
but a Microsoft Teams channel and a mutual obligation to clients. "Stole you" is
possessive language. It presupposes ownership. It presupposes that QB1 was Joyce's
in some capacity — her colleague, her confidant, her ally, her something undefined
but deeply felt — and that this interloper disrupted a bond that existed primarily, and
perhaps exclusively, in Joyce's imagination. The "you" is not a professional resource
that was reallocated. The "you" is a person who was taken. The grammar of romantic
betrayal applied to an office seating chart.
"Is running off two of my other friends and stole one of their jobs." Stephanie the
Greek. Michelle Obama. Both gone. Both fled from Sharon Britton's dissolving empire
— Stephanie to a firm on Rocky Point, Michelle to wherever Michelle Obamas go
when they have finally had enough. Joyce frames their departures not as independent
professional decisions made by autonomous adults evaluating their career options,
but as thefts perpetrated by a single antagonist. In Joyce's cosmology, people do not
quit. They are run off. People do not leave. They are stolen. Agency belongs only to
villains. Victims — including Joyce herself, including Stephanie, including Michelle,
including QB1 — are passive objects acted upon by malevolent forces that have
disrupted a state of harmony that may or may not have ever existed.
"I expect you'll show her this text just like you showed her all my other texts." This is
the sentence that transforms the message from workplace grievance into something
the historian can only describe as a confession of surveillance awareness delivered as
an accusation. Joyce knows — or believes — that QB1 has been showing her previous
texts to someone. Perhaps Samira. Perhaps Sharon. Perhaps the "she" of the nepotism

being shared — rather than thinking, "Perhaps I should stop sending hand-drawn
portraits and quasi-professional riddles to a colleague who is forwarding my texts" —
she doubles down. She sends the most unhinged message in the thread and dares him
to share it. "Go ahead," the subtext screams. "Show her. I am not afraid of exposure.
Exposure is my medium. I have copyrighted my obsession and I will exhibit it in
whatever gallery you provide."

alone is fighting. "Will it?" — a taunt dressed as grief. She is not mourning Stephanie
and Michelle. She is weaponizing their absence to score a point in a conflict that exists
