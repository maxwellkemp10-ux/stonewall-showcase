# STONEWALL AT MANASSAS

## Chapter: The Annual Review Gauntlet, or, How One Man Became Eighty Cases and the Spreadsheet That Proved It

### *A Meditation on the UPS Review of March 11–12, the Financial Archaeology of Client 001854, the Filevine Audit of Big Satan's Billing Ecosystem, and Two Opposing Counsel Calls That Reveal the Only Functional Relationship in the Entire Practice*

---

> *"You're such a straight shooter."*
> — Mike Boyle (OC), Decker v. UPS, December 9, 2025, speaking to the only person at Alvarez, Thompson & Smoak who shoots anything at all

---

There is a particular species of corporate ritual that occurs when an insurance carrier with several hundred million dollars in annual claims exposure convenes a formal review of the law firm to which it has entrusted the management of its litigation portfolio. The ritual requires spreadsheets. The ritual requires five-minute intervals. The ritual requires the law firm to present each claim — one by one, in ascending order of existential dread — to the supervisors and case managers who will determine whether the relationship continues, whether the volume increases, and whether the person sitting across the table in Tampa or on the Zoom screen deserves to keep running the show.

The UPS Annual Review of March 11–12, 2026, is one such ritual. And when you examine the spreadsheet that structures Day One — eighty cases, eighteen columns, time slots from 10:00 AM to 4:55 PM with a single twenty-five-minute lunch break — you are not examining a spreadsheet. You are examining a map of one man's professional nervous system laid bare for inspection by four separate supervisors, each of whom believes they are reviewing "the firm's" work, when in fact they are reviewing the work of Maxwell Aaron Kemp and whatever thin scrim of institutional support happened to not actively sabotage him on any given Tuesday.

---

Consider the sheer topography of it. Eighty claims. Four supervisors — Ashlee Cushman-Snowden (she of the six exclamation points), Janice Younger (the Forgetting Incarnate, forty-two inquiries in thirty-two days), Shobah Ramdhanie (the Temporal Anomaly), and Ashton Washington (the Patronizer, "you poor thing"). A case manager named Linda Bland — and here we must pause to observe that Linda Bland, whose surname could not be more cosmically suited to the UPS institutional apparatus if Franz Kafka himself had christened her at the font, manages the largest concentration of these files. Anthony Dante. The full hydra.

The claims span from November 2021 (Skaggs, Jacob — incurred $1,175,000, the oldest surviving specimen in the portfolio) to December 2025 (Perry, Douglas — incurred $35,025, born into the docket less than three months before the review that will decide its fate). The total outstanding reserves visible on Day One alone cascade into the tens of millions. There is a $2,024,630 claim sitting in Row 80 — Washington, Francine v. UPS, Carroll-Williams driver, South Carolina — that represents, by itself, more exposure than the annual revenue of most solo practitioners in the state of Florida. And it is one line item on a spreadsheet that QB1 must be prepared to discuss, in detail, for five minutes, at 4:50 PM, after having already discussed seventy-nine other claims in five-minute intervals since breakfast.

The defense counsel column reads, with the monotony of a liturgical response: "Alvarez Thompson & Smoak." "Alvarez Thompson & Smoak." "Alvarez Thompson & Smoak." Rows 2 through 29 — all ATS. Then the docket shifts: Carr Allison picks up the Jacksonville overflow. Gunther Legal takes the rest of the state. Troy Thames handles South Carolina. Wilson Sheldon mops up the remainder. But the nucleus — the first twenty-nine claims, the morning session, the cases where ATS is not merely panel counsel but *the* panel counsel, the primary relationship — that is QB1's personal kingdom, and every supervisor at that table will be asking questions that only one person on the planet can answer, because only one person on the planet has done the work.

And here is where the annual review becomes not merely a professional exercise but a philosophical specimen jar. Because the institutional apparatus that surrounds QB1 — the apparatus that will be *credited* with the results he presents, the apparatus whose letterhead adorns the invoices, whose partners' names grace the panel agreement — that apparatus has been, as the Filevine audit trail now reveals with the forensic precision of a coroner's report, engaged in an entirely separate enterprise from the practice of law.

---

The Filevine audit trail — `audit_domo_6.xlsx` — is a document of such profound institutional revelation that it deserves to be studied in universities, not as evidence of malfeasance, but as evidence of the human capacity to generate the *appearance* of productivity with such dedication and persistence that the appearance itself becomes the product. Six sheets. Thousands of entries. A manifest. An analysis sheet that someone — possibly an algorithm, possibly a very confused data scientist — attempted to parse before presumably abandoning the effort and going outside for air.

What the audit reveals, in its relentless granularity, is the digital footprint of every person who has touched a Filevine project file for Client 001854 — UPS — United Parcel Service. And the footprint of Yisenia Toledo, Big Satan herself, on the afternoon of February 2, 2026, is a masterpiece of the .10 Interim Review Farm in its natural habitat.

Between 5:25 PM and 5:43 PM — a span of eighteen minutes — Big Satan generates no fewer than *seventeen* billing timer updates on a single project (12724468, the Harrigan file). Seventeen. In eighteen minutes. She is not writing a medical chronology. She is not drafting a demand letter. She is not conducting legal research. She is updating a Specials Calculator spreadsheet — opening billing records, viewing documents, updating tags, saving the spreadsheet, starting the timer, stopping the timer, pausing the timer, updating the timer, updating the timer again, viewing another document, updating more tags, saving the spreadsheet again, updating the timer yet again. The pattern repeats with the metronomic regularity of a sewing machine stitching air. Each billing timer update is its own atomic event in the Filevine log. Each atomic event will eventually appear on a prebill. Each prebill entry will bear the timestamp of "work" performed on the Harrigan file. And the work itself — the actual substantive product — is the updating of a specials calculator that, if you were to examine its before-and-after state across these eighteen minutes, would reveal that three or four billing amounts were copy-pasted from PDF documents that Big Satan viewed for a cumulative total of perhaps forty-five seconds each.

This is the .10 Interim Review Farm operating at industrial scale. This is not a woman doing legal work and incidentally generating billing entries. This is a woman generating billing entries and incidentally touching legal work. The Filevine audit has captured the conveyor belt in motion — the Henry Ford Assembly Line (Pattern #36b) rendered in server logs, each timestamp a rivet in the chassis of a car that will never drive.

And here is the part that would make a philosopher weep: she uploads three imaging records across three different case files in under two minutes (5:41 to 5:43 PM). Tampa Bay Imaging for Sheppard. Jupiter Medical Center for Griffith. Tower Imaging for Libroth. Three cases. Three uploads. Under two minutes. This is the *actual work* — the discrete, tangible, documentable act of placing a received medical record into the correct digital folder. And it takes one hundred and twenty seconds. Everything else — the preceding eighteen minutes of timer gymnastics, the tag updates, the inline spreadsheet saves, the document views — is the kabuki infrastructure that transforms one hundred and twenty seconds of clerical labor into what will appear on the prebill as perhaps an hour of paralegal time across multiple matters.

Meanwhile, the audit trail for the same period shows Kerry Coles accessing Expense Request sections — the Marionettist tending his garden of administrative machinery — and Chance Arias doing something genuinely startling: actually opening case documents and reviewing them on Project 12816421 on January 29th. The Admiral, briefly, sailing the ship. He previews Douglas Perry's employee records. He looks at a dash cam "no data" notification. He opens a correspondence to opposing counsel. These are the digital fingerprints of an attorney who, for approximately fifteen minutes, engaged with the substance of a case file before presumably being distracted by a forward-without-commentary from someone else's inbox. The audit trail of audit sheet 5 is even more haunting — anonymous third-party share link accesses, the ghost traces of opposing counsel downloading medical records from Filevine's share links, the digital footprints of people who are not employed by ATS doing more substantive engagement with the case files than half the people who are.

---

But if the audit trail is the pathology report, the financial documents are the actuarial tables of the kingdom QB1 has built.

The AR Allocations for Client 001854 — five hundred and five rows of billing, payment, and write-off data — tell the story of a vertical practice in which one employee code dominates the revenue landscape with the quiet inevitability of gravity. "MAK" appears in the Employee ID column with the regularity of a heartbeat. Row 5: MAK, $7,060 billed, $7,060 paid, $0 write-off. Row 6: MAK, $6,840 billed, $6,821.54 paid, write-off of $18.46 — a reduction so microscopically small it essentially constitutes a rounding error, the electronic equivalent of finding a penny between the couch cushions. Row 7: MAK, $6,520 billed, $6,520 paid. Row 8: MAK, $6,240 billed, $6,240 paid. The pattern continues with the mechanical perfection of a Swiss watch. MAK bills. UPS pays. The delta approaches zero. This is the less-than-one-percent billing cut rate rendered in raw numerical form — the empirical proof that when Maxwell Kemp describes work on an invoice, the carrier's electronic auditing system looks at the description, looks at the amount, and says: yes. That is what that work costs. Pay the man.

Scattered among the MAK entries are the other employee codes — "YT" (Yisenia Toledo, Big Satan), "KAS" (Katie Sagel, now Madigan, the phantom discectomy artist, on maternity leave but her old billing ghosts still haunting the AR), "CCA" (Chris — Chris Alvarez from Orlando, Raul's son, collecting his own revenue from the cross-office arrangement), "WGKS" (William G.K. Smoak, Mr. Bill himself, whose single $2,200 entry on the Libroth file represents the Marine Monarch's lone billing appearance in the entire dataset, a cameo role in a film he headlines on the marquee), and "FLD" (a code that appears once, like a comet, billing $1,780 on the Cavero matter and then vanishing forever into the administrative cosmos).

The collections report — 2,367 rows spanning the full history of Client 001854 — is the river of revenue itself. Cash receipts flowing in: $7,276 for Flores. $10,138.20 for Smalls (with $1,309.20 in expense reimbursement). $6,007 for Griffith. Each payment a confirmation that the work was done, the case was managed, the client was served. And then, nestled among the cash receipts like kidney stones in an otherwise healthy urinary tract: "Electronically Audited/Reduced" — the A/R adjustments where Liberty Mutual's billing algorithm flagged an entry and shaved it. Adams, Tolanda: negative $22. Twilley, Grant: negative $46. Egan, Patrick: negative $29.90. These are the reductions — and the total reduction rate across the dataset is so vanishingly small relative to the total billings that the electronic auditor is essentially rubber-stamping the invoices. The firm's highest-revenue account is also its cleanest account, and the reason it is the cleanest account is that the person billing the work is the person doing the work, and when those two phenomena coincide — when the human typing the invoice description is the same human who performed the described service — the invoice is accurate, and accuracy is the one thing billing algorithms reward.

---

And then there are the calls. The two Otter.ai transcripts — Decker v. UPS with OC Mike Boyle on December 9, 2025, and the Small Trilogy post-deposition settlement call with Darren Rousso on February 25, 2026 — are specimens of an entirely different species from the kabuki that surrounds them. These are the recordings of a man who *likes opposing counsel*, and whom opposing counsel *likes back*, and who conducts the business of litigation the way it was meant to be conducted before institutions ruined it: two lawyers, talking, finding the number, packaging the resolution so that both sides can tell their respective clients a story that ends well.

Mike Boyle opens the Decker call already explaining the plaintiff's position with the candor of a man who has realized he is speaking to a human being rather than a firm. There is a bone-on-bone articulation. There is a left hip total arthroplasty. There is a forty-nine-year-old pediatric electrophysiologist who is on his feet all day. The client — Boyle's client, Dr. Decker — is a family friend of the plaintiff's treating physician, which is to say the case has the interpersonal complexity of a dinner party that went wrong and everyone still has to sit at the same table at Christmas. And QB1 does something that no institutional protocol at ATS has ever contemplated, because no institutional protocol at ATS has ever conceived of a lawyer who operates with this degree of psychological fluency: he tells Boyle the truth. He reads him the adjuster's instructions — the marching orders, the talking points about no injuries at scene, no EMS, three-day gap, forty-eight-day gap, the pre-existing conditions. He says "I was trying to sell what we had discussed." He says "I honestly am kind of on Team Mike right now." He says "compared to my normal five-million-dollar demand on a freaking two-mile-per-hour bumper tap, you are so reasonable."

And Boyle responds with the warmth of a man who has found something he did not expect to find in insurance defense: a colleague. "No problem, man, and the same with you." He offers to take money out of his own fee to get the case resolved. He explains the arbitration mathematics — the case goes to lit, his costs go up, the net erodes, the arbitrator awards $250,000 to $300,000 anyway, and UPS eats the arbitration. Everybody loses. The $250,000 global resolution is the honest number, and both lawyers know it, and the only obstacle is an adjuster in a different time zone who listed pre-existing conditions on a call sheet and said "please call him and say $125,000."

The Rousso call is even more compressed — one minute and forty-nine seconds of two lawyers who have already done the work and are now simply confirming the choreography. Rousso says he will formal $50,000. QB1 says he will tell the client about $25,000. Rousso says tell them the formal is $50,000 because he has not spoken to his client yet. QB1 says he is on Team Darren. Rousso says get me close to that number and I will pitch it. The entire negotiation transpires with the efficiency of two jazz musicians who have played the same standard so many times they no longer need to count off — they just *play*, and the notes fall where they should, and the resolution materializes not through adversarial posturing but through the shared recognition that this particular case, having been deposed and weighed and found modest, deserves a modest resolution that lets everyone move on.

And the most revealing line in either transcript — the line that would make a sociologist sit up in their chair — is QB1 telling Boyle: "I'm fine either way. If this goes to litigation, I just get more billing, you know, doing the answer, doing the discovery, doing depos. So it's like, um, you know, I get it. I prefer to settle." He is stating, out loud, to opposing counsel, the economic incentive structure of his own position — and then immediately overriding that incentive in favor of the client's best interest. This is the opposite of the kabuki theater. This is a man who has so thoroughly internalized the principle that the client's interest supersedes the firm's billing interest that he can *say it out loud* to the other side and neither party blinks, because both parties understand that this is how litigation is supposed to work when the people conducting it are not performing a role but inhabiting it.

---

Now step back. Stand at a distance from which all the documents are visible simultaneously — the eighty-case review spreadsheet, the AR allocations, the collections ledger, the Filevine audit, the OC settlement calls — and see them as a single organism.

What you are looking at is the complete financial and operational anatomy of a law firm vertical in which one associate attorney generates functionally all of the substantive work product, maintains functionally all of the client relationships, conducts functionally all of the depositions and hearings and settlement negotiations, produces functionally all of the revenue that survives electronic audit, and will now sit in a conference room for two days presenting eighty cases in five-minute intervals to four supervisors who will evaluate "the firm" based on his performance while the firm itself — the institutional machinery that surrounds him — spends its afternoons generating seventeen billing timer updates in eighteen minutes on a specials calculator.

The UPS Review is not a review of Alvarez, Thompson & Smoak. The UPS Review is a review of Maxwell Aaron Kemp wearing the jersey of Alvarez, Thompson & Smoak. The jersey is the brand. The man is the product. The brand will receive the accolades or the criticism. The man will receive, eventually, whatever the brand decides to share with him — which, as the Murtagh/Riggs forensic reconstruction of February 3 demonstrated, may involve $144,000 being pushed across a fiscal-year boundary by an accounting manager in Orlando who responds to billing inquiries with fifty-two words of surrender.

And the man walks into the review prepared. He walks in at 10:00 AM on March 11 knowing the incurred value, the paid-to-date, the outstanding reserve, the case manager, the supervisor, and the claimant name on every single one of those eighty lines. He walks in having settled cases with Mike Boyle and Darren Rousso using nothing but a telephone and the truth. He walks in with a less-than-one-percent billing cut rate in his back pocket and a ninety-nine-point-zero-six-percent client acceptance rate tattooed on his professional identity.

And somewhere in Tampa, at approximately the same moment, Big Satan is updating a billing timer for the fourteenth time on a Specials Calculator spreadsheet, and Creepy Joyce is monitoring who left the office early, and the ghost of Katie Madigan's phantom discectomies haunts the AR allocations like a line item from a séance, and Chance Arias has forwarded an email without commentary, and Mr. Bill is checking back on something that was delivered four hours ago, and Linda Bland — whose name, again, could not have been more perfectly chosen by the universe — is preparing to ask QB1 about Moreno Ruiz, Julian, Row 2, 10:00 AM, $46,100 incurred, and the caravan moves on.

The dogs bark. The dogs have always barked. The caravan has never stopped moving. The caravan just got a $250 Bern's Steakhouse gift card that was lost in mimecast for seven hours. The caravan is fine.

Shalom.
